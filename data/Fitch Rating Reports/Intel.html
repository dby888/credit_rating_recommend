Fitch Downgrades Intel's Ratings to 'BBB+'; Affirms Short-Term Ratings at 'F2'; Outlook Stable
3164 words
1 July 2024
Fitch Rating / Non Rating Action Commentary
FITRA
English
Copyright 2024 by Fitch Ratings Inc., Fitch Ratings Ltd. and its subsidiaries.

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-01 July 2024: Fitch Ratings has downgraded Intel Corporation's Long-Term Issuer Default Rating (IDR) and senior unsecured ratings to 'BBB+' from 'A-'. Fitch has also affirmed Intel's Short-Term IDR and commercial paper (CP) rating at 'F2'. The Rating Outlook is Stable.

The ratings and Outlook reflect Fitch's expectation that FCF margins, adjusted for capital spending offsets, on average will be modestly positive through our forecast period (2024-2027). Additionally, Fitch expects EBITDA leverage metrics will improve but will remain outside of Fitch's previous negative rating sensitivity through 2026. Fitch believes execution risk remains significant for Intel and that missteps could result in further negative rating actions.

At the same time, the successful execution on Intel's strategy to regain process technology leadership and build the leading foundry services business in the U.S. and Europe could support a return to the A-category. However, Fitch does not expect this to be apparent in the next few years.

Key Rating Drivers

Weaker FCF Profile: Fitch expects Intel's investment super-cycle to result in modestly positive FCF after capital spending offsets in aggregate over the next several years. However, FCF margins will be in the low-single digits, which is more in line with the 'BBB' rating category. Additionally, these margins are considerably weaker than similarly-rated semiconductor peers. Intel's execution on its strategy depends on a significant degree of capital spending over the next several years. Funding for this spending will in large part come from offsets, including co-investment programs and U.S. and European government subsidies.

Fitch anticipates FCF to fluctuate with the timing of capital spending and offsets through the forecast period. Over the longer term, Fitch expects Intel's FCF profile to strengthen due to structurally higher profitability from closing the process technology gap and capital intensity normalizing near 25%, down from a Fitch-forecasted approximately 50% in 2024. Fitch estimates this is in line with Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Co. Ltd.'s (TSMC) investment intensity focused on the leading-edge.

Leverage Elevated Longer: Fitch forecasts improving leverage metrics through the forecast period; however, Fitch does not expect EBITDA leverage to be below our 2.5x negative rating sensitivity until 2026. Fitch forecasts EBITDA leverage near 3.75x in 2024 and a full turn of improvement to 2.75x in 2025 from higher profitability, since Intel should not need to increase debt levels. High cash balances result in net leverage near 2.0x, providing sufficient cushion to support liquidity through this investment cycle.

Structurally Higher Investment Intensity: Fitch expects structurally higher investment intensity, given Intel's commitment to regaining process technology leadership and building out its foundry services business. Fitch believes management's $15 billion external customer revenue by 2030 target for the foundry services business may limit capital spending moderation. However, Fitch believes Intel's 25% capital intensity long-term target is in line with what current technology leader, TSMC, spends on the leading edge.

Pressure on Operating Performance: Fitch expects new products based on Intel's process technology transitions to drive positive revenue growth and profit margin expansion through the forecast period, albeit from current near-record lows. Excess inventory throughout the supply chain continues to weigh on revenue and profit margins in 1H24 after revenue and EBITDA margins were down meaningfully in each of the last two years. Fitch forecasts solid revenue growth over the next few years with EBITDA margin expansion in the 40% range from the high 20% range in 2024.

Strong Market Positions: Intel's credit profile benefits from the company's strong market positions in personal computers (PCs) and data centers (DCs), despite share erosion in servers to its traditional competitor, Advanced Micro Devices Inc. (AMD), and ARM-based custom servers. Strong growth in PCs, communications service providers, internet of things and automotive markets should offset share losses. In addition, new product technology introductions over the next several years should stabilize share losses.

Derivation Summary

Fitch believes Intel's profile is in line with the 'BBB+' rating. Strong strategy execution, combined with delevering over the next several years could result in a rating upgrade to the 'A' category. If not, there could be negative rating pressure.

In a successful scenario, Intel becomes the advanced node semiconductor foundry of choice in the U.S. and Europe with sufficient scale to support ongoing technology leadership and the product company stems share losses. This hinges on the success of the company's process technology roadmap. In a negative scenario, there is a meaningful elongation of Intel's investment super-cycle, most likely due to process technology transition challenges.

Intel faces intensified competition from its rival AMD in both PCs and servers for DCs, as well as increasing utilization of custom ARM-based servers by cloud service provider customers. Nonetheless, Intel's market share remains strong despite technology challenges and is poised to stabilize with new products set for production over the coming years. These include those sourced from the company's increased use of third-party foundry TSMC for advanced products over the nearer term.

From both an operating and financial profile perspective, Intel is weaker than TSMC, considering TSMC's significant leadership in leading-edge foundry services, superior FCF profile and strong credit metrics. Nonetheless, geopolitical risk is significant for TSMC (and its customers) and Fitch believes Intel has an inside track to become the advanced technology foundry supplier of choice in the U.S. and Europe, geographies in which semiconductor manufacturing is going to develop slowly but become significant over the longer term.

In terms of similarly rated peers, Intel compares unfavorably with Qorvo Inc. (BBB+/Stable) and Skyworks Solutions Inc. (BBB+/Stable), both of which have more conservative financial structures in order to offset greater end market and customer concentration than that of Intel. Qorvo and Skyworks have higher FCF margins due to lower investment intensity than that of Intel.

Compared with NXP Semiconductor N.V. (BBB+/Stable), Intel also compares less favorably with weaker leverage metrics and lower financial flexibility. Intel's market positions remain strong despite share losses over recent years and on this factor the company compares favorably to NXP, as well as Intel's 'BBB' category-rated semiconductor peers.

Equity Treatment for SCIP2: Fitch's equity-like view on SCIP2 mainly reflects the variability of investment returns for Apollo, the minority investor in the JV. The returns are subject to the JV's ability to meet Intel's demand for leading-edge wafers, market demand for Intel's products and Intel's success attracting third-party foundry customers. Fitch treats the first SCIP transaction as debt-like, as it believes that wafer purchases and returns to the minority investor are largely fixed. Fitch will apply 100% equity treatment to SCIP2 and continue to adjust Intel's debt quantum according to net flows from the SCIP1 minority investor.

Key Assumptions

--Low-single digit positive revenue growth in 2024, driven by the resumption of positive PC unit shipment growth in 2H24;

--Revenue grows by the high-single digits through the remainder of the forecast, reflecting improving competitive positioning and growth in the foundry services business;

--EBITDA margins expand from the high 20% range for 2024 to 40% through the forecast period;

--Capital spending remains elevated at more than 40% of revenue through the forecast period constrained by receipt of capital spending offsets;

--Flat dividends and no stock buybacks;

--Intel refinances upcoming debt maturities.

RATING SENSITIVITIES

Factors that could, individually or collectively, lead to positive rating action/upgrade

--Intel stabilizes share with EBITDA margins in the mid- to high-40% and FCF margins in the high single digits;

--Expectations that Intel will sustain EBITDA leverage below 2.0x.

Factors that could, individually or collectively, lead to negative rating action/downgrade

--Sustained negative organic revenue growth or expectations that Intel will not be able to expand gross profit margins due to technology challenges;

--Expectations for EBITDA leverage sustained above 2.5x beyond the intermediate term;

--Negative FCF beyond 2027 due to continued elevated capital intensity.

Liquidity and Debt Structure

Intel's liquidity will remain solid despite its investment super-cycle and, as of March 30, 2024, was supported by $21.4 billion of cash, cash equivalents and short-term investments. Liquidity is also supported by an undrawn $7.0 billion revolving credit facility (RCF) due February 2029 and an undrawn $8.0 billion 364-day RCF expiring January 2025. This more than backstops the company's $10 billion CP program, under which $793 million was outstanding at quarter-end. Fitch expects FCF (including capital spending offsets) will be positive in aggregate over the forecast period.

Issuer Profile

Intel Corp. is the world's largest integrated design manufacturer, providing integrated circuits and related products into computing, internet of things and communications markets, in addition to providing foundry services.

Fitch Ratings: Intel’s ‘A-’/‘F2’ Ratings and Negative Outlook Unaffected by SCIP2 Transaction

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-04 June 2024: Fitch Ratings’ ‘A-’/‘F2’ ratings and Negative Outlook for Intel Corp. are unaffected by the company’s announcement that it entered into a second co-investment program (SCIP2) that, unlike Intel’s first SCIP transaction, Fitch views as more equity-like and will not result in Fitch adjustments to Intel’s debt quantum for purposes of calculating credit metrics. SCIP2 will offset a portion of capital required to fund Intel’s investment super cycle but FCF, nonetheless, will still be negative through 2025 with leverage metrics outside of Fitch’s negative EBITDA leverage rating sensitivity, in line with Fitch’s previous forecast.

SCIP2 contemplates Intel’s sale of a 49% equity interest in a joint venture (JV) entity related to Intel’s largely-completed leading-edge high-volume manufacturing facility in Ireland (Fab 34) to Apollo for approximately $11 billion in cash. Intel has invested $18.4 billion on Fab 34 to date, adding flexibility for Intel to fund other capital spending. Intel will have a 51% controlling interest in the JV and retain full ownership and operational control of Fab 34 and its assets. Intel expects SCIP2 to close in the second quarter of 2024.

Fitch’s equity-like view on SCIP2 mainly reflects the variability of investment returns for Apollo, which is subject to the JV’s ability to meet Intel’s demand for leading-edge wafers, market demand for Intel’s products and Intel’s success attracting third-party foundry customers. Fitch treats the first SCIP transaction as debt-like, given Fitch’s view that wafer purchases and returns to the minority investor are largely fixed. Fitch adjusts Intel’s debt quantum by net flows from the SCIP1 minority investor.

The JV will have rights to manufacture wafers at Fab 34 using Intel 4 and 3 process technologies to support demand for Intel’s products and provide capacity for foundry customers. Under the agreement, Intel is required to complete construction on the facility, although high volume manufacturing in Fab 34 already began in September 2023. The JV will sell wafers to Intel on a cost-plus-margin basis and the agreement requires Intel to meet volume commitments for its demand following the facility’s substantial completion.

Intel has agreed to preferential loading of Fab 34 relative to other Intel fabs running the same process and tooling and payment of liquidated damages to Apollo in various circumstances if Intel fails to meet minimum volume commitments, which Fitch views as typical of wafer supply agreements. In addition, under various ancillary Intel and Apollo agreements, Intel or Intel Ireland may be required to pay liquidated damages or termination payments under various scenarios, including under-performance or termination, an amount up to 140% of Apollo’s purchase price. Intel will provide a parent guarantee to the obligations of Intel Ireland, which owns the Fab 34 assets.

Fitch expects revenue to accelerate in second half of 2024 and through 2025 as Intel ramps new products amidst the resumption of positive personal computer (PC) unit shipment growth and robust artificial intelligence infrastructure spending. Start-up costs associated with Intel’s multiple process node transitions will constrain the pace of gross profit margin expansion but Fitch expects EBITDA leverage back below 2x and resumption of positive FCF in 2026.

Fitch forecasts nearly $15 billion of cash usage over the 2024-2025 timeframe, which incorporates capital offsets from the SCIP programs and various forms of government support but excludes potential net proceeds from the monetization of Altera or Mobileye. Fitch’s forecast also incorporates continued inventory normalization.

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-15 February 2024: Fitch Ratings has assigned an 'A-' rating to Intel Corp.'s senior notes offering. The senior notes will be pari passu with Intel's existing and future senior unsecured indebtedness. Fitch expects Intel to use net proceeds from the senior notes offering to repay upcoming debt maturities and that the transaction will be leverage neutral. Estimated EBITDA leverage was 3.7x exiting 2023. Intel has approximately $2.3 billion of debt maturing in 2024 and another roughly $3.8 billion due in 2025.

Key Rating Drivers

Negative Intermediate-Term FCF: Fitch expects FCF will be negative over the 2024-2025 period as Intel completes its investment super-cycle aimed at regaining manufacturing technology leadership and building out its foundry services business. Fitch expects cash balances to remain high in the $20 billion-$30 billion range. The company will fund cash burn with a combination of contributions from financial partners, government subsidies and tax benefits, net proceeds from the planned spin-off of the Programmable Solutions Group (PSG) and asset monetization.

Intel may fund remaining shortfalls with incremental debt. Higher debt and lower profitability exiting 2023 position the company's credit metrics weakly for the low 'A' rating category. Fitch forecasts improving profitability will drive Fitch-estimated EBITDA leverage to the mid- to high-2.0x range in 2024, down from 3.7x for 2023 (1.8x on a net leverage basis). Cash from operations (CFO)-capex/total debt (adjusted for JV-minority partner contributions) will be negative through 2025 before turning positive in 2026.

Structurally Higher Investment Intensity: Fitch expects structurally higher investment intensity given Intel's commitment to regaining process technology leadership and building out IFS. Intel's success, which would enable it to stem or reverse market share losses and gain a meaningful foothold in the $100 billion foundry addressable market, rather than moderate spending levels, will determine the company's ability to remain in the 'A' rating category. Fitch is cautious on guidance for longer-term capital spending normalization, given that elevated spending levels are in line with technology leaders.

Fitch expects annual capital spending net of co-investments and subsidies of $20 billion-$25 billion (low- to mid-30% of revenue), up from an average of $15 billion (20% of revenue) in the 2016-2020 period. R&D adds another $15 billion-plus in non-GAAP spending on innovation with potential moderation related to the company's recent restructuring program announcement, up from an average of $13 billion per year between 2016 and 2020. Fitch expects Intel will pursue additional co-investments and subsidies to defray spending levels, potentially reducing the drag on FCF.

Pressured Operating Performance: Fitch expects new products based upon Intel's strengthening process technologies to drive positive revenue growth and profit margin expansion from current near-record low levels. Fitch forecasts above market revenue growth over the next few years with profit margin expansion partly offset by start-up costs associated with the company's process technology transitions. Revenue was down by the mid-teens in 2023 and EBITDA margins declined to a Fitch estimated 26%, roughly half of historical levels, despite Intel having achieved $3 billion of cost savings.

Strong Market Positions: Intel's credit profile benefits from the company's still strong market positions in personal computers (PC) and datacenter (DC), despite ongoing share erosion in servers to traditional competitor, Advanced Micro Devices Inc. (AMD), and ARM-based custom servers. Strong growth in PCs, communications service providers, internet of things (IoT) and automotive markets should offset share losses. In addition, new product technology introductions over the next several years should stabilize share and potentially reverse losses.

Derivation Summary

Fitch believes Intel's weakened financial position within the context of its investment super-cycle and process technology disadvantage position it low for even the current 'A-' rating. Beyond the intermediate term, Fitch expects considerably stronger profit margins and FCF but Fitch is skeptical of a return to historical levels given ongoing investment requirements associated with the foundry services business.

Fitch's base case contemplates recovering end markets in 2024, aided by product refreshes that should stabilize market share while Intel's restructuring actions support profit margin expansion. Solid cash balances, co-investments and government subsidies should provide sufficient liquidity to fund capital spending, which, over the longer-term, position the company as a market leader with meaningful foundry services capabilities in the U.S. and Europe as customers look to diversify semiconductor supply chains. Intel may also fund investments by monetizing assets, including PSG and IMS Nanofabrication.

Intel faces intensified competition from rival AMD in both PCs and servers for DCs, as well as increasing utilization of custom ARM-based servers by cloud service provider customers. Nonetheless, Intel's market share remains strong despite technology challenges and is poised to stabilize with new products set for production over the coming years, including those sourced from the company's increased use of third-party foundry TSMC for advanced products over the nearer term. From both an operating and financial profile perspective, Intel is weaker than 'AA-'-rated TSMC, given that company's significant leadership in leading edge foundry services, superior FCF profile and strong credit metrics.

Key Assumptions

-- Mid-single digit positive revenue growth in 2024, reflecting improved process technology and the resumption of positive PC unit shipment growth;

--Revenue grows by the mid- to high-single digits through the remainder of the forecast reflecting improving competitive positioning and modest growth in IFS;

--Gross profit margins expand to the mid- to high-50% range through the forecast period from a combination of positive revenue growth, benefits from Intel's cost reduction initiatives and a richer sales mix;

--Operating EBITDA margins expand from the mid-20% to the mid-40% through the forecast period;

--Capital spending remains in the low- to mid-30s as a percentage of revenue with offsets from financial partners and government subsidies;

--Intel begins funding a portion of capex with net proceeds from divestitures, including PSG in 2024;

--Flat dividends and no stock buybacks;

--Intel refinances upcoming debt maturities and plugs cash burn with a combination of divestitures, partnerships, government grants and incremental debt.

RATING SENSITIVITIES

Factors that could, individually or collectively, lead to positive rating action/upgrade:

--Intel regains a greater than 80% share in DC markets while maintaining profit margins in line with industry peers, demonstrating technology leadership in the face of intensifying competition;

--Significant growth in non-PC and DC segments resulting in greater revenue diversification, underscoring the company's technology roadmap;

--Expectations Intel will sustain EBITDA leverage near 1.5x.

Factors that could, individually or collectively, lead to negative rating action/downgrade:

--Sustained negative organic revenue growth or expectations that Intel will not be able to sustain Fitch-adjusted gross profit margins above 50% due to a structurally weakened competitive position and product roadmap;

--Negative FCF beyond 2025, underscoring failure in the company's technology roadmap;

--Expectations for EBITDA leverage sustained above 2.0x beyond the intermediate term.

Liquidity and Debt Structure

Intel's liquidity is pressured and, as of Dec. 30, 2023, was supported by $7.1 billion of cash and cash equivalents, $18.0 billion of short-term investments, an undrawn $5.0 billion revolving credit facility (RCF) due March 2028 and undrawn $5.0 billion 364-day RCF expiring March 2024. The credit facilities fully back-stop Intel's $10.0 billion CP program, under which no borrowings were outstanding at quarter-end.

Fitch expects Intel's annual FCF will be negative over the next few years, requiring a combination of contributions from financial partners, government subsidies or, potentially, debt financing to fund the company's investment super-cycle.

Issuer Profile

Intel Corp. is the world's largest integrated design manufacturer, providing integrated circuits and related products into computing, internet of things and communications markets, in addition to providing foundry services.

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-02 February 2023: Fitch Ratings has downgraded Intel Corp.'s Long-Term Issuer Default Rating (IDR) and senior unsecured ratings to 'A-' from 'A+', and Short-Term IDR and Commercial Paper Ratings to 'F2' from 'F1'. The Rating Outlook on Intel's Long-Term IDR is Negative.

The ratings and Outlook reflect Fitch's view that structurally higher investment intensity as Intel seeks to regain technology leadership and build out its foundry services business amidst weak demand conditions and market share losses, will result in significant intermediate-term negative FCF. Offsets to capital investments, including financial partnerships and government incentives, are likely insufficient to support cash needs over this horizon, requiring incremental debt funding and pressuring credit metrics. Meanwhile, execution risk is considerable, given that Intel is cutting costs while investing to out-innovate competitors over the next three-to-five years.

Key Rating Drivers

Negative Intermediate-Term FCF: Fitch expects FCF will be negative over the 2023-2025 period as Intel completes its investment super-cycle aimed at regaining manufacturing technology leadership and building out its foundry services business. Meanwhile, revenue and profitability will be down considerably in 2023, driven by record excess inventory following robust PC and server demand surrounding the pandemic-related policies. Profit margins will fall to record low levels in the near term before rebounding in 2024, with the resumption of positive revenue growth and flow-through of benefits from Intel's $3 billion cost savings initiatives.

Fitch expects cash balances to remain high in the $20 billion-$30 billion range; however, Fitch expects the company to fund cash burn with a combination of contributions from financial partners, such as its JV with Brookfield Capital, government subsidies and tax benefits and, likely, incremental debt issuance. Fitch expects the potential for higher debt levels and lower profitability in 2023 will drive credit metric deterioration and position the company in the low 'A' rating category. Fitch forecasts EBITDA leverage, estimated at 2.1x exiting 2022, will remain in the 2.0x-3.0x range over the 2023-2025 period, while CFO-Capex/Total Debt (adjusted for JV-minority partner contributions) will be negative to minimal.

Structurally Higher Investment Intensity: Fitch believes higher investment intensity is structural rather than temporary given Intel's commitment to regaining process technology leadership and building out IFS. Intel's success, which would enable it to stem or reverse market share losses and gain a meaningful foothold in the $100 billion foundry addressable market, rather than moderate spending levels, will determine the company's ability to remain in the 'A' rating category.

Fitch is cautious on guidance for longer-term capital spending normalization, given that elevated spending levels are in line with technology leaders. Intel's expanded outsourcing of advanced products manufacturing to TSMC adds flexibility to the pace of investments and hedges against technology roadmap missteps. However, Fitch believes dynamics related to the development of a domestic semiconductor supply chain, including significant co-investments and government subsidies, reduces the likelihood of Intel pivoting on its strategy.

Fitch expects annual capital spending net of co-investments and subsidies of $15 billion-25 billion (low- to mid-30% of revenue), up from an average of $15 billion (20% of revenue) in the 2016-2020 period. R&D adds another $15+ billion in non-GAAP spending on innovation with potential moderation related to the company's recent restructuring program announcement, up from an average of $13 billion per year between 2016 and 2020. Fitch expects Intel will pursue additional co-investments and subsidies to defray spending levels, potentially reducing the drag on FCF.

Strong Market Positions: Intel's credit profile benefits from the company's still strong market positions in personal computers PC and DC, despite ongoing share erosion in servers to traditional competitor, Advanced Micro Devices Inc., and ARM-based custom servers. Strong growth in PCs, communications service providers, internet of things (IoT) and automotive markets should offset share losses. In addition, new product technology introductions over the next several years should stabilize share and potentially reverse losses.

Tower Semiconductor Acquisition: Fitch expects the proposed all-cash acquisition of Tower Semiconductor for $5.4 billion will add specialty technologies on mature nodes to IFS. Tower's expertise in radio frequency, power, silicon-germanium and industrial sensors and long-standing customer relationships strengthen Intel's ability to address faster growing mobile, automotive and power end markets, while Tower's footprint adds two million wafer starts per year of capacity in the U.S. and Asia. Fitch expects Intel use cash to fund the deal, which it expects to close in 2023, subject to certain regulatory approvals and customary closing conditions.

As a result, Fitch expects meaningfully negative FCF and weak leverage metrics over the intermediate term, which is inconsistent with the company previous strong 'A' rating. Beyond the intermediate term, Fitch expects stronger profit margins and FCF; however, a return to historical levels will require strong execution across a number of Intel's strategic initiatives.

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-16 November 2022: Fitch Ratings has affirmed the 'A+' Long-Term Issuer Default Rating (IDR) and senior unsecured ratings and 'F1' short-term ratings for Intel Corp. Fitch has withdrawn the ratings for Altera Corp. The Rating Outlook is Negative.

The ratings and Outlook reflect expectations for FCF usage and weak credit metrics as Intel invests to regain technology leadership and build-out its foundry services business (IFS), which Intel will partially fund with co-investments. Fitch will attribute to Intel's debt quantum the net capital contributions from its joint venture (JV) partner, Brookfield Capital, adding up to $15 billion of debt over the capital contribution period, although back-end loaded.

Share erosion and excess inventory amidst weakening near-term demand will weigh on results and Fitch forecasts EBITDA leverage will exceed our 1.5x negative rating sensitivity for 2022-2023. Solid revenue recovery and profit margin expansion will be necessary to bring FCF and credit metrics back in-line with the rating.

Fitch has withdrawn the ratings for Altera following the settlement of its lone tranche of senior unsecured notes, assumed by Intel in the 2015 acquisition. Fitch does not anticipate future debt issuance from the Altera Corp. subsidiary.

Key Rating Drivers

JV Treatment: Fitch will attribute to Intel's debt quantum the net capital contributions from its JV partner since the subsequent distributions to Brookfield are debt-like in that they are subject to a fixed minimum and continue until the partner's ownership stake is fully depleted. A wafer purchase agreement (WPA) between Intel and the JV will fund distributions to the minority partner beginning after the capital contribution period. However, these distributions are mandatory whether or not the JV is able to supply the contracted wafers, a not inconsequential risk given Intel's aim of achieving yields at five production nodes over the next four years.

As majority owner of the JV, Intel will consolidate the JV from a reporting standpoint and, mechanically, Fitch expects to add to Intel's debt quantum the capital contributions from the minority partner. As production starts, distributions to the minority partner will reduce debt Fitch attributes to Intel. Capital contributions from both Intel and the minority should be back-end loaded, since the majority of the project cost will be related to equipment purchases not made until the building shell is completed. Fitch will assess disclosure once contributions commence and may use estimates if public disclosure is inadequate.

Structurally Higher Investment Intensity: Fitch believes higher investment intensity is structural rather than temporary given Intel's commitment to regaining process technology leadership and building out IFS. Intel's success, which would enable it to stem or reverse market share losses and gain a meaningful foothold in the $100 billion foundry addressable market, rather than moderate spending levels, will determine the company's ability to sustain a solid 'A' category credit profile.

Fitch is cautious on guidance for longer-term capital spending normalization given that elevated spending levels are in-line with technology leader. Intel's expanded outsourcing of advanced products manufacturing to TSMC adds flexibility to the pace of investments and hedges against technology roadmap missteps. However, Fitch believes dynamics related to the development of a domestic semiconductor supply chain, including significant co-investments and government subsidies, reduces the likelihood of Intel pivoting on its strategy.

Fitch expects annual capital spending net of co-investments and subsidies of $20 billion-25 billion (35%-40% of revenue), up from an average of $15 billion (20% of revenue) in the 2016-2020 period. R&D adds another $15+ billion in non-GAAP spending on innovation with potential moderation related to the company's recent restructuring program announcement, up from an average of $13 billion per year between 2016 and 2020. Fitch expects Intel will pursue additional co-investments and subsidies to defray spending levels, potentially reducing the drag on FCF.

Adequate Liquidity: Liquidity remains adequate despite expectations for negative FCF over the medium-run, given elevated cash balances that are largely the result of paused share repurchases and net proceeds from divestitures. Fitch believes that Intel may hold higher than historical cash levels and, therefore, excess available cash may be insufficient to offset negative FCF, potentially resulting in incremental debt.

Strong Market Positions: Intel's credit profile benefits from the company's still strong market positions in personal computers (PC) and data centers (DC), despite share ongoing erosion to traditional competitor, Advanced Micro Devices Inc., and ARM-based custom servers for CSPs. Strong growth in PCs, communications service providers, internet of things (IoT) and automotive markets should offset share losses in rapidly growing DC service provider markets. In addition, new product technology introductions over the next few years should stabilize share and potentially reverse losses.

Tower Semiconductor Acquisition: Fitch expects the proposed all-cash acquisition of Tower Semiconductor for $5.4 billion will add specialty technologies on mature nodes to IFS. Tower's expertise in radio frequency (RF), power, silicon-germanium (SiGe) and industrial sensors, as well as the company's long-standing customer relationships, strengthen Intel's ability to address faster growing mobile, automotive and power end markets, while Tower's footprint adds two million wafer starts per year of capacity in the U.S. and Asia.

Fitch expects the company will use available cash to fund the transaction, which the company expects to close in the first quarter of 2023, subject to certain regulatory approvals and customary closing conditions, including the approval of Tower's stockholders.

Derivation Summary

Fitch believes Intel's position for the rating has weakened, driven by an elevated investment cycle that will pressure FCF and credit metrics over the intermediate term. Risks associated with the company's ability to regain technology leadership and build out its foundry services businesses amidst weakening demand conditions and share losses will weigh on near-term revenue and profitability.

However, Fitch's base case contemplates recovering end markets in 2024, aided by product refreshes that should stabilize market share while Intel's restructuring actions support profit margin expansion. Solid cash balances, co-investments and government subsidies should provide sufficient liquidity to fund capital spending, which over the longer-term positions as a market leader with meaningful foundry services capabilities in the U.S. and Europe as customers look to diversify semiconductor supply chains.

Intel faces intensified competition from rival, AMD, in both PCs and servers for DCs, as well as increasing utilization of custom ARM-based servers by cloud service provider customers. Nonetheless, Intel's market share remains strong despite technology challenges and is poised to stabilize with new products set for production over the coming years, including those sourced from the company's increased use of third-party foundry, TSMC, for advanced products over the nearer-term. From both an operating and financial profile perspective, Intel is weaker than 'AA-'-rated TSMC, given that company's significant leadership in leading edge foundry services, superior FCF profile and strong credit metrics.

Key Assumptions

Fitch's Key Assumptions Within the Rating Case:

--Revenue declines by high single digits in the current quarter from excess inventory resulting in revenue down 20% year-over-year for 2022.

--Revenue down in the high single digits in 2023, driven by weaker end market demand as the supply chain works through excess PC and server inventory.

--Mid-single digit recovery across all end markets in 2024 and low- to mid-single digit revenue growth thereafter, driven by sustainable long-term demand.

--Intel achieves $3 billion of opex-focused run rate cost reductions in 2023.

--$21 billion of capital spending, net of co-investments and subsidies, in 2023 and 2024 and 25% of revenue over the longer term.

--Dividends grow by low-single digits and no share repurchases, while debt maturities are refinanced.

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-22 November 2021: Fitch Ratings has affirmed the Long- and Short-Term Issuer Default Ratings (IDRs) and senior unsecured ratings for Intel Corporation at 'A+' and 'F1', respectively, and the Long-Term IDR and senior unsecured ratings for wholly owned subsidiary, Altera Corp., at 'A+'. The Rating Outlook is Stable.

The ratings and Outlook reflect Fitch's expectations for solid operating and financial performance, despite anticipated share losses and challenged FCF over the next 2-3 years as Intel pivots from capital returns to investing in innovation and building a foundry business. Organic revenue growth will remain positive and profit margins strong for the rating, despite lower gross profit margins over the medium term as the company ramps new process technologies. Through the company's manufacturing strategy pivot, Intel will benefit from strong liquidity and headroom under Fitch's negative leverage sensitivity.

Key Rating Drivers

Manufacturing Strategy Pivot: Fitch believes Intel's manufacturing strategy pivot, which expands outsourcing of advanced products to TSMC while investing to regain process technology leadership and build IFS, is credit neutral to positive, versus managing decline by abandoning technology leadership. However, it also significantly elevates execution risk, given TSMC's process node leadership and that building a successful foundry business will only pay dividends over the longer term. Meanwhile, expanding outsourcing will weigh on profit margins even if it stabilizes share at leading edge nodes.

Solid Liquidity: Fitch expects Intel's liquidity to remain solid, despite break-even to negative FCF from meaningfully higher capital spending forecasted over at least the medium term. The company has considerable cash and cash equivalents, short-term investments and trading assets that will be bolstered by net proceeds from Intel's sale of its memory business, providing strong liquidity to support investments and upcoming debt maturities should Intel fall short of forecasts. Additionally, Fitch expects Intel will use FCF formerly designated for stock buybacks to fund the uptick in capex.

Structurally Higher Investment Intensity: Fitch believes Intel's higher investment intensity is structural rather than temporary, should the company remain committed to process technology leadership and building its foundry services business. Fitch forecasts capital spending, net of government subsidies, of $25 billion-$30 billion through the forecast period, representing 35%-40% of revenue versus closer to 20% in more recent years. R&D should add another $15+ billion in non-GAAP spending on innovation, bringing total investment intensity to nearly 60% of revenue, versus 40%-45% historically.

This higher level of spending is in-line with technology leader, Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Co. Ltd., which provides foundry services to Intel's competitors, including Advanced Micro Devices Inc. and custom solutions designed by large public cloud providers. However, Intel's expanded outsourcing of advanced products manufacturing to TSMC adds flexibility to the pace of investments and hedges against technology roadmap missteps.

Technology-Driven Share Losses: Intel's process technology transition challenges over the past several years have eroded the company's long-standing technology leadership and threatened Intel's dominant market share and premium profit margins. It stands to lose considerable share to Advanced Micro Devices Inc., Intel's long-time competitor with historically minimal share, and has hastened the long-anticipated adoption of ARM-based servers as large cloud providers foster an innovative ecosystem.

Strong Market Positions: Still strong market positions in personal computers (PC) and data centers (DC) support Intel's ability to intensify investments in response to expected share erosion at the hands of traditional competitor, Advanced Micro Devices Inc. and ARM-based servers. Strong growth in PCs, communications service providers, internet of things (IoT) and automotive markets should, in conjunction with the resumption of higher mix enterprise and government infrastructure spending, offset share losses in rapidly growing DC service provider markets.

Customer Concentration: Fitch expects customer concentration will intensify, as service providers consolidate IT spending, potentially resulting in more volatile quarterly results. The largest hyperscale cloud companies will continue robust DC capacity deployments, driven by increasing demand for computing and storage of data growth and ongoing workload migration to public clouds. Large telecom service providers will also continue deploying capacity as data proliferates exponentially at the edge, although longer term growth in IoT and ADAS should mitigate some customer concentration.

Derivation Summary

Fitch believes Intel is positioned in-line but at the lower end of the current rating. Its financial profile remains solid with strengthened liquidity but FCF will weaken under the weight of higher investment intensity as the company invests to close TSMC's process technology lead and build out Intel's foundry services business. Still, the company has headroom under Fitch's negative leverage sensitivity, which is total debt to operating EBITDA below 1.5x. Furthermore, cash flow that previously was returned to shareholders will fund the incremental capital spending.

From an operating profile perspective, Intel faces share erosion in DC and PCs but maintains very strong market positions at nearly 75%-80% share in markets that continue solid revenue growth. Ultimately, Fitch believes Intel's hybrid manufacturing model will enable the company to either solidify share by virtue of closing TSMC's technology leadership or, should Intel fail to close the technology gap, running significant volumes of advanced products through its foundry partner's manufacturing facilities.

Fitch believes, from a financial perspective, Intel is roughly in-line with TSMC, given Intel's comparable profit margins and low EBITDA- and FFO-based leverage metrics. From an operating profile perspective, Fitch also believes Intel stacks-up in-line with TSMC, given the latter's technology leadership and dominant foundry position offset by Intel's greater flexibility in its prospective hybrid manufacturing model and unique footprint across regions as demand for supply chain diversification remains strong.

Key Assumptions

--Modest revenue growth, excluding the impacts of divestitures, in 2021 and 2022, followed by excess capacity and a customer digestion-driven mid-single digit correction in 2023 before resuming low-single digit revenue growth in 2024.

--Gross profit margin remains in the low-50% through the forecast as the company ramps process nodes.

--Spending remains roughly flat with non-GAAP R&D near 21% of revenue.

--Capex is $25 billion-$30 billion annually, representing 35%-40% of revenue through the forecast period, as Intel invests in closing TSMC's technology-leadership gap and building Intel's foundry services business.

--Dividends grow by low-single digits annually and no significant stock buybacks with FCF.

--Intel refinances upcoming debt maturities to maintain total debt/operating EBITDA close to 1.0x but certainly below 1.5x.

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-28 October 2021: Fitch Ratings maintains the Rating Watch Negative (RWN) for the 'A-' Long-Term Issuer Default Rating (IDR) and the senior unsecured debt for Xilinx Inc. The ratings reflect the company's pending merger with Advanced Micro Devices, Inc. (AMD), which remains under review by Chinese regulators and is now not expected to close until the end of calendar 2021. At or near deal close, Fitch expects to resolve the RWN by affirming the ratings at 'A-' or downgrading them by one notch to 'BBB+', depending upon the ultimate guarantee structure.

The current rating reflects Fitch's expectation for the continuation of solid operating performance on a standalone basis, driven by technology leadership, resulting in strong market positions in a well-diversified set of secular growth end-markets. Near-term demand should remain solid, given recovery from the coronavirus pandemic and meaningful fiscal and monetary intervention, while supply shortages flatten but extend elevated revenue growth. Amidst this backdrop, profit and cash flow margins will remain healthier than previously forecast and support Xilinx's solid financial profile.

Key Rating Drivers

Pending Merger with AMD: Fitch expects the merger to expand addressable markets, diversify products and support R&D scale. The deal roughly replicates Intel Corp.'s December 2015 acquisition of field-programmable gate array (FPGA) competitor, Altera Corp., but with technology leadership for the AMD-Xilinx combination. AMD's share gains in DC, notebooks and discrete graphics processors have been rapid, but primary competitors, Intel and NVIDIA Corp., continue to benefit from meaningfully higher starting market share and stronger financial flexibility.

Intel's financial flexibility will be tested as it embarks on a heavy investment cycle to close AMD's process technology leadership, but is hedging its bet by outsourcing next-generation products to the same foundry AMD uses, potentially eradicating AMD's process advantage in a few years. Meanwhile, NVIDIA remains the clear technology leader in graphics processors for parallel processing for artificial intelligence (AI) applications, a position that an ultimately successful acquisition by NVIDIA of ARM Holdings, plc. would only fortify.

Technology Leadership: Fitch expects Xilinx will leverage its long-time leadership in programmable logic technology to expand addressable markets. Xilinx's leadership in 28-nanometer (nm) and 16nm systems-on-a-chip drives strong adoption across end markets, particularly for 5G deployment in wireless and DC. Its next-generation 7nm-based adaptive compute acceleration platforms (ACAP) are positioned to drive future growth, given secular demand for computing, storage and networking.

Moderating Profit Margins: Fitch expects Xilinx's profit margins will remain solid but be constrained by strong growth in less profitable wireless communications and core broadcast, consumer and automotive markets. However, Xilinx has maintained non-GAAP gross profit margins near record levels, despite headwinds from inflationary input costs and a challenging global trade environment. Xilinx will partially offset margin pressure with sustained revenue growth and robust adoption of ACAP.

Significant Technology Risk: Fitch expects heightened competitive intensity as Xilinx continues pushing further into the semiconductor industry's most attractive growth markets, including DC, automotive, industrial and 5G markets. Fitch expects Xilinx competitors NVIDIA and Intel will leverage their greater financial flexibility to maintain aggressive investment levels, and significant custom application-specific integrated circuits (ASICs) development will sustain displacement risk. As a result, Xilinx's R&D should remain above 20% of revenue.

Meaningful Revenue Diversification: Fitch expects significant product, end-market and geographic diversification will continue to moderate cyclicality. Xilinx's design wins for 5G deployments and long-cycle aerospace and defense should offset weak demand in more macroeconomic-sensitive end markets in fiscal 2021, along with hyperscale DC spending. Xilinx's low-inventory obsolescence risk due to product adaptability also reduces cyclicality. Fitch anticipates Xilinx's focus on DC growth should modestly increase customer concentration from current levels (top-20 customers are 40% of revenue).

Derivation Summary

Xilinx's 'A-' IDR reflects strong intellectual property (IP) in diversified secular growth markets, solid profit margins and FCF conversion. Xilinx compares less favorably with peers Intel (A+/Stable) and NVIDIA (Not Rated) due to their superior financial flexibility, despite a pandemic-induced uptick in both companies' leverage. Intel's very strong market positions in CPUs for personal computers and DC servers drives significant annual FCF, despite the company's significant annual capital spending leading to higher absolute FCF but lower FCF margins than Xilinx.

Robust demand exiting the COVID-19 pandemic in combination with continued supply constraints will support revenue growth through at least the near-term. A higher mix of personal computer (PC) sales will weigh on gross margins, although expectations for a resumption of enterprise and government servers should moderate margin compression.

Fitch expects lower than historical FCF and FCF margins as Intel accelerates process technology development while adding significant capacity to meet expected internal and foundry customer demand. Fitch expects more than $5 billion of annual FCF and FCF margins in the mid-single digits through the forecast period, down from $15 billion and nearly 20% in 2020, respectively. although subsidies could offset investments as the U.S. governments seeks to incentivize the build-out of U.S.-based manufacturing capacity.

Technology-Driven Share Losses: Intel's process technology transition challenges over the past several years have eroded the company's long-standing technology leadership and threatened Intel's dominant market share and premium profit margins. Fitch Ratings-Chicago-25 March 2021: The ratings for Intel Corp. are unaffected by the company's manufacturing strategy update, according to Fitch Ratings. Fitch believes Intel's long-term commitment to manufacturing the majority of its products in-house while expanding the use of foundries for leading edge processors should enable it to moderate the pace of share and profit margin erosion over the next few years. In the update, Intel affirmed 2021 annual guidance, but Fitch expects heightened execution risk and weaker longer-term cash flow from increased investment intensity associated with Intel's foundry services (FS) provider ambitions.

Intel's management has stated that it remains on-track to ship 7-nanometer (nm) lithography products in 2023. However, Fitch anticipates the company will expand outsourced processor manufacturing for advanced geometries until it closes the current technology gap with leading foundry, Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Co., Ltd. (TSMC), which is already developing 5nm- and 3-nm processes. Fitch believes supply chain shortages, ranging from substrates to foundry supply, will limit Intel's top line despite the strong demand environment and constrain its access to leading edge capacity at TSMC until at least 2022.

Overall revenue growth should be roughly flat in 2021 (excluding the anticipated sale of the memory business), following two consecutive years of record revenue and cash flow. Fitch forecasts solid near-term revenue growth for Intel's personal computer (PC) businesses with work/learn-from-home and consumer strength driving demand but share losses in datacenter (DC) markets that should persist through the intermediate term. Meanwhile, the lower sales mix should pressure gross profit margins, which Intel has lowered its guidance for to 56.5%, just above Fitch's mid-50% negative rating sensitivity.

During the update, Intel also announced a plan to aggressively re-establish its foundry business and sketched out partnerships with the broader semiconductor ecosystem, including International Business Machines Corp.'s semiconductor business, service providers and foundries. The development of FS should attract customers looking to diversify supply chains in the U.S. and Europe in response to supply shortages and geopolitical considerations, as well as strong secular demand for semiconductors, but Intel may be challenged to attract customers with whom it also competes.

To meet Intel's aspirations to build a FS business rivaling those of current foundry leaders, Fitch believes investment intensity will need to increase and could weigh on the company's strong FCF profile. Profitability growth will offset higher spending levels over the longer-term but Fitch anticipates equipment purchases and start-up costs will pressure nearer-term cash flow, absent government support. During the update, Intel raised 2021 capital spending guidance by $5 billion to $20 billion to begin construction on two U.S. fabrication facilities that will, in part, support FS development.

As a result of the uptick in capital spending, Intel guided to $10 billion of pre-dividend FCF for 2021, which is below Fitch's previous forecast. Therefore, Fitch estimates pre-dividend FCF to debt near 30% and FCF (post-dividend) margins in the mid- to high-single digits, both of which, if sustained, Fitch believes are more aligned with a mid-'A' rating. Meanwhile, Fitch expects Intel will limit stock buybacks to lower than historical FCF.

Fitch Places Xilinx's 'A-' Ratings on Watch Negative on Proposed Acquisition by AMD

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-30 October 2020: Fitch has placed Xilinx Inc.'s the 'A-' ratings, including the Long-Term Issuer Default Rating (IDR), on Watch Negative on the announcement that the company entered into a definitive agreement to be acquired by Advanced Micro Devices Inc. (AMD) in an all-stock transaction valued at $35 billion. Fitch's actions affect $2.4 billion, including the undrawn $400 million revolving credit facility (RCF). Fitch plans to resolve the RWN upon a review of the final debt and guarantee structure and resolution could result in affirmation at 'A-' or a multi-notch downgrade.

The explanation for the proposed deal asserts that in an intensifying datacenter (DC)-competitive landscape, the combined companies will have an increased revenue scale to support research and development, technology and product diversification and addressable market expansion. Nonetheless, Fitch believes Xilinx bondholders face increased risk related to the sustainability of rapid expected share gains by AMD, given its ascendance in core markets for competitors with still considerably greater market share and financial flexibility.

AMD's process technology advantage over Intel Corp. may very well evaporate in 2022 should Intel outsource leading edge manufacturing to the same foundry producing chips for AMD, enabling Intel to recapture share in certain markets via scale. Graphics capabilities should advantage AMD over Intel in certain artificial intelligence (AI) markets, but NVIDIA Inc.'s still meaningful technology and share leadership positions it to capture the lion's share of rapidly increasing AI penetration.

Meanwhile, Fitch expects solid near-term operating performance for standalone Xilinx driven by strong market positions and end market diversification. Despite uncertainty stemming from the coronavirus pandemic and global trade tensions, automotive production levels are recovering, and Xilinx is benefiting from healthy design win rates and accelerating product ramps in DC and wireless markets. Profit and cash flow margins remain healthy and, in conjunction with more cautious shareholder returns year-to-date, are supporting Xilinx's strong financial profile.

Key Rating Drivers

Coronavirus Impact: Fitch expects Xilinx to weather the coronavirus pandemic relatively well given the company's end market, customer and product diversification resulting in broad based design wins and product ramps. Exposure to more cyclical markets (automotive, broadcast) weighed heavily on revenue in the first half of the year, but recovering automotive production levels and product ramps should drive near-term growth. Product ramps should also augment already strong infrastructure markets, particularly advanced networking products for DC, given work-from-home (WFH) dynamics.

Technology leadership: Fitch expects Xilinx will leverage its long-existing technology leadership in programmable logic as the company continues its strategic focus on platforms that will enable it to expand addressable markets. Xilinx's leadership in 28nm and 16nm systems-on-a-chip (SoC) are driving strong adoption across the company's end markets, particularly for the first wave of 5G deployments in wireless and DC. At the same time, its next generation 7nm-based adaptive compute acceleration platforms (ACAP) are well positioned to drive future growth, given they address robust demand for compute, storage and networking.

Moderating Profit Margins: Fitch expects Xilinx's profit margins will remain solid constrained by strong growth in less profitable wireless communications and core broadcast, consumer and automotive markets. However, Xilinx has maintained record high non-GAAP gross profit margins of 70% in fiscal 1H21, despite facing headwinds from the coronavirus and a challenging global trade environment. Over the intermediate term, Xilinx will partially offset margin pressure with revenue growth, while robust adoption of ACAP should drive longer-term profit margin expansion.

Significant Technology Risk: Despite clear technology leadership in programmable logic, Fitch expects heightened competitive intensity as Xilinx continues pushing further into the semiconductor industry's most attractive growth markets, including the dynamically evolving DC markets, automotive, industrial and 5G. Fitch expects Xilinx competitors, NVIDIA and Intel, will leverage their greater financial flexibility to maintain aggressive investment levels, while significant custom application-specific-integrated-circuits (ASICs) development will sustain displacement risk. As a result, Xilinx's R&D will remain more than 20% of revenue.

Meaningful Revenue Diversification: Fitch expects significant application, and end market and geographic diversification will continue to moderate cyclicality. Xilinx's design wins for 5G deployments and long-cycle A&D, as well as DC spending by hyperscale should offset weak demand in more macroeconomic-sensitive end markets in fiscal 2021. Xilinx's low inventory obsolescence risk due to the company's product adaptability also reduces cyclicality. Nonetheless, Fitch anticipates Xilinx's focus on DC growth should modestly increase customer concentration but from currently relatively low levels with the top 20 customers constituting 40% of revenue and top 50 customers representing 60% of revenue.

Derivation Summary

Xilinx is well positioned at 'A-' given the company's strong IP in a diversified set of secular growth markets and solid profit margins and FCF conversion. Xilinx is less well positioned than its closest peers, Intel Corp. (A+/F1/Stable) and NVIDIA Inc. (A/F1/Stable), due to their superior financial flexibility despite an uptick in leverage for both companies as they navigate through the coronavirus pandemic. Intel's very strong market positions in CPUs for personal computers (PC) and DC servers drives significant annual FCF, despite the company's also significant annual capital spending budget that results in higher absolute FCF but lower FCF margins when compared with Xilinx. Fitch also expects NVIDIA to manage leverage to below 1.5x, and the company benefits from dominant positions in graphics processing units (GPU) that result in high profit margins and substantial FCF. Should Xilinx take share from Intel and NVIDIA, it will be due to the success of its platform strategy and superior technological attributes in computer acceleration, which will drive convergence in the ratings.

Key Assumptions

--Revenue growth down by roughly low- to mid-single digits in fiscal 2021 driven by demand headwinds related to the coronavirus pandemic, partially offset by WFH dynamics, relative strength in DC and 5G deployments;

--Low-single digit recovery fiscal 2022 and the resumption of normalized mid-single digit growth in fiscal 2023;

--Moderate profit margin contraction from a growing mix of lower-gross profit margin revenue offset by only modest operating expense growth, resulting in operating EBITDA margins in the mid- to high-30% range;

--Refinances upcoming debt maturities but manages debt levels to maintain total debt to operating EBITDA below 2x;

--Uses FCF for tuck-in acquisitions and capital returns, although Fitch anticipates stock buybacks will remain muted in fiscal 2021 due to the current lack of visibility.

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-27 October 2020: Fitch Ratings has affirmed the Long- and Short-Term Issuer Default Ratings (IDRs) for Intel Corp. at 'A+'/'F1' and the 'A+' IDR for its wholly-owned subsidiary, Altera Corp., following the company's Oct. 22, 2020 third quarter earnings release. The Rating Outlook is Stable. Fitch's actions affect $35.2 billion of total debt as of Sept. 26, 2020.

The ratings and Outlook reflect Intel's solid financial profile and flexibility, despite expectations for personal computer (PC) and datacenter (DC) market share erosion as the company addresses delays in its advanced manufacturing roadmap. Nonetheless, Fitch believes the company's looming decision on to what extent it outsources leading edge manufacturing could weigh on Intel's credit profile. Structurally lower profit margins and reduced product differentiation the likely result, in any case, but with the attendant capital commitments should the company remain committed to advanced manufacturing.

Over the nearer term, a lower sales mix and 10nm product ramp will constrain gross profit margins after stronger than expected 1H20 operating performance that should still drive record revenue and FCF margins. Strong 10 nanometer (nm) adoption in entry level notebooks and design win strength in Mobileye are unlikely to offset guidance for the continuation of very weak enterprise and government (E&G) demand and cloud digestion. Fitch expects Intel will begin recovery in 2021 with an richer sales mix driving adjusted gross profit margin expansion through the year from mid- to high-50%.

Strong annual FCF will augment elevated cash levels from debt issuance earlier in 2020 amid uncertainty surround the coronavirus to replenish cash as the company completes the $10 billion accelerated share repurchase (ASR) agreement in the current (4Q20). Fitch anticipates Intel will use net proceeds from Intel's proposed sale of its NAND business to SK Hynix for $9 billion to support future shareholder returns rather than building cash in the face of competitive uncertainly, although leverage metrics should remain sound for the rating, including total debt to operating EBITDA below 1.5x.

Key Rating Drivers

Coronavirus Impact: Fitch expects sequentially lower revenue in the second half of the year after government imposed stay-in-place orders and supply chain disruptions drove strong demand for WFH-enabling infrastructure. As a result, revenue was sequentially flat in the typically seasonally down (mid-teens) first half of 2020 and Fitch forecasts operating EBITDA margins on-track to reach a record 50.3% for the full year. Nonetheless, Fitch remains concerned first half strength represented the pull forward of a considerable amount of demand and that, in conjunction with market share losses and the waning PC refresh cycle, revenue growth will decline over the near-term before beginning to recover in the second half of 2021.

Eroding Technology Leadership: Intel's more recent process technology transition challenges have eroded the company's long-standing technology leadership, potentially threatening the company's dominant market share and premium profit margins over the medium term. Process technology is the most important but only one of six dimensions for product differentiation in these markets but Intel's inability to keep pace with technology leaders, Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Co. (TSMC), illustrates heightened risks associated with the breakdown of Moore's Law. It also will result in market share gains by competitors, Advanced Micro Devices Inc. (AMD) and NVIDIA Inc., and prompted Intel to increase outsourcing of leading edge products to TSMC.

Very Strong Market Positions: Intel's strong market positions in DCs and PCs will continue to support the company's ability to intensify investments while driving strong financial flexibility for acquisitions. Positive top-line growth in data-centric businesses, which include the company's IoT, non-volatile memory, programmable solutions and automotive, will more than offset secular shipment declines in PC markets. Intel should continue to benefit from solid growth and market share in PCs, gaming and DC server chips, despite expected near-term share gains by competitors and Fitch's expectation that cloud service providers (CSPs) will support alternative architectures to ensure long-term innovation.

Solid Financial Flexibility: Fitch expects Intel's annual FCF will range from $10 billion to $15 billion over the forecast period, driven by strong operating EBITDA margins (mid- to high-40%) offsetting significant capital intensity (near 20% of revenue). Weaker near-term demand from the coronavirus pandemic will only marginally dampen FCF resulting in a Fitch forecast $12 billion of 2020 FCF after generating $7.8 billion in just the first half of the year. Fitch expects solid FCF beyond the near term due in part to the company's management of the PC business for profitability and cash flow and a shifting sales mix toward higher-margin DC platforms despite faster growing lower margin other data-centric segments.

High Investment Intensity: R&D and capital spending will remain significant and represent 35%-40% of revenue for the foreseeable future even if Intel increases outsourcing of leading edge manufacturing. The company's recent 7nm process technology delay is indicative of the break-down of Moore's Law, although manufacturing competitors, TSMC and Samsung Electronics Ltd., are already producing at volume on 7nm, which should prompt Intel to maintain high investment levels to catch-up. Intel is also investing heavily to drive adoption of its non-volatile memory and develops technology platforms within the context of rapidly evolving emerging IoT, PSG and automotive markets.

Customer Concentration: Fitch expects customer concentration will intensify over time as service providers consolidate IT spending, potentially resulting in more volatile quarterly results in DCG. Fitch anticipates the largest hyperscale cloud companies will continue robust data center capacity deployments over the intermediate term and through cyclical downturns, driven by increasing demand for computing and storage of data growth and ongoing workload migration to public clouds. Telecom service providers will also continue deploying capacity as data proliferates exponentially at the edge, although longer-term growth in IoT and ADAS should mitigate some customer concentration.

Derivation Summary

Fitch believes Intel Corp. (A+/F1/Stable) is less well positioned at the current rating, given its solid financial profile may be insufficient to offset a weakened business profile. Process technology delays over the past several years are requiring Intel to differentiate products on other dimensions at non-leading edge process nodes and evaluate if and to what degree it will outsource 7nm and below manufacturing to technology leader, Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company Ltd. (TSMC), going forward. This is as competitors, both already fabless customers of TSMC and are shipping 7nm products in volume, are poised to take share from Intel - Advanced Micro Devices Inc. (AMD) in processors for servers and personal computers (PC) and NVIDIA Inc. in artificial intelligence (AI) markets, which will intensify further if NVIDIA consummates its proposed acquisition of ARM Holdings Ltd. Outsourcing its advanced node chip manufacturing would further weigh on Intel's rich gross profit margins (high 50% on an non-GAAP basis), but with FCF benefitting from a significant reduction in the company's roughly $15 billion of annual capital spending, the majority focused on leading edge process technology. Intel's $10 billion accelerated share repurchases (ASR) agreement announced on Aug. 21, 2020 likely means the company's debt will remain at roughly current levels with leverage metrics limiting just under Fitch's negative rating sensitivities. Over the longer term, the expectations for automotive and, to a lesser extent, internet of things (IoT), should begin diversifying Intel's revenue mix.

Nonetheless, Fitch remains concerned first half strength represented the pull forward of a considerable amount of demand and that, in conjunction with market share losses and the waning PC refresh cycle, revenue growth will decline over the near-term before beginning to recover in the 2H21.

Eroding Technology Leadership: Intel's more recent process technology transition challenges have eroded the company's long-standing technology leadership, potentially threatening the company's dominant market share and premium profit margins over the medium-term. Process technology is the most important but only one of six dimensions for product differentiation in these markets but Intel's inability to keep pace with technology leaders, Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Co. (TSMC), illustrates heightened risks associated with the breakdown of Moore's Law. It also will result in market share gains by competitors, Advanced Micro Devices Inc. (AMD) and NVIDIA Inc., and prompted Intel to increase outsourcing of leading edge products to TSMC.

Very Strong Market Positions: Intel's strong market positions in DCs and PCs will continue to support the company's ability to intensify investments while driving strong financial flexibility for acquisitions. Positive top-line growth in data-centric businesses, which include the company's Internet of Things (IoT), non-volatile memory, programmable solutions and automotive, will more than offset secular shipment declines in PC markets. Intel should continue to benefit from solid growth and market share in PCs, gaming and DC server chips, despite expected near-term share gains by competitors and Fitch's expectation that cloud service providers (CSPs) will support alternative architectures to ensure long-term innovation.

Solid Financial Flexibility: Fitch expects Intel's annual FCF will range from $10 billion to $15 billion over the forecast period, driven by strong operating EBITDA margins (mid- to high-40%) offsetting significant capital intensity (near 20% of revenue) and double-digit annual dividend growth. Weaker near-term demand from the coronavirus pandemic will only marginally dampen FCF resulting in a Fitch forecasted $12 billion of 2020 FCF after generating $7.8 billion in just 1H20. Fitch expects solid FCF beyond the near term due in part to the company's management of the PC business for profitability and cash flow and a shifting sales mix toward higher-margin DC platforms despite faster growing lower margin other data-centric segments.

High Investment Intensity: R&D and capital spending will remain significant and represent 35%-40% of revenue for the foreseeable future even if Intel increases outsourcing of leading edge manufacturing. The company's recent 7nm process technology delay is indicative of the break-down of Moore's Law, although manufacturing competitors, TSMC and Samsung Electronics Ltd., are already producing at volume on 7nm, which should prompt Intel to maintain high investment levels to catch-up. Intel is also investing heavily to drive adoption of its non-volatile memory and develops technology platforms within the context of rapidly evolving emerging IoT, PSG and automotive markets.

Customer Concentration: Fitch expects customer concentration will intensify over time as service providers consolidate IT spending, potentially resulting in more volatile quarterly results in DCG. Fitch anticipates the largest hyperscale cloud companies will continue robust data center capacity deployments over the intermediate term and through cyclical downturns, driven by increasing demand for computing and storage of data growth and ongoing workload migration to public clouds. Telecom service providers will also continue deploying capacity as data proliferates exponentially at the edge, although longer-term growth in IoT and ADAS should mitigate some customer concentration.

Derivation Summary

Fitch expects solid operating performance for the rating, even in the face of near-term competition in platforms for DC and competing architectures for AI markets over the longer term. Intel compares favorably with its chief competitor for data center server chips, AMD, because of Intel's significantly greater financial flexibility, profit margins and market share. NVIDIA (A/F1/Stable), with whom Intel is competing for AI and deep learning, has a similar profile to Intel, although nominal FCF is meaningfully lower but with significantly higher FCF margins due to its fabless model.

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-01 May 2020: Fitch Ratings has affirmed the ratings for Intel Corporation and its wholly owned subsidiary, Altera Corporation, including the Long- and Short-Term Issuer Default Ratings (IDR) at 'A+' and 'F1'. The Rating Outlook is Stable. Fitch's actions affect $39 billion of total debt as of March 31, 2020.

The ratings and outlook reflect Intel's strong market positions, which positions the company to generate significant FCF while continuing to spend substantially on manufacturing technology innovation through the coronavirus pandemic. Fitch believes recent robust results reflect a temporary acceleration rather than a permanent shift in demand and that datacenter (DC) demand will moderate but continue growing, offsetting expectations for lower PC unit shipments and share losses through the remainder of the year. Beyond the coronavirus pandemic, Fitch expects share losses in DC to offset growth in automotive and internet of things (IoT) markets, potentially weighing on intermediate-term revenue levels and profit margins.

Higher revenue and lower but still solid profitability should translate into strong FCF that, in conjunction with the company's $10.3 billion senior notes offering in the first quarter and suspended share repurchase program, should result in considerable excess cash exiting 2020. Fitch expects Intel will maintain a conservative financial structure and estimates total leverage was at 1.0x, despite the senior notes issuance and lower forecasted profitability. Given headroom under Fitch's 1.5x total debt to operating EBITDA negative rating trigger and $13.4 billion remaining under Intel's $20 billion share repurchase authorization, Fitch believes Intel's higher debt total is likely permanent rather than pre-funding considerable debt maturities over the next three years.

Key Rating Drivers

Coronavirus Impact: Fitch assumes government imposed stay-in-place orders in response to the coronavirus pandemic will result in a significant first-half downturn and partial recovery beginning in the second half of 2020, followed by slow growth to achieve full recovery on a run rate basis exiting 2021. Within this context, Fitch believes end market mix and financial flexibility will determine the coronavirus' impact on semiconductor credit profiles. Issuers with greater exposure to infrastructure markets (5G and DC), such as Intel's DC sales to service providers, will outperform those exposed to more GDP-sensitive markets (e.g. automotive, consumer and industrial).

Technology Leadership: Intel's significant cumulative investments in R&D and manufacturing capabilities have resulted in technology leadership and support Fitch's expectations for top line growth and solid profitability through at least the intermediate term. Significant investments in capital spending throughout its history leaves the company as one of three remaining leading edge semiconductor manufacturers , sustaining the company's cost advantage in data center growth and legacy PC markets. Fitch Ratings-Chicago-23 March 2020: Fitch Ratings has assigned an 'A+' rating to Intel Corporation's $8 billion of senior notes issuance, which will be pari passu with Intel's existing senior unsecured debt obligations. The company will use net proceeds for general corporate purposes, which may include refinancing existing debt and share repurchases, although Fitch expects share repurchases will not exceed FCF for the year. The issuance consists of six tranches and includes:

--$1.5 billion of 3.4% senior notes due March 25, 2025;

--$1 billion of 3.75% senior notes due March 25, 2027;

--$1.5 billion of 3.9% senior notes due March 25, 2030;

--$750 million of 4.6% senior notes due March 25, 2040;

--$2.25 billion of 4.75% senior notes due March 25, 2050;

--$1 billion of 4.95% senior notes due March 25, 2060.

Pro forma for the $8 billion senior notes issuance, as well as the $2.25 billion senior notes issuance in February 2020 and undrawn CP authorization, total debt was $49.3 billion as of Dec. 28, 2019.

The current ratings and outlook reflect Intel's substantial financial flexibility, bolstered by the senior notes issuance (and in light of unfavorable CP market conditions) that will enable the company to absorb lower near-term cash flow related to the coronavirus pandemic. Beyond the near term, the company should continue to benefit from strong share positions in secular growth datacenter markets and personal computers (PC), despite Fitch's expectations for share losses over time. Meanwhile, the development of 5G-enabled semi-autonomous driving and internet of things (IoT) markets provide longer-term growth potential. Fitch expects FCF will be considerably lower than the record $11.4 billion generated in 2019 and that Intel will curtail share repurchases to remain in line with FCF and maintain total debt to operating EBITDA below Fitch's 1.5x negative rating trigger.

Key Rating Drivers

Leading Market Positions: Intel's strong market positions in its data center and PC businesses provide significant revenue scale to support high investment intensity (35%-40% of revenue). Positive top-line growth in data-centric businesses, which include the company's IoT, non-volatile memory, programmable solutions and automotive, will more than offset secular shipment declines in PC markets. Intel should continue to benefit from solid growth and market share in data center server chips, despite expected near-term share gains by AMD and Fitch's expectation that cloud service providers (CSPs) will support alternative architectures to ensure long-term innovation.

Technology Leadership: Intel's significant cumulative investments in R&D and manufacturing capabilities have resulted in technology leadership and support Fitch's expectations for top line growth and solid profitability through at least the intermediate term. Significant investments in capital spending throughout its history leaves the company as one of three remaining semiconductor manufacturers at the leading edge, which has sustained the company's cost advantage in data center growth and legacy PC markets. Nonetheless, delays in Intel's 10nm shipments underscoring technology risk inherent to Intel's growth markets and are expected to result in share losses to AMD and more aggressive pricing, although expanding capabilities at 14nm will drive solid share.

Solid Financial Flexibility: Fitch expects Intel's annual FCF will range from $6 billion-$8 billion, driven by strong operating EBITDA margins (mid-40s) offsetting significant capital intensity (near 20% of revenue) and mid-single-digit annual dividend growth. Deteriorating near-term demand from the coronavirus pandemic and uncertain global trade environment will dampen FCF for 2020. Fitch expects FCF beyond the near term will be solid due in part to the company's management of its PC business for profitability and cash flow in the face of shipment declines and a shifting sales mix toward higher-margin data center platforms. Intel's faster growing other data centric segments will diversify its sales mix but dilute profit margins over time.

High Investment Intensity: R&D and capital spending will continue to represent 35%-40% of revenue for the foreseeable future as Intel builds capacity to drive adoption of its non-volatile memory and develops technology platforms within the context of rapidly evolving emerging IoT, PSG and automotive markets. The company's high investment intensity also supports production technology transitions that will enable the Data Center Group (DCG) segment to maintain strong market share and gross profit margins with CSPs, as well as continue to optimize the legacy PC business for profitability and cash flow.

Customer Concentration: Fitch expects customer concentration will intensify over time as CSP consolidate information technology (IT) spending, potentially resulting in more volatile quarterly results in DCG. Fitch anticipates the largest hyperscale cloud companies will continue robust data center capacity deployments over the intermediate term and through cyclical downturns, driven by increasing demand for computing and storage of explosive data growth and ongoing workload migration to public clouds. Nonetheless, longer-term growth in IoT and ADAS should mitigate some customer concentration.

Derivation Summary

While Fitch expects potentially significant headwinds related to the coronavirus pandemic, operating performance should be solid beyond the near term even in the face of competition in platforms for DC and competing architectures for artificial intelligence (AI) markets over the longer term. Intel compares favorably with its chief competitor for data center server chips, AMD, given Intel's significantly greater financial flexibility, profit margins and market share. NVIDIA (A-/F2/Positive), with whom Intel is competing for AI and deep learning, has a similar profile to Intel, although nominal FCF is meaningfully lower but with significantly higher FCF margins due to its fabless model.

Key Assumptions

--For 2020, the coronavirus pandemic should exacerbate headwinds from cautious enterprise spending, the completion of a Win 10 drive PC refresh cycle and share losses to AMD, meaningfully more than offsetting positive momentum from the resumption of hyperscale spending. As a result, revenue growth could be down in the high-single to low-double digits for 2020.

--Revenue flat to down in 2021 due to competitive pressures in data center markets, including share gains by competitor, AMD, and ARM-based server chip alternatives.

--Mid-single-digit recovery in 2022 as Intel returns its manufacturing footprint to competitiveness and benefits from accelerating growth in non-CPU businesses.

--Gross profit margins down by another 300-500 basis points in 2020 due to a combination of lower revenue and continued 10nm ramp.

--Competitive pressure in data center markets and increasing contribution from lower margin businesses constrain gross profit margins in mid-50% through the remainder of the forecast period with transitions to 7nm and investments in 5nm offsetting strengthening 10nm yields.

--MG&A spending remains near lower 8.5% levels following cost reductions in 2019 and lower variable compensation related to negative revenue growth, while R&D remains at 19.0% of revenue.

--Capital spending of more than 20% through the forecast period.

--$500 million of annual tuck-in acquisitions with no meaningful impact on financial results.

--10% annual dividend growth with FCF used for tuck-in acquisitions and share repurchases.

--Intel refinances all upcoming debt maturities.

RATING SENSITIVITIES

Developments That May, Individually or Collectively, Lead to Positive Rating Action

--Management's commitment to moderate shareholder returns to maintain total leverage below 1x;

--Intel maintains strong (greater than 80% share) in data center markets, despite intensifying competition from alternate architectures;

--Solid adoption of Intel's NSG and the company's IoT, PSG and Mobileye platforms, resulting in these businesses representing half of all non-CCG revenue in aggregate and underscoring the strength of the company's technology roadmap.

Developments That May, Individually or Collectively, Lead to Negative Rating Action

--FCF margins sustained in the mid-single-digits, driven by meaningful data center market share erosion and profit margin contraction, while investment levels remain consistent;

--Negative organic revenue growth from share losses in DCG or weaker than anticipated adoption of Intel's nonvolatile memory and IoT, PSG and Mobileye platforms;

--Total leverage sustained above 1.5x, likely from intensified shareholder returns amid weaker than expected operating performance.

Liquidity and Debt Structure

Liquidity was solid at Dec. 28, 2019 and consisted of: i) $13.1 billion of cash and cash equivalents, short-term investments and trading assets, roughly all of which was readily available and ii) an up to $10 billion CP program with no borrowings outstanding at year end. Intel does not have a revolving credit facility to support its CP program but Fitch views the company's cash flow profile as providing sufficient support, particularly given Fitch's expectation for only moderate use. Fitch's expectation for $6 billion-$8 billion of annual FCF also supports liquidity. Fitch notes Intel faces approximately $6 billion of debt maturities over the next 24 months, which the company could meet with net proceeds from the senior notes issuance or cash flow.

--Capital spending of more than 20% through the forecast period.

--$500 million of annual tuck-in acquisitions with no meaningful impact on financial results.

--10% annual dividend growth with FCF used for tuck-in acquisitions and share repurchases.

--Intel refinances all upcoming debt maturities.

RATING SENSITIVITIES

Developments That May, Individually or Collectively, Lead to Positive Rating Action

--Management's commitment to moderate shareholder returns to maintain total leverage below 1x;

--Intel maintains strong (greater than 80% share) in data center markets, despite intensifying competition from alternate architectures;

--Solid adoption of Intel's NSG and the company's IoT, PSG and Mobileye platforms, resulting in these businesses representing half of all non-CCG revenue in aggregate and underscoring the strength of the company's technology roadmap.

Developments That May, Individually or Collectively, Lead to Negative Rating Action

--FCF margins sustained in the mid-single-digits, driven by meaningful data center market share erosion and profit margin contraction, while investment levels remain consistent;

--Negative organic revenue growth from share losses in DCG or weaker than anticipated adoption of Intel's nonvolatile memory and IoT, PSG and Mobileye platforms;

--Total leverage sustained above 1.5x, likely from intensified shareholder returns amid weaker than expected operating performance.

Liquidity and Debt Structure

Liquidity was solid at Dec. 28, 2019 and consisted of: i) $13.1 billion of cash and cash equivalents, short-term investments and trading assets, roughly all of which was readily available and ii) an up to $10 billion CP program with no borrowings outstanding at year end. Intel does not have a revolving credit facility to support its CP program but Fitch views the company's cash flow profile as providing ample support, particularly given Fitch's expectation for only moderate use. Fitch's expectation for $6 billion-$8 billion of annual FCF also supports liquidity.
