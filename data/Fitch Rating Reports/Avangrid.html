Fitch Ratings-New York-05 September 2024: Fitch Ratings has affirmed the Long-Term Issuer Default Rating (IDR) of Avangrid, Inc. (Avangrid) at 'BBB+' with a Stable Rating Outlook. Fitch has also affirmed the Long-Term IDRs and of Avangrid's following utility subsidiaries:

--The Berkshire Gas Company (BGC) at 'A-'/Stable;

--Central Maine Power Company (CMP) at 'BBB+'/Stable;

--Connecticut Natural Gas Corporation (CNG) at 'A-'/Stable;

--New York State Electric & Gas Corporation (NYSEG) at 'BBB+'/Negative;

--Rochester Gas and Electric Corporation (RGE) at 'BBB+'/Stable; and

--The Southern Connecticut Gas Company (SCG) 'A-'/Stable.

Fitch has in addition affirmed the rating of The United Illuminating Company (UI) at 'A-'. UI's Outlook has been revised to Negative from Stable.

The Stable Outlook on Avangrid reflects the expectation of greater financial support and flexibility upon the completion of the Iberdrola private take-over transaction.

The revision of UI's Outlook to Negative reflects the ongoing regulatory challenges in Connecticut. The recently approved base rate increase and passing of Bill 7 are considered negative for credit quality that can impact cash flow profitability and predictability. Fitch estimates UI's funds from operations (FFO) leverage remains high between 4.3x-4.6x through 2026. Absence of a clear path to improve the regulatory construct in Connecticut, cash flow predictability and leverage below Fitch's negative sensitivity of 4.3x on a sustained basis could result in a downgrade.

Key Rating Drivers

Avangrid:

Weak Financial Metrics: Avangrid's credit metrics was weak in 2023 with FFO leverage of 5.9x mainly due to weak utilities performance from New York and Connecticut. Over the forecast period, absent of any external support, Fitch estimates Avangrid's FFO leverage remains high at about 6.3x for in 2024, and to 5.7x in 2025 and 2026, above its downgrade sensitivity. The elevated leverage can be attributed to large capital spending, which totals to about $11.5 billion over the next three years, and requires material external financing.

Private Takeover from Iberdrola: On May 17, 2024, Iberdrola reached an agreement to acquire the remaining 18.4% interest of Avangrid for $2.6 billion. Iberdrola already owns 81.6% of the company. Post-transaction, Iberdrola intends to take Avangrid private. The proposed transaction has been approved by Avangrid's board of directors; pending shareholder and regulatory approval.

Fitch views the transaction could be credit positive for Avangrid because it could benefit from the financial flexibility of Iberdrola, one of the large utilities holding companies in the world with total assets of about $170.4 billion (€153.8 billion) while Avangrid's total assets is about $45.7 billion as of 2Q24. In Fitch's view, financial flexibility at Avangrid could come in the form of unrestrained timely equity injection or right-sizing dividends to the parent as needed to preserve cash for large capital plan and to improve the company's weak financial measures.

Uplift from Iberdrola Support: Fitch determines Avangrid's Standalone Credit Profile (SCP) to be 'bbb' based upon consolidated metrics, which is one-notch below Iberdrola's 'BBB+' rating. The linkage between the two follows a strong parent/weak subsidiary approach. Fitch considers legal incentives medium as about 20% of Iberdrola's total debt has cross-default provisions or wind-up provisions with Avangrid. Strategic incentives are medium as Iberdrola owns 81.6% of the of Avangrid, which receives material financial contributions from Iberdrola, more than $3 billion in the last three years, and around 40% of Iberdrola's growth is expected to come from Avangrid.

Operational incentives are weak as 70% of Avangrid's EBITDA is derived from regulated U.S. entities with minimal synergies with Iberdrola's other businesses other than offshore wind projects. There is a level of integration between management teams but Avangrid has an independent treasury function. As a result, Fitch equalized Avangrid's rating with Iberdrola. If Avangrid's SCP were to be two or more notches lower than Iberdrola under the same linkage factors, Fitch would limit Avangrid's rating uplift to one-notch lower than Iberdrola. Absent of additional incentives between the two entities, we do not expect the private take-over by Iberdrola would impact the linkage between the two entities.

Mostly Regulated Business Mix: Avangrid's utilities provide electric transmission and distribution and natural gas distribution service in parts of New York, Connecticut, Maine and Massachusetts. Fitch considers the low risk nature of the operations to be supportive of credit quality. CMP and UI have a large, beneficial exposure to electric transmission assets regulated by the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC), which account for nearly 21% of Avangrid's consolidated rate base.

Fitch believes Avangrid's renewable energy business (mostly contracted 9.3GW, accounting for 20% of 2023 EBITDA) has a moderately higher risk profile than the company's regulated utilities. Fitch views favorably the company's decision to find a 60% partner for its onshore renewable growth going forward, as it mitigates need for growth financing.

Weak Regulatory Environment: Fitch considers Avangrid's utility operations to be of relatively low risk; but, the regulatory environment in Maine and Connecticut are considered more challenging as reflected in recent rate case outcomes and utility performances. Also, in authorized ROEs tend to be below the nationwide average, particularly for the New York and Connecticut utilities. Nonetheless, Avangrid's utilities continue to benefit from cost-recovery and revenue-stabilizing mechanisms, such as revenue decoupling, to reduce regulatory lag and provide cash flow stability.

Vineyard Wind Construction Delay - On July 13, 2024, one of the turbine blades from the Vineyard Wind project shattered and fell into the ocean. As a result, the U.S. Bureau of Safety and Environmental Enforcement (BSEE) issued a suspension order that halted construction and operations at the Vineyard wind farm. Initial investigation indicates the issue is likely related to a manufacturing defect of the blade by GE. On August 13, the BSEE allowed limited construction at Vineyard but does not allow further blade installation or power production.

Large Capex Plan: Capex is elevated at the regulated utilities and the renewable energy business, totaling about $11.5 billion over the next three years. The electric and natural gas utilities are ramping up infrastructure spending primarily to enhance safety and grid resilience, comprising around 70% of the total with the remaining spend on renewable operation.

Avangrid's New England Clean Energy Connect (NECEC) electric transmission project includes a 145-mile transmission line that would provide New England with 1,200MW of hydroelectric generation capacity from Quebec, Canada. NECEC is expected to cost over $1.5 billion, a portion of which has already been spent. The project resumed construction in August 2023, after a previous voter ballet to stop construction was overturned by the courts. At this time, Fitch assumes its in-service date will be beyond 2025.

Utility Parent/Subsidiary Linkage: There is parent/subsidiary linkage between Avangrid and its rated utility subsidiaries. Fitch considers the utility subsidiaries to have stronger SCPs then Avangrid. As a result, the linkage between Avangrid and its utility subsidiaries is assessed following weak parent/strong subsidiary factors. Emphasis is placed on the subsidiaries' status as regulated entities. Legal ring fencing is porous, given the general protections afforded by economic regulation, and access and control are also porous.

Avangrid centrally manages the treasury function for all of its utility subsidiaries and is the sole source of equity; however, each subsidiary issues its own long-term debt. Due to the aforementioned assessment, Fitch will limit the difference between Avangrid and any of its higher-rated regulated subsidiaries to two notches.

New York State Electric & Gas Corporation (NYSEG)

Weak Financial Metrics: NYSEG's financials have been weak, with FFO leverage of 5.7x in 2022 and 7.3x in 2023. While the last approved multi-year rate plan provides relief to credit metrics, it is offset by a large capex plan. Fitch projects NYSEG's FFO leverage to remain high in 2024-2026, with the 2024 level similar to 2023's and about 5.3x in 2025 and 2026. These projections assume NYSEG successfully securitizes its regulatory asset associated with unfunded storm costs.

To further deleverage, the company is also pursuing additional measures to ensure timely recovery of expenses including: establish a recovery mechanism for major storm costs, recovery for Climate Leadership and Community Protection Act (CLCPA) Phase 1 while project is under construction, special recovery mechanism for make-ready infrastructure spend and securitization of arrears, which could take multi-years to implement. Absence of a clear regulatory path to reduce leverage below Fitch's negative sensitivity of 5.0x could result in a downgrade.

Large Capex Plan: NYSEG has a large capex plan to execute that is about $600 million more than Fitch previously forecast. The increase in capital spending is largely related to investments in Phase I of the CLCPA. The additional capital spending will pressure credit metrics while these assets are under construction.

Challenging Regulatory Environment but Signs of Improvement: Fitch believes the regulatory environment in New York has improved in the current administration, but is still somewhat challenging. ROEs authorized by the New York Public Service Commission (NYPSC) are among the lowest in the U.S., and New York has a history of political involvement in the regulatory process. However, ratemaking features, such as revenue decoupling, a commodity pass-through mechanism and use of a forward-looking test year, are credit supportive.

Additionally, the recently passed Bill S9339A that allows the use of securitization to recover excess storm costs is beneficial. Discussions between NYSEG and NYPSC to reduce regulatory lag and allow timely recovery of capital investments and storm expenses are ongoing. A constructive outcome can lead to strengthening of NYSEG's financial profile.

Rate Case Settlement Approved: Fitch views the recent approval of NYSEG's joint proposal as positive. NYSEG, NYPSC and other parties entered into a joint settlement agreement for three-year electric and gas rate plans. The settlement approval resulted in annual levelized electric distribution rate increases of $137.3 million for rate year one, $160.7 million for rate year two and $200.6 million for rate year three. NYSEG gas rates increase by $11.7 million for rate year one, $12.4 million for year two and $12.9 million for year three, based on a 9.20% ROE and 48% equity capitalization.

The levelized amounts represent overall returns of 6.40% in rate year one, 6.52% in year two and 6.58% in year three. The agreement also favorably continues revenue decoupling for electric and gas, and institutes rate adjustment mechanisms for items such as storm costs, uncollectible expenses, late payment charges and property taxes.

These new mechanisms are likely to help prevent the build-up of large deferral balances in between rate cases. The rate plan also includes a 50% sharing mechanism if the utilities' earned return exceeds a 9.70% threshold. The approved joint proposal covers the period from May 1, 2023 through April 30, 2026.

The United Illuminating Company (UI)

Regulatory Headwinds Emerge: The recent passing of Bill 7 into law introduces several restrictive measures that can reduce cash flow predictability for utilities. The law imposes restrictions on recoveries, including discontinuation of the electric system improvements charge, and considers several factors, including macroeconomic conditions in the ROE determination, mandatory base rate review within four years, greater gubernatorial oversight, and performance-based restrictions on management compensation if customer bills increase over a certain threshold.

Also, the law gives Public Utilities Regulatory Authority (PURA) greater discretion on the implementation of rate decoupling, which may lead to inconsistencies treatment amongst utilities. Depending on the implementation of these measures, it could lead to higher leverage for utilities and future negative rating actions.

Challenging Rate Case Outcome: Fitch views the outcome of UI's rate case as restrictive and negative for credit quality. The final decision issued on Aug. 25, 2023 recommends a one-year rate hike of $23.0 million at an 8.63% ROE, at a 50% equity capitalization. The 8.63% ROE is inclusive of a 47bps reduction to the proposed of 9.10%, due to penalties for management and operational performance issues.

The new rates came into effect Sept. 1, 2023.This is a material decrease compared to the request of a $130.6 million three-step rate increase at an ROE of 10.2% and 52% equity capitalization. At the end of 1Q24, UI's earned ROE was 4.43%, materially lower than the authorized ROE of 8.63%.

FERC-Regulated Electric Transmission: UI's FERC-regulated electric transmission assets account for more than one-third of the rate base. Fitch considers FERC regulation to be among the most constructive in the utilities sector due to formula rates, strong and predictable cash flows and timely recovery of invested capital. FERC's constructive oversight of UI's growing electric transmission business helps counterbalance Connecticut's restrictive regulatory environment for electric utilities.

Weak Financial Metrics: UI's credit metrics has been high for the past two years with FFO leverage at 4.9x. The recently approved one-year base rate increase was materially lower than expected. Along with a large capital plan to execute, and a challenging regulatory environment in Connecticut that decreases cash flow visibility and predictability, Fitch expects UI's FFO leverage in the range of 4.4x-4.6x through 2025 and just at 4.3x by 2026. Absence of a clear path to improve the regulatory construct in Connecticut, cash flow predictability and leverage below Fitch's negative sensitivity of 4.3x on a sustained basis could result in a downgrade.

The request is attributable to higher investment in gas infrastructure to maintain safety and improve reliability of the gas system by accelerating the replacement of older leak-prone pipes, and for rising operations and maintenance expenses. A final decision is expected by YE 2024.

Adequate Financial Metrics: CNG's financial metrics are strong, benefiting from management's conservative capitalization structure. Fitch expects FFO leverage to average 3.0x through 2026.

Central Maine Power Company (CMP)

Somewhat Challenging Regulatory Environment: Fitch considers the regulatory environment overseen by the Maine Public Utilities Commission (MPUC) to be somewhat challenging. Ratemaking features such as full revenue decoupling and a recovery mechanism for incremental storm restoration costs support CMP's stable financial profile.

Rate Case Settlement Approved: Fitch views a recent approval of CMP's rate settlement as somewhat restrictive. CMP, MPUC and other parties entered into a joint settlement agreement for a two-year electric rate increase, effective July 1, 2023. The settlement approval will result in levelized distribution rate increases of $67.0 million, based upon a 9.35% ROE at a 50% equity capitalization.

The levelized amounts represent a 6.71% return in year one and a 6.74% return in year two. The company's initial filing request was for a three-year $100 million increase based upon 10.2% ROE and a 50% equity capitalization. The adopted settlement includes a hyperinflation reconciliation provision, a downward-only plant addition reconciliation, an earning sharing mechanism above 10.35%, and a stay out provision till July 1, 2025.

The existing revenue decoupling mechanism and storm recovery mechanism continue to remain in place with some modifications. CMP is also required to meet certain customer service metrics related to frequency and duration of outages, call answering and billing services. If they fail there are penalties of up to $8.8 million that may be assessed annually.

FERC-Regulated Electric Transmission: CMP's large electric transmission business accounts for roughly two-thirds of the rate base and is subject to oversight by the FERC, which Fitch considers to be among the most constructive regulatory bodies in the utilities sector. FERC-regulated electric transmission assets receive formula rates, strong and predictable cash flows and timely recovery of invested capital. FERC's constructive oversight of CMP's large electric transmission business helps counterbalance Maine's somewhat restrictive recent rate order.

Adequate Financial Metrics: CMP's financial metrics are expected to weaken over Fitch's forecast period with FFO leverage to average 4.3x through 2026 mainly driven by large capital expenditure. Capital spending for the forecast period average to $380 million per year through 2026, about 25% more than the historic period.

The Berkshire Gas Company (BGC)

Low-Risk Utility Operations: BGC has a low-risk business profile. The utility provides an essential service, benefits from monopolistic characteristics in its service territory, earns a regulated return on investment and has no commodity exposure.

Generally Supportive Regulatory Environment: Fitch considers the regulatory environment overseen by the Massachusetts Department of Public Utilities (DPU) to be supportive of BGC's strong financial profile. BGC has a purchased-gas cost adjustment clause with semi-annual resets and a mechanism that recovers losses due to energy efficiency programs. Full revenue decoupling provides further stability to earnings and cash flow.

Energy Transition Risk: BGC's ratings are predicated on the expectation that the gas infrastructure transition in Massachusetts (MA) will be constructive and gradual and will allow gas utilities sufficient time to comply with various environmental mandates, implement new technologies and adjust their capital structures as necessary. Indication of an accelerated time frame or changes in regulation that are likely to result in significant unrecoverable stranded assets could result in negative rating actions.

In October 2020, the DPU examined the role natural gas local distribution companies in helping to meet the state's climate goal of a net zero emissions target by 2050 and the federal target of reducing greenhouse gas emissions by 50% by 2030 compared with 2005 levels. Subsequently, MA finalized greenhouse gas emission sub-limits for transportation, building and electricity sectors, among others. At this time, there are no significant changes in the ability to recover costs; however, the company has some exposure to state regulations or policy changes that could introduce additional costs and risks.

Recent Rate Case Settlement: The DPU issued an order in October, 2022 authorizing multi-step rate increases of up to $5.7 million for BGC. The rate increases will be effective Jan. 1, 2023 and Jan. 1, 2024 and reflect a 9.7% ROE at 54% equity capitalization which is the same as the utility's prior rate case, but higher than some other recently settled rate cases. The increase is attributable to hiring of incremental safety and reliability personnel and certain capital projects already placed into service. The approved settlement also requires BGC to stay out till Nov. 1, 2025.

Adequate Financial Metrics: BGC's financial metrics are adequate for its rating, benefiting from management's conservative capitalization structure. Fitch expects FFO leverage to average 3.8x through 2026.

Derivation Summary

Avangrid is weaker than peers at the 'BBB+' rating. Avangrid's business risk profile is supported by the company's ownership of low-risk, regulated electric and natural gas utilities. The utility subsidiaries of Avangrid and peer Eversource Energy (BBB/Stable) operate in some of the same states in the Northeast in regulatory environments that have grown weaker. Avangrid and Eversource benefit from a meaningful amount of regulatory diversification and significant exposure to electric transmission assets that are regulated by FERC, favorable factors that peer Consolidated Edison, Inc. (BBB+/Stable) lacks.

Avangrid's unregulated renewable energy business accounts for around 20% of consolidated EBITDA, weakening its business risk profile. Avangrid and Eversource are also engaged in the development of large offshore wind projects in the Northeast, which include increased risk during the multiyear permitting and construction phases, but would provide long-term contracted cash flows once in operation.

Avangrid's FFO leverage has been deteriorating due to weakness in its NY and Connecticut operations. Fitch expects leverage to improve with the securitization law passed in NY that allows utilities to recover excess storm expenses. Fitch forecasts FFO leverage to remain high in 2024 at 6.3x before reaching to 5.7x in 2025 and 2026. For Eversource, Fitch forecast leverage of over 7.0x and improving to around 5.5x by the end of the forecast period upon completion of its asset sales and equity issuance plans.

Key Assumptions

--Adjusted earnings per share CAGR of 6%-7% through 2025;

--Total capex in line with management assumptions;

--Networks rate base growing at 7% CAGR through 2025;

--Offshore wind projects: Vineyard Wind I going into service in 2023, with full capacity reached in 2024. No other offshore wind project is budgeted in the forecast period;

--Targeted payout ratio of 65%-75%;

--Normal weather;

--Debt maturities to be refinanced.

Best/Worst Case Rating Scenario

International scale credit ratings of Non-Financial Corporate issuers have a best-case rating upgrade scenario (defined as the 99th percentile of rating transitions, measured in a positive direction) of three notches over a three-year rating horizon; and a worst-case rating downgrade scenario (defined as the 99th percentile of rating transitions, measured in a negative direction) of four notches over three years. The complete span of best- and worst-case scenario credit ratings for all rating categories ranges from 'AAA' to 'D'. Best- and worst-case scenario credit ratings are based on historical performance. Liquidity and Debt Structure

Adequate Liquidity: Fitch considers the liquidity for Avangrid and each of its regulated utility subsidiaries to be adequate. Avangrid's liquidity is primarily supported by the company's $2.0 billion CP program, which is backstopped by a $3.575 billion revolving credit facility (RCF) that matures on Nov. 22, 2026. Avangrid shares this RCF with seven of the company's regulated utility subsidiaries: BGC, CMP, CNG, NYSEG, RGE, SCG and UI.

Fitch believes AVANGRID's SCP is consistent with a 'BBB' rating in its base case which assumes the merger with PNM Resources Inc (PNMR) is completed, closing in 2023. In that scenario, AVANGRID's business risk profile improves and leverage would spike higher in 2023 and 2024 before improving to below 5.5x in 2025. Regulated utility operations would represent approximately 80% of consolidated AVANGRID cash flow, up from approximately 70% prior to merger close.

If regulators were to reject AVANGRID's proposed acquisition of PNMR, FFO leverage would improve compared to our base case, averaging around 4.7x over the next three years, with no improvement in business mix. In this instance, Fitch believes AVANGRID's SCP would remain consistent with a 'BBB+' rating.

Key Rating Drivers

Uplift From Iberdrola Support: Fitch determines AVANGRID's SCP to be 'BBB' based upon consolidated metrics, which is one-notch below Iberdrola's 'BBB+' rating. The linkage between AVANGRID and Iberdrola follows a strong parent/weak subsidiary approach and as such Fitch has equalized the IDRs of AVANGRID and Iberdrola.

In applying its Parent and Subsidiary Linkage Rating Criteria, Fitch scores legal incentives at medium as approximately 20% of Iberdrola's total debt has cross-default provisions or winding-up provisions with AVANGRID. Strategic incentives are medium as Iberdrola owns 81.6% of the common shares of AVANGRID, and AVANGRID receives material financial contribution from Iberdrola (over $3 billion in the last two years) and approximately 40% of Iberdrola's growth is expected to come from AVANGRID.

Fitch views operational incentives as weak as 70% of the AVANGRID's EBITDA is derived from regulated U.S. entities with little operational overlap with Iberdrola's other businesses other than on the offshore wind projects. While there is a level of integration between management teams, AVANGRID has an independent treasury function and, as a publicly traded entity, can access equity separately. As a result of stronger parent linkage factor assessments, Fitch has equalized AVANGRID's rating with Iberdrola. If AVANGRID's SCP were to be two or more notches lower than Iberdrola under the same linkage factors, Fitch would limit AVANGRID's rating uplift to one-notch lower than Iberdrola.

Mostly Regulated Business Mix: AVANGRID's utilities provide electric transmission and distribution (T&D) and natural gas distribution service in parts of New York, Connecticut, Maine and Massachusetts. Fitch considers the low risk nature of the operations to be supportive of credit quality. Fitch believes AVANGRID's renewable energy business (mostly contracted 9.2GW, accounting for 30% of 2022 EBITDA) has a moderately higher risk profile than the company's regulated utilities. Fitch views favorably, the company's decision to find a 60% partner for its onshore renewable growth going forward, as it mitigates need for growth financing.

Authorized ROEs tend to be below the nationwide average, particularly for the New York and Connecticut utilities. The project is expected to start operations by YE 2023 and be fully operational in 2024, adding significant contracted cashflows.

The company views the current contract terms as being weak for profitable development given the escalation in costs over the last 3-4 years. Liquidity and Debt Structure

Adequate Liquidity: Fitch considers the liquidity for AVANGRID and each of its regulated utility subsidiaries to be adequate. AVANGRID's liquidity is primarily supported by the company's $2.0 billion CP program, which is backstopped by a $3.575 billion revolving credit facility (RCF) that matures on Nov. 22, 2026. AVANGRID shares this RCF with seven of the company's regulated utility subsidiaries: BGC, CMP, CNG, NYSEG, RGE, SCG and UI. The RCF contains maximum sublimits of $2.5 billion for AVANGRID at the parent level; $700 million for NYSEG; $300 million for RG&E; $200 million for CMP; $250 million for UI; $150 million each for CNG and SCG; and $50 million for BGC. There were $397 million of CP and no RCF borrowings outstanding as of Dec. 31, 2022, leaving $3.178 billion of availability under the RCF.

AVANGRID uses its CP program as a source of funding to provide its regulated utility subsidiaries with loans under a bilateral credit agreement. CMP, NYSEG, RGE and UI each can borrow up to $500 million under the agreement; CNG and SCG can each borrow up to $250 million and BGC can borrow up to $50 million.

The regulated utilities participate in a virtual money pool, which allows AVANGRID's investment grade regulated utility subsidiaries to lend to or borrow from each other, enabling AVANGRID to efficiently manage the cash at its regulated utilities. CMP, CNG, NYSEG, RGE, SCG and UI each have a lending/borrowing limit of $100 million, and BGC has a lending/borrowing limit of $15 million.

AVANGRID also has a $500 million credit facility with Iberdrola Financiacion, S.A.U., a subsidiary of Iberdrola. This facility matures on June 18, 2023 and had no borrowings outstanding as of Dec. 31, 2022. AVANGRID and its regulated utilities require modest cash on hand to fund their operations, and it had $69 million of unrestricted cash and cash equivalents as of Dec. 31, 2022.

Long-term debt maturities over the next five years are manageable.

Fitch Ratings-New York-21 October 2020: Fitch Ratings has affirmed the 'BBB+' Long-Term Issuer Default Rating (IDR) of AVANGRID, Inc. and maintained the Stable Rating Outlook following the company's announcement that it has reached an agreement to acquire PNM Resources, Inc. (not rated). The acquisition is expected to be all cash, and AVANGRID has received a funds commitment letter from its ultimate parent and 81.5% owner, Iberdrola S.A. (BBB+/Stable), for the $4.3 billion acquisition cost.

Fitch expects the acquisition to increase the regulated portion of AVANGRID's consolidated EBITDA to 80% from 75%, improving the consolidated business mix. The acquisition is expected to be accretive to earnings, while possibly slightly improving FFO leverage.

The acquisition requires approval from the New Mexico Public Regulation Commission, the Public Utility Commission of Texas, the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission, the Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States and other regulatory bodies. The acquisition is expected to close in the second half of 2021.

The ratings on AVANGRID's existing utility subsidiaries are unaffected by this transaction.

Key Rating Drivers

Low-Risk Utility Operations: AVANGRID's Long-Term IDR largely reflects the strength of the company's eight utility subsidiaries: The Berkshire Gas Company (BGC; A-/Stable); Central Maine Power Company (CMP; BBB+/Stable); Connecticut Natural Gas Corporation (CNG; A-/Stable); New York State Electric & Gas Corporation (NYSEG; BBB+/Stable); Rochester Gas and Electric Corporation (RGE; BBB+/Stable); The Southern Connecticut Gas Company (SCG; A-/Stable); The United Illuminating Company (UI; A-/Stable) and Maine Natural Gas Corporation (MNGC; not rated).

These utilities provide electric transmission and distribution, and natural gas distribution service in areas of New York, Connecticut, Maine and Massachusetts. CMP and UI also have a large and beneficial exposure to electric transmission assets regulated by the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC), which account for nearly 21% of AVANGRID's consolidated rate base.

AVANGRID's utilities have a low-risk business profile. The utilities provide an essential service, benefit from monopolistic characteristics in their service territories, earn regulated returns on investment and have no commodity exposure.

Balanced Regulatory Environment: AVANGRID has a relatively balanced regulatory environment, incorporating utility operations in New York, Connecticut, Maine and Massachusetts, and FERC-regulated electric transmission assets in Maine and Connecticut. Authorized ROEs are lower than the nationwide average, particularly for the New York and Connecticut utilities, but cost-recovery and revenue-stabilizing mechanisms, such as revenue decoupling, help reduce regulatory lag and provide cash flow stability.

Renewable Energy Business: Fitch believes AVANGRID's renewable energy business has a moderately higher risk profile than the company's regulated utilities. AVANGRID owns an aggregate of nearly 7.4 gigawatts (GW) of renewable energy generation capacity, nearly all of which is onshore wind. At Dec. 31, 2019, approximately 69% of capacity was contracted for an average period of 9.5 years and an additional 6% was hedged, mitigating some of the business risk. The company's renewable energy business currently accounts for nearly 25% of consolidated EBITDA, with that percentage expected to decrease to 20% following the acquisition of PNM Resources.

Offshore Wind: Vineyard Wind, AVANGRID's 50/50 joint venture with Copenhagen Infrastructure Partners (CIP), is developing an 800MW offshore wind farm southeast of Martha's Vineyard. A delay from the Bureau of Ocean Energy Management (BOEM) in issuing its environmental impact study and record of decision has pushed back the expected in-service date of the Vineyard Wind project to no earlier than 2023. AVANGRID's share of the cost in Vineyard Wind is expected to total $1.4 billion. AVANGRID is also partnered with CIP on the development of the 804MW Park City Wind Project off the shore of New England, which has an expected in-service date of 2025.

Fitch expects the development of these large, multibillion dollar offshore wind projects will slightly weaken AVANGRID's leverage metrics over the near term. Cost overruns, further delays or other problems in getting these large offshore wind projects in service could pressure credit ratings due to the large cost and multiyear construction time frame; the projects will not generate cash flow until after they go into service.

Large Capex Plan: Capex is increasing significantly in the forecast, both at the regulated utilities and the renewable energy business. The electric and natural gas utilities are ramping up infrastructure spending primarily related to safety and resiliency of the grid. AVANGRID's New England Clean Energy Connect (NECEC) electric transmission project includes a 145-mile transmission line that would provide New England with 1,200MW of hydroelectric generation capacity from Quebec, Canada. NECEC is expected to cost approximately $950 million. Permitting delays have pushed the start of construction back to 3Q20 and for the project to be in service by YE 2022.

Strong growth in the renewable energy business is also contributing to AVANGRID's large capex plan. AVANGRID expects to increase its renewable energy portfolio by at least 2.1GW of generation capacity over 2020-2024, including more than 1.6GW of onshore wind and solar and 400MW of offshore wind.

Weakening Financial Metrics: AVANGRID's financial metrics are expected to weaken over the forecast period, but remain supportive of credit quality. The company's moderately leveraged balance sheet is expected to be less of a strength going forward due to an increased amount of debt used to finance growth initiatives. Fitch expects FFO leverage to average around 5.0x through 2023, which is greater than the long-term negative sensitivity of 4.8x. AVANGRID's elevated leverage over the forecast period is due to the company's offshore wind growth projects, which require multiple years of investment, permitting and construction. Fitch does not expect cash inflows from Vineyard Wind until YE23, resulting in higher near-term leverage.

However, once the offshore wind projects are on line, they are expected to provide robust and stable cash flows supported by long-term contracts and near-term tax benefits. The strength and stability of these future cash flows provide some comfort that AVANGRID's FFO leverage metric will likely return within the threshold of Fitch's long-term negative sensitivity. Significant delays and cost-overruns, or other project concerns, could result in Fitch taking a negative rating action.

Parent/Subsidiary Linkage: Fitch uses a bottom-up approach in determining the ratings on AVANGRID and its utility subsidiaries. The linkage follows a weak parent/strong subsidiary approach. Fitch considers the utility subsidiaries to be stronger than AVANGRID due to the utilities' low-risk regulated operations and exposure to relatively balanced regulatory jurisdictions.

There is weak linkage between AVANGRID's Long-Term IDR and that of each of the utility subsidiaries. The utilities have good access to debt capital markets and benefit from regulatory ring-fencing measures, including a minimum equity ratio requirement, commitments to maintain separate books and records, and a prohibition on commingling of funds. These provisions enhance the utilities' credit profiles by adding a layer of protection from AVANGRID, AVANGRID's nonregulated renewable energy business, and ultimate parent Iberdrola. Fitch could allow for a two- to three-notch difference in the Long-Term IDR of AVANGRID and that of any of its utility subsidiaries.

There is moderate linkage between AVANGRID and Iberdrola. Iberdrola owns 81.5% of the common shares of AVANGRID. However, AVANGRID is publicly traded and has independent treasury functions, its own access to the equity market and a separate management team. At AVANGRID's current rating level, Fitch would consider a maximum one-notch difference in its Long-Term IDR and that of Iberdrola.

Fitch Ratings-Barcelona-21 October 2020: Fitch Ratings has affirmed Iberdrola S.A.'s 'BBB+' Long-Term Issuer Default Rating (IDR) with a Stable Outlook, following the group's agreement to acquire 100% of US utility network PNM Resources, Inc. The affirmation considers the additional pressure on consolidated funds from operations (FFO) net leverage by 2022 following the acquisition of PNMR, but also the benefit of the new hybrid's 50% equity content. It also considers a marginal improvement in Iberdrola's average operating environment and in the regulated portion of the group's business profile. As a result of the transaction, Iberdrola's rating headroom would be exhausted from 2022.

The Stable Outlook is supported by Fitch's expectations of a credit-supportive business plan update in November 2020, including additional defensive measures to protect post-transaction credit ratios and maintenance of its FFO-to-net debt financial target by 2022 at 22%, which is commensurate with the 'BBB+' rating.

Key Rating Drivers

IBERDROLA

Exhausted Rating Headroom: The transaction places additional pressure on Iberdrola's consolidated FFO net leverage by 2022, which is mitigated by the equity content of the new hybrid. Fitch forecasts around 5.0x FFO net leverage by 2022 (compared with 4.6x before), which is slightly above the negative sensitivity for the 'BBB+' rating of 4.9x.

Fitch Ratings-New York-07 April 2020: LEAD PARAGRAPH

Fitch Ratings has assigned a 'BBB+' rating to AVANGRID, Inc.'s issuance of senior unsecured green bonds. The green bonds rank pari passu with AVANGRID's other senior unsecured debt.

Net proceeds will be used to finance eligible green expenditures.

AVANGRID's Long-Term Issuer Default Rating (IDR) is 'BBB+'/Outlook Stable.

Key Rating Drivers

Renewable Energy Business: Fitch believes AVANGRID's renewable energy business has a moderately higher risk profile than the company's regulated utilities. AVANGRID owns an aggregate of nearly 7.4 gigawatts (GW) of renewable energy generation capacity, nearly all of which is onshore wind. At Dec. 31, 2019, approximately 69% of capacity was contracted for an average period of 9.5 years and an additional 6% was hedged, mitigating some of the business risk. The company's renewable energy business accounts for nearly 25% of consolidated EBITDA.

Large Capex Plan: Capex is increasing significantly in the forecast, both at the regulated utilities and the renewable energy business. The electric and natural gas utilities are ramping up infrastructure spending primarily related to safety and resiliency of the grid. AVANGRID's New England Clean Energy Connect (NECEC) electric transmission project includes a 145-mile transmission line that would provide New England with 1,200MW of hydroelectric generation capacity from Quebec, Canada. NECEC is expected to cost approximately $950 million. Permitting delays have pushed the start of construction back to third quarter 2020 and for the project to be in service by the end of 2022.

Strong growth in the renewable energy business is also contributing to AVANGRID's large capex plan. AVANGRID expects to increase its renewable energy portfolio by at least 2.1GW of generation capacity over 2020-2024, including more than 1.6GW of onshore wind and solar and 400MW of offshore wind.

Offshore Wind: Vineyard Wind, AVANGRID's 50:50 joint venture with Copenhagen Infrastructure Partners (CIP), is developing an 800MW offshore wind farm southeast of Martha's Vineyard. A delay from the Bureau of Ocean Energy Management (BOEM) in issuing its environmental impact study and record of decision has pushed back the expected in service date of the Vineyard Wind project to no earlier than 2023. AVANGRID's share of the cost in Vineyard Wind is expected to total $1.4 billion. AVANGRID is also partnered with CIP on the development of the 804MW Park City Wind project, which has an expected in service date of 2025. Cost overruns, further delays or other problems in getting these large offshore wind projects in service could pressure credit ratings due to the large cost and multiyear construction timeframe; the projects will not generate cash flow until after they go into service.

Weakening Financial Metrics: AVANGRID's financial profile benefits from a relatively conservative capital structure and robust cash flow. However, the company's moderately levered balance sheet is expected to be less of a strength going forward due to an increased amount of debt used to finance growth initiatives. AVANGRID's financial metrics are expected to weaken over the forecast period, but remain supportive of the rating.

Fitch expects FFO leverage to average around 5.0x through 2022, but improve in 2023, when Vineyard Wind starts generating operating cash flow.

Balanced Regulatory Environment: AVANGRID has a relatively balanced regulatory environment, incorporating utility operations in New York, Connecticut, Maine and Massachusetts, and FERC-regulated electric transmission assets in Maine and Connecticut. Authorized ROEs are lower than the nationwide average, particularly for the New York and Connecticut utilities, but cost-recovery and revenue-stabilizing mechanisms, such as revenue decoupling, help reduce regulatory lag and provide cash flow stability.

Parent/Subsidiary Linkage: Fitch uses a bottom-up approach in determining the ratings on AVANGRID and its utility subsidiaries. The linkage follows a weak parent/strong subsidiary approach. Fitch considers the utility subsidiaries to be stronger than AVANGRID due to the utilities' low-risk regulated operations and exposure to relatively balanced regulatory jurisdictions.

There is weak linkage between AVANGRID's Long-Term IDR and that of each of the utility subsidiaries. The utilities have good access to debt capital markets and benefit from regulatory ring-fencing measures, including a minimum equity ratio requirement, commitments to maintain separate books and records, and a prohibition on commingling of funds. These provisions enhance the utilities' credit profiles by adding a layer of protection from AVANGRID, AVANGRID's nonregulated renewable energy business, and ultimate parent Iberdrola, S.A. (BBB+/Stable). Fitch could allow for a two-to-three-notch difference in the Long-Term IDR of AVANGRID and that of any of its utility subsidiaries.

There is moderate linkage between AVANGRID and Iberdrola. Iberdrola owns 81.5% of the common shares of AVANGRID. However, AVANGRID is publicly traded and has independent treasury functions, its own access to the equity market and a separate management team. At AVANGRID's current rating level, Fitch would consider a maximum one-notch difference in AVANGRID's Long-Term IDR and that of Iberdrola.

Derivation Summary

AVANGRID is well positioned at its 'BBB+' rating. AVANGRID has a good business risk profile, supported by the company's ownership of low-risk, regulated electric and natural gas utilities. The utility subsidiaries of AVANGRID and peer Eversource Energy (BBB+/Stable) operate in some of the same states in the Northeast in relatively balanced regulatory environments. AVANGRID and Eversource both benefit from a meaningful amount of regulatory diversification and significant exposure to electric transmission assets that are regulated by FERC, favorable factors that peer Consolidated Edison, Inc. (BBB+/Stable) lacks. AVANGRID's unregulated renewable energy business accounts for nearly 25% of consolidated EBITDA, weakening AVANGRID's business risk profile because of the riskier nature of these assets compared with that of regulated utilities, and limiting the likelihood of a rating upgrade.

AVANGRID's financial metrics are expected to weaken over the forecast period, but remain adequate for the rating. Fitch expects FFO leverage to average around 5.0x through 2022, similar to the financial metrics of peers. Fitch expects Eversource's FFO leverage to average around 5.3x-5.5x through 2023.

Liquidity and Debt Structure

Adequate Liquidity: Fitch considers the liquidity for AVANGRID and each of its regulated utility subsidiaries to be adequate.

AVANGRID's liquidity is primarily supported by the company's $2.0 billion CP program, which is backstopped by a $2.5 billion revolving credit facility (RCF) that matures on June 29, 2024. AVANGRID jointly shares this RCF with seven of the company's regulated utility subsidiaries: The Berkshire Gas Company (BGC; A-/Stable), Central Maine Power Company (CMP; BBB+/Stable), Connecticut Natural Gas Corporation (CNG; A-/Stable), New York State Electric & Gas Corporation (NYSEG; BBB+/Stable), Rochester Gas and Electric Corporation (RGE; BBB+/Stable), The Southern Connecticut Gas Company (SCG; A-/Stable) and The United Illuminating Company (UI; A-/Stable). The RCF contains maximum sublimits of $2 billion for AVANGRID at the parent level; $400 million each for CMP, NYSEG, RGE and UI; $150 million each for CNG and SCG; and $40 million for BGC. There was $859 million of CP outstanding and no RCF borrowings outstanding as of Feb. 28, 2020, leaving $1.641 billion of availability under the RCF.

AVANGRID uses its CP program as a source of funding to provide its regulated utility subsidiaries with loans under a bilateral credit agreement. CMP, NYSEG, RGE and UI each can borrow up to $500 million under the agreement; CNG and SCG each can borrow up to $250 million and BGC can borrow up to $50 million.

The regulated utilities participate in a virtual money pool, which allows AVANGRID's investment-grade-rated, regulated utility subsidiaries to lend to or borrow from each other, enabling AVANGRID to efficiently manage the cash at its regulated utilities. CMP, CNG, NYSEG, RGE, SCG and UI each have a lending/borrowing limit of $100 million, and BGC has a lending/borrowing limit of $15 million.

AVANGRID also has a $500 million credit facility with Iberdrola Financiacion, S.A.U., a subsidiary of Iberdrola. This facility matures on June 18, 2023 and had no borrowings outstanding as of Dec. 31, 2019.

AVANGRID and its regulated utilities require modest cash on hand to fund their operations. AVANGRID had $178 million of unrestricted cash and cash equivalents as of Dec. 31, 2019.

Long-term debt maturities over the next five years are manageable. At the parent level, AVANGRID has $450 million of 4.625% senior unsecured notes due Oct. 1, 2020 and nothing else due until $600 million of 3.15% senior unsecured notes mature on Dec. 1, 2024.
