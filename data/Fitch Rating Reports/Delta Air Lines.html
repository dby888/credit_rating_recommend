Fitch Affirms Delta Air Lines at 'BBB-'; Revises Outlook to Positive

Fitch Ratings-Toronto-29 August 2025: Fitch Ratings has affirmed Delta Air Lines, Inc.'s (Delta) Long-Term Issuer Default Rating at 'BBB-' and revised the Rating Outlook to Positive from Stable. Fitch has also affirmed Delta's LaGuardia industrial revenue bonds at 'BBB' and its unsecured ratings at 'BBB-'.

The Outlook revision reflects significant debt reduction and progress toward achieving the company's conservative financial targets. Delta reduced adjusted gross debt by roughly $12 billion over the past three years and expects unencumbered assets to reach $40 billion over the intermediate term as capex is primarily funded through internal cash flows. Fitch forecasts EBITDAR leverage in the low-to-mid-2x range, fixed-charge coverage above 4.5x, and positive FCF over the next several years, in-line with 'BBB' credit metrics.

Delta benefits from a solid business profile underpinned by its leading position in key markets and strong brand reputation. Delta also benefits from growing premium and loyalty-related revenues, providing diversification in a historically cyclical industry. The ratings also take into consideration Delta's conservative financial and capital allocation policies.

Key Rating Drivers

Debt Reduction; Declining Leverage: Fitch expects Delta's leverage to be in the low-to-mid 2x range over the next 12-18 months, supporting the Positive Outlook. Delta ended 2Q25 with EBITDAR leverage at 2.5x, down from 3.0x at YE 2023. Delta repaid $4.6 billion debt in 2024 and a further $1.5 billion in 1H25, bringing gross debt and lease liabilities to $23 billion, down from nearly $33 billion at YE 2022. Fitch anticipates a gradual decline in leverage to around 2.0x over the medium term, primarily through scheduled debt maturities.

Improving Market Conditions: Delta and other network carriers have reported stable or improving booking trends as economic uncertainty from 2Q25 abates. The industry is also benefiting from slower capacity growth, particularly in domestic markets, as airlines focus on more profitable routes and key hubs. Delta is well-positioned to generate strong operating margins and cash flows, as yields have started to recover after declining for most of 2025.

Demand for premium products remains robust as consumers increasingly upgrade for better experiences. Delta reported a 6% increase in premium revenue in 1H25, despite lower overall airfares. Although premium product trends remain strong, the industry risks becoming over-indexed to this segment as more carriers adopt similar strategies. Fitch believes Delta's strong hub positions, industry-leading operating metrics, and customer loyalty provide durable competitive advantages.

Solid FCF Profile: Fitch expects Delta to generate positive FCF in 2025-2028. We expect margins to compress slightly in 2025, driven by lower unit revenues and a small increase in unit costs. Although 1H25 results are weaker than the prior year's, we expect Delta's profitability to remain among the industry's best. Fitch anticipates EBITDAR margins to stabilize in the mid-teens in the rating horizon, with sufficient cash from operations to cover capex of around 9% of revenue annually. This will likely drive FCF margins in the low-single digits as a percentage of revenue, allowing Delta to manage its sizable capex plan while reducing debt and gradually increasing shareholder returns.

Conservative Financial Policy: Delta maintains a conservative financial policy, reflected in its recently revised gross leverage target of 1.0x, down from the previous 2.0x-3.0x range. The company has prioritized debt reduction in recent years to strengthen its balance sheet. Delta recently resumed its share repurchase program but repurchases remains modest compared to expected FCF. Fitch expects shareholder returns to grow gradually as the company prioritizes debt reduction and growing its unencumbered assets.

Loyalty and Non-Traditional Revenues: Airline loyalty program revenue continue to grow in importance and could be an offsetting factor in economic downturns. Delta expects to generate $8 billion in total remuneration from its partnership with American Express in 2025 and aims to reach $10 billion annually in the long term. Loyalty revenue has proven resilient, rebounding to pre-pandemic levels by YE 2021, ahead of travel-related revenue. Delta reports that its loyalty programs, premium products, cargo, and other non-main-cabin revenue now makes up 62% of total revenues, up from 60% in 2024 and 58% in 2023.

Strong Business Profile: Delta's investment-grade rating is supported by its position as a leading network airline in the consolidated U.S. market. Dominant positions in key hubs and a high level of network utility provide a competitive advantage over smaller airlines while driving growth in Delta's lucrative loyalty program. The company benefits from greater size and scale relative to non-U.S. network carriers. Geographic diversification, along with diverse non-airline revenue streams including loyalty, maintenance, repair and overhaul (MRO), and vacations businesses also compare favorably to smaller peers.

Peer Analysis

Delta's 'BBB-' rating is higher than that of its two major network competitors, United Airlines, Inc. (BB/Positive) and American Airlines, Inc. (B+/Stable). The rating differential is driven in part by the fact that Delta has maintained leverage at more favorable levels and better FCF metrics than its peers historically, which Fitch expects to continue through the forecast period. Delta maintains a lower total debt balance than United, although they are similar on a net basis.

However, Delta has lower capex commitments than United in the coming four years and better prospects for FCF generation along with lower execution risk in its fleet renewal plan. Delta is rated two notches lower than Southwest Airlines Co. (BBB+/Negative). The rating differential is largely driven by Southwest's net cash position. However, Southwest has limited rating headroom due to near-term profitability pressures.

Delta compares favorably with investment-grade peers in Europe. Fitch expects Delta to maintain gross leverage in line with Deutsche Lufthansa's (BBB-/Stable) and marginally better than Air France/KLM's (BBB-/Stable). We expect Delta's FCF metrics to be better than its European counterparts'. Delta's business profile also benefits from its size and foreign-exchange exposure relative to European peers.

Key Assumptions

-- Capacity growth of around 3.5% in 2025 and in low-single digits annually in 2026-2028;

-- Continued passenger growth, keeping load factors at 83%-84%;

-- Slight decline in unit revenues in 2025, followed by low single-digit growth to 2028;

-- Jet fuel prices averaging around $2.35/gallon in 2025, implying Brent crude prices in the mid $70/barrel range;

-- Capex in line with Delta's public guidance;

-- Gradual increase in share repurchases and dividends through the forecast.

RATING SENSITIVITIES

Factors that Could, Individually or Collectively, Lead to Negative Rating Action/Downgrade

--Sustained adjusted debt/EBITDAR above 3.3x or EBITDAR/interest plus rents falling below 3.5x on a sustained basis;

--FCF margins declining to neutral on a sustained basis;

--Deviation in capital allocation and financial policies, leading to a reduction in liquidity or leverageable assets.

Factors that Could, Individually or Collectively, Lead to Positive Rating Action/Upgrade

--Sustained commitment to conservative financial policies leading to adjusted debt/EBITDAR below 2.5x;

--Establishment and commitment to a financial policy that maintains robust through-the-cycle financial flexibility;

--EBITDAR margins maintained near 20% or better or expectations for through-the-cycle FCF margins in the low single digits or better.

Liquidity and Debt Structure

As of June 30, 2025, Delta had $6.4 billion in total liquidity, consisting of $3.3 billion in cash and short-term investments and availability under its RCFs. Fitch views Delta's liquidity as adequate for its rating, given our expectations for positive FCF generation over the forecast period, along with the financial flexibility provided by the company's growing unencumbered assets at over $30 billion.

Delta's industrial revenue bonds (IRBs) were issued by the New York Transportation Development Corporation (NYTDC) in connection with the redevelopment of LaGuardia Airport. Delta is the guarantor of the debt, and the bonds are secured by Delta's leasehold interest in the space at LaGuardia.

The IRB ratings are in line with Delta's secured debt as the bonds benefit from a low likelihood of rejection in a distress scenario and the desirability of the asset as LGA is a slot- and space-constrained airport.

Issuer Profile

Delta, one of the world's largest airlines, operates hubs in Atlanta, Boston, Detroit, Los Angeles, Minneapolis-St. Paul, New York-JFK and LaGuardia, Salt Lake City, and Seattle. It belongs to the SkyTeam Alliance, one of three major global airline networks

Fitch Rates Delta Air Lines' Unsecured Bonds 'BBB-'

Fitch Ratings-Toronto-04 June 2025: Fitch Ratings has assigned a rating of 'BBB-' to Delta Air Lines' (Delta) proposed senior unsecured bond offering.

Delta plans to issue up to $2 billion in senior unsecured notes which will rank pari passu with its existing unsecured offerings. Proceeds are intended to refinance the first tranche of the company's payroll support loans and for general corporate purposes.

Delta's 'BBB-' Issuer Default Rating is supported by its solid business profile reflected in its leading position in key markets and strong brand reputation. The ratings consider Delta's conservative financial policies, with management consistently prioritizing manageable debt levels. Fitch expects credit metrics to weaken modestly in 2025 due to a softer demand environment, but to remain within negative rating sensitivities. Fitch expects further balance sheet strengthening over time as the company works toward its 1x gross leverage target.

Key Rating Drivers

Proposed Notes: Delta's proposed notes will represent a general unsecured obligation of the company and proceeds will go toward refinancing $1.6 billion representing the first of three tranches of Payroll Support Program (PSP) government loans the company received during the pandemic. The loans were structured with a fixed rate of 1% per year for the first five years and SOFR plus 2% in years five through 10, with the first tranche switching from fixed to floating in April 2025.

Solid Balance Sheet: Delta ended the first quarter with EBITDAR leverage of 2.6x, down from 2.9x a year prior. Fitch expects leverage to end the year near 3x on modestly contracting margins driven by the current demand environment. Fitch anticipates leverage will trend to the mid-to-low-2x range over the next two years as Delta continues to pay down debt. The company maintains a target of achieving a 1x gross leverage ratio.

Delta has been effective in repaying debt accumulated during the pandemic. The company made nearly $4 billion in debt payments in 2024 and $531 million in the first quarter of 2025, bringing gross debt and lease liabilities to $22.3 billion from nearly $33 billion at YE 2022.

Positive FCF Expected: Fitch expects Delta to generate positive FCF through its forecast period. We anticipate EBITDAR margins dropping modestly to the low double digits in 2025 and potentially 2026, lower than our prior forecast. These assumptions drive FCF margins in the low single digits as a percentage of revenue through the forecast, allowing Delta to address its planned capital spending while decreasing debt. Fitch expects positive FCF to enable a growing base of unencumbered assets, which Delta reports at $30 billion as of YE 2024.

Softer Demand Environment: U.S. airlines are facing a weaker demand outlook that is concentrated in domestic leisure travel. Fitch expects this softness to be most heavily felt in the budget segment, while premium segments will exhibit more resilient demand. International travel has held up well despite reports of fewer inbound visitors to the U.S., as U.S. point of sale accounts for 75%-80% of international revenue for U.S. network carriers. Fitch views legacy carriers such as Delta as better positioned to manage these headwinds versus low-cost competitors.

Strong Business Profile: Delta's investment grade rating is supported by its position as a leading network airline in the consolidated U.S. market. Dominant positions in key hubs and a high level of network utility provide a competitive advantage over smaller airlines while driving growth in Delta's lucrative loyalty program. The company benefits from greater size and scale relative to non-U.S. network carriers. Geographic diversification along with diverse non-airline revenue streams including loyalty, maintenance, repair and overhaul (MRO), and vacations businesses also compare favorably to smaller peers.

Peer Analysis

Delta's 'BBB-' rating is higher than its two major network competitors: United Airlines, Inc. (BB/Positive) and American Airlines, Inc. (B+/Stable). The rating differential is driven in part by Fitch's expectations for Delta to maintain leverage and FCF metrics favorable to its peers through the forecast period. Delta maintains a lower total debt balance than United, but they are similar on a net basis.

However, Delta has lower capital spending commitments than United in the coming years and better prospects for FCF generation along with lower execution risk relative to United's fleet renewal plan. Delta is rated two notches lower than Southwest Airlines Co. (BBB+/Negative). The rating differential is largely driven by Southwest's balance sheet, as it remains in a net cash position. However, Southwest has limited headroom at its current rating due to near-term profitability pressures.

Delta compares well to investment grade peers in Europe. Fitch expects Delta to maintain gross leverage metrics in line with Deutsche Lufthansa AG (BBB-/Stable) and marginally better than Air France KLM (BBB-/Stable). FCF metrics are expected to be better for Delta than its European counterparts. Delta's business profile also benefits from its relative size and scale and foreign exchange exposure compared European peers.

Key Assumptions

-- Capacity growth of around 1% in 2025 and low single digits annually thereafter;

-- Load factors maintained around 84% annually;

-- Modestly decreasing unit revenues in 2025 followed by low-single-digit growth thereafter;

-- Jet fuel prices averaging around $2.40/gallon in 2025, implying Brent crude prices in the mid-to-upper $60/barrel range.

--EBITDAR margins maintained near 20% or better and expectations for through-the-cycle FCF margins in the low single digits or better;

--Maintaining or boosting financial flexibility through increasing unencumbered assets and moving toward a less encumbered capital structure.

Liquidity and Debt Structure

As of March 31, 2025, Delta had $6.8 billion in total liquidity, consisting of $3.7 billion in cash and short-term investments and availability under its revolving credit facilities. Fitch considers Delta's liquidity to be adequate for its rating given our expectations for positive FCF generation over the forecast period, along with the financial flexibility provided by the company's unencumbered assets.

The IRB ratings are in line with Delta's secured debt as the bonds benefit from a low likelihood of rejection in a distress scenario and the desirability of the asset as LGA is a slot- and space-constrained airport.

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-18 July 2024: This is a correction of a release issued July 16, 2024. The rating table in the prior release incorrectly listed the rating for one series of Delta's Industrial Revenue bonds as 'BBB-' rather than 'BBB'.

Fitch Ratings has upgraded Delta Air Lines' (Delta) Long-Term Issuer Default Rating to 'BBB-' from 'BB+'. The Rating Outlook is Stable. Fitch has also upgraded Delta's senior secured debt ratings to 'BBB' from 'BBB-'/'RR1', its LaGuardia industrial revenue bonds to 'BBB' from 'BB+'/'RR4', and its unsecured ratings to 'BBB-' from 'BB+'/'RR4'.

The upgrade reflects that Delta's credit metrics improved considerably after the pandemic. The company has reduced gross debt and operating leases roughly $11 billion over the past three years and expects unencumbered assets to total $30 billion by year end. Fitch expects Delta to sustain EBITDAR leverage in the mid 2x range, fixed charge coverage of greater than 4x, and generate consistent positive FCF, consistent with an investment grade credit profile.

Delta also benefits from a solid business profile reflected in its leading position in key markets and strong brand reputation. The ratings consider Delta's conservative financial policies, with management consistently prioritizing manageable debt levels.

Key Rating Drivers

Leverage Improving: Delta ended the second quarter with EBITDAR leverage below 3x, down from 3.9x a year prior. Fitch expects leverage to trend to the mid to low 2x range over the next year to 18 months, which supports today's upgrade. The company's goal is to reach and maintain a 2.0x-3.0x leverage metric.

Delta made $4.1 billion in debt payments in 2023, and $2.1 billion in the first half of 2024 bringing gross debt and lease liabilities to $27 billion from nearly $33 billion at YE 2022. Although not captured in Fitch's leverage metrics, the company benefits from reduced pension risk. Delta reported that pension obligations were fully funded at YE 2023. This is a material improvement from the period before the pandemic when the plans were underfunded by more than $5 billion.

Positive FCF Expected: Fitch expects Delta to generate positive FCF through its forecast period. We anticipate EBITDAR margins stabilizing in the mid- to upper-teen range, deriving sufficient cash from operations to cover capital expenditures of around 10% of revenue annually. These assumptions drive FCF margins in the low single digits as a percentage of revenue through the forecast, allowing Delta to address its planned capital spending while decreasing debt. Fitch expects positive FCF to enable a growing base of unencumbered assets, which Delta expects to reach $30 billion by YE 2024.

Solid 2024 Results Expected: Delta is well-positioned to generate healthy operating margins and cash flows in 2024. Fitch's model incorporates margin compression in 2024 driven by mostly flat unit revenues and a low-single digit increase in unit costs. Although results are weaker than the prior year, we expect Delta's profitability to remain among the industry's best. Capacity growth is slowing as the company transitions away from a phase of post-pandemic network restoration. We expect to see total capacity growth in the mid-single digits in 2024, down from 16.6% in 2023, with growth mostly focused on Delta's core hubs where it generates its highest margins.

Delta has reported an uptick in corporate travel in the first part of the year, a positive development after corporate demand plateaued in 2023. Demand for premium products also continues to hold up, with consumers increasingly willing to buy up for better experiences. The company reported a 10% increase in premium revenue in Q2. Fitch views the industry's focus on premium as creating some longer-term risk as competition for premium travel increases, though Delta has competitive advantages due to strong positions in its hubs and loyalty base.

Supportive Environment: Fitch anticipates that general travel demand will remain healthy through 2024. This expectation is supported by record numbers of travelers processed through TSA checkpoints in recent weeks along with Delta's recent commentary on forward bookings for the second half of the year. Fitch continues to monitor changes in pressure on consumers, but current trends indicate that air travel remains an area of priority spending. Airline pricing power should remain broadly supported by limits on global seat supply driven by original equipment manufacturer (OEM) production delays and engine maintenance issues.

Manageable Cost Pressures: Fitch expects Delta's non-fuel unit costs to increase in the low single digits in 2024, which we view as manageable. An increase in heavy maintenance checks and higher costs from supply chain issues has created a headwind. Pressures are partly offset by improving aircraft utilization and deliveries of new technology planes that are likely to provide cost tailwinds. Delta has also lapped the bulk of the wage increases driven by updated union contracts, easing some pressure on yoy comparisons.

Strong Business Profile: Delta's investment grade rating is supported by its position as a leading network airline in the consolidated U.S. market. Dominant positions in key hubs and a high level of network utility provide a competitive advantage over smaller airlines while driving growth in Delta's lucrative loyalty program. The company benefits from greater size and scale relative to non-U.S. network carriers. Geographic diversification along with diverse non-airline revenue streams including loyalty, maintenance, repair & overhaul (MRO), and vacations businesses also compare favorably to smaller peers.

Loyalty and Non-Traditional Revenues: Revenues from airline loyalty programs continue to grow in importance to the industry and could be an offsetting factor in potential downturns. Delta generated $6.8 billion in total remuneration from its partnership with American Express in 2023 and maintains a long-term goal of reaching $10 billion annually. Loyalty revenue has proven resilient, rebounding to pre-pandemic levels by YE 2021, well ahead of travel-related revenue. Delta reports that Loyalty, premium products, cargo and other revenues outside of main-cabin ticket sales now comprise 56% of total revenues.

Derivation Summary

Delta's 'BBB-' rating is higher than its two major network competitors, United Airlines, Inc. (BB-/Stable) and American Airlines, Inc. (B+/Stable). The rating differential is driven in part by Fitch's expectations for Delta to maintain leverage and FCF metrics favorable to its peers through the forecast period. Delta maintains a lower total debt balance than United, although they are similar on a net basis.

However, Delta has lower capital spending commitments than United in the coming years and better prospects for FCF generation along with lower execution risk relative to United's fleet renewal plan. Delta is rated two notches lower than Southwest Airlines Co. (BBB+/Stable). The rating differential is largely driven by Southwest's balance sheet, as it remains in a net cash position. However, Southwest has limited headroom at its current rating due to near-term profitability pressures.

Delta compares well to investment grade peers in Europe. Fitch expects Delta to maintain gross leverage metrics in line with Deutsche Lufthansa (BBB-/Stable) and marginally better than Air France/KLM (BBB-/Stable). FCF metrics are expected to be better for Delta than its European counterparts. Delta's business profile also benefits from its relative size and scale and relative foreign exchange exposure compared European peers.

Key Assumptions

Fitch's Key Assumptions Within Our Base Case for the Issuer Include

-- Capacity growth of around 5% in 2024 and low single digits annually thereafter;

-- Continued passenger growth keeping load factors at 83%-84%;

-- Flat to modestly decreasing unit revenues in 2024 and 2025 followed by low single digit growth thereafter;

-- Jet fuel prices averaging around $2.80/gallon in 2024, implying Brent crude prices in the low-$80/barrel range, while crack spreads remain elevated above historical averages;

-- Capital spending in line with the company's public guidance;

-- Fitch's forecast does not include any share repurchases.

RATING SENSITIVITIES

Factors that Could, Individually or Collectively, Lead to Positive Rating Action/Upgrade

--Sustained commitment to conservative financial policies leading adjusted debt/EBITDAR to below 2.5x;

--EBITDAR margins maintained near 20% or better and expectations for through-the-cycle FCF margins in the low single digits or better;

--Maintaining or boosting financial flexibility through increasing unencumbered assets and moving toward a less encumbered capital structure;

Factors that Could, Individually or Collectively, Lead to Negative Rating Action/Downgrade

--Sustained adjusted debt/EBITDAR above 3.3x or EBITDAR/interest + rents falling below 3.5x on a sustained basis;

--FCF margins declining to neutral on a sustained basis;

--A deviation of FCF deployment causing total liquidity to fall below $7 billion absent an offsetting increase in leverageable assets

Liquidity and Debt Structure

Adequate Liquidity: As of June 30, 2024, Delta had $7.2 billion in total liquidity, consisting of $4.2 billion in cash and short-term investments and availability under its revolving credit facilities. Delta has pared down liquidity more quickly than its large network peers in the U.S. Fitch considers Delta's liquidity to be adequate for its rating given our expectations for positive FCF generation over the forecast period, along with the financial flexibility provided by the company's unencumbered assets.

Delta's total adjusted debt declined to $26.7 billion at 2Q24 from $33 billion at YE 2022 and more than $35 billion at its peak during the pandemic. The company made $4.1 billion of debt payments in 2023. Maturities of approximately $3 billion in both 2024 and 2025, are manageable, given Fitch's expectations for the company to generate significant FCF in the coming two years.

LaGuardia Industrial Revenue Bonds (IRBs): Delta's IRBs were issued by the New York Transportation Development Corporation (NYTDC) in connection with the redevelopment on LaGuardia airport. Delta is the guarantor of the debt. The bonds are secured by Delta's leasehold interest in the space at LaGuardia.

Fitch historically rated the bonds in line with Delta's unsecured debt, primarily due to construction risk, as the project is unfinished. Fitch has upgraded the ratings in line with Delta's secured debt as the project is nearing completion in 2024 and construction risk is diminished. The ratings on the bonds benefit from a low likelihood of rejection in a distress scenario and the desirability of the asset as LGA is a slot-constrained and space constrained airport. In addition to upgrading the existing bonds, Fitch has assigned a rating of 'BBB' to the series 2023 bonds which were previously unrated.

Issuer Profile

Delta is one of the largest airlines in the world, operating hubs in Atlanta, Boston, Detroit, Los Angeles, Minneapolis-St. Paul, New York-JFK and LaGuardia, Salt Lake City and Seattle. It is part of the SkyTeam Alliance, one of three major alliance networks of global airlines.

Leverage Improving: Delta ended the year with EBITDAR leverage at roughly 3x, ahead of our prior forecast, and approximately a turn higher than prior to the pandemic. Fitch expects leverage to trend to the mid to low 2x range over the next year to 18 months, which we view as supportive of a ratings upgrade. Delta maintains a publicly stated goal to reach and maintain a 2.0x-3.0x leverage metric. Delta made $4.1 billion in debt principal payments in 2023, bringing gross debt and lease liabilities to $29 billion from nearly $33 billion at YE 2022.

Although not captured in Fitch's leverage metrics, the company also benefits from reduced pension risk. Delta reported that pension obligations were fully funded at YE 2022, marking a material improvement from prior to the pandemic, when the plans were underfunded by more than $5 billion.

Positive FCF Expected: Fitch expects Delta to generate positive FCF through its forecast period. Fitch anticipates EBITDAR margins stabilizing in the mid to upper teen range, driving sufficient cash from operations to cover capex that are likely to total around 10% of revenue annually. These assumptions drive FCF margins in the low single digits as a percentage of revenue through the forecast period, allowing Delta to address its planned capital spending while also bringing down debt. Fitch also expects positive FCF to enable a growing base of unencumbered assets, which Delta currently cites at $26 billion.

Favorable Positioning Heading into 2024: Fitch views Delta as well-positioned to generate healthy operating margins and cash flows in 2024. Headwinds including potential areas of overcapacity and cost pressures are likely to keep profitability in line with or slightly below 2023 levels. While we assume margins remain below historical levels, Fitch's assumptions nevertheless allow for further improvement in Delta's credit metrics.

Capacity growth is slowing as the company transitions away from a phase of post-pandemic network restoration. Fitch expects to see total capacity growth in the mid-single digits in 2024, down from roughly 16.6% in 2023, with growth mostly focused on Delta's core hubs where it generates its highest margins. Premium product offerings are also likely to hold up well as more affluent travelers are less impacted by potentially slower economic growth. The company may also have opportunities to benefit in places where competitors are struggling such as with capacity constraints for Spirit and JetBlue created by engine availability issues and potential merger-related complications.

Constructive Environment: Fitch anticipates that demand for air travel will remain healthy through 2024, evidenced by recent reports of solid booking trends through the fourth quarter and the potential for improvement in business travel. Although we expect slower U.S. economic growth next year, Fitch's Global Economic Outlook no longer anticipates a U.S. recession. Consumer resilience is likely to continue to include prioritizing spend on travel. Airline pricing power should remain broadly supported by limits on global seat supply driven by original equipment manufacturer (OEM) production delays and engine maintenance issues. However, inflationary pressure on consumer spending, overcapacity in certain markets, and the potential for weaker international travel following a resurgence in 2023 present downside risks.

Manageable Cost Pressures: Fitch expects Delta's non-fuel unit costs to increase in the low single digits in 2024 in line with management's guidance, which we view as manageable. The company had previously guided to lower unit costs in 2024, but higher labor and maintenance costs along with slower planned growth will put pressure on unit metrics. Pressures are partly offset by improving aircraft utilization and deliveries of new technology planes that are expected to provide cost tailwinds. Delta has also been operating with additional buffers in place to support operational reliability as it went through its network restoration process. Normalized levels of growth will allow slack to come out of the system. 2023 non-fuel unit costs remain roughly 25% above the comparable 2019 period, driven by inflationary pressures and higher labor costs.

Resilient Loyalty and Non-Traditional Revenues: Revenues from airline loyalty programs continue to grow in importance to the industry, and act as an offsetting factor in potential downturns. Delta generated $6.8 billion in total remuneration from its partnership with American Express in 2023 and maintains a long-term goal of reaching $10 billion annually. Loyalty revenue has proven resilient, rebounding to pre-pandemic levels by YE 2021, well ahead of travel-related revenue. Delta reports that Loyalty, premium products, cargo and other revenues outside of main-cabin ticket sales now comprise 55% of total revenues.

Loyalty revenues face regulatory risks from proposed legislation seeking to increase competition in the credit card industry and reduce credit card fees. Airline loyalty program revenues rely fees generated from card swipes and passage of the Credit Card Competition Act could have a materially adverse impact on these revenue streams. Fitch views the likelihood of the legislation being enacted as low in the current political environment, but this will remain a watch item.

Delta's EETC 2019-1 Ratings:

Class AA Certificate Affirmation: Loan-to-value (LTV) of the class AA certificates stood at 102% under Fitch's 'BBB' level stress, a slight improvement from our prior review but still above 100%. Collateral coverage was initially strong for the transaction but the LTV has risen over time due to the transaction's bullet structure. The pandemic impact on aircraft values and degradation of the 737-900ERs to Tier 2 aircraft under Fitch's assessment have also pushed up LTVs.

The rating of the class AA is derived via a bottom up approach which is typically used for subordinated tranches. Fitch applies a three-notch uplift to the class AA from Delta's 'BB+' IDR, bringing the issue rating to 'BBB+'. The class AA is theoretically eligible for a four-notch uplift, consisting of a high affirmation factor (+2), the presence of a liquidity facility (+1) and strong recovery prospects (+1). However, Fitch caps the rating achieved via the bottom up approach at 'BBB+' for non-investment grade issuers as per Fitch's criteria.

Class A Certificate Affirmation: The rating for the A certificates is also based on Fitch's bottom-up approach as the LTV remains above 100% under Fitch's 'BBB' level stress scenario. The class A certificates are theoretically eligible for up to three notches of uplift from Delta's 'BB+' IDR, including +2 for the high affirmation factor and +1 for the benefit of a liquidity facility. No recovery uplift is applied due to the certificates' weak recovery prospects. The rating is capped at 'BBB' to maintain a rating distinction between the class AA and A certificates.

Fitch considers the affirmation factor for 2019-1's pool of aircraft to be high primarily due to the sheer number of older planes in Delta's fleet that are more likely to be rejected in a distress scenario. The collateral of the 2019-1 transaction consists of young vintage A220-100s, A321-200s, A350-900s and 737-900ERs. Fitch views the aircraft models as largely strategic to Delta's fleet planning; however, this is offset by the small collateral pool relative to Delta's fleet.

Derivation Summary

Delta's 'BB+' rating remains higher than its two major network competitors, United Airlines, Inc. (B+/Stable) and American Airlines, Inc. (B+/Stable). The rating differential is driven in part by Fitch's expectations for Delta to maintain leverage metrics favorable to its peers through the forecast period, along with its strong margin and FCF history relative to its major competitors. Delta maintains a lower total debt balance than United, though on a net basis, the two are similar.

However, Delta has lower capital spending commitments than United in the coming years and better prospects for FCF generation along with lower execution risk relative to United's fleet renewal plan. Delta is rated three notches lower than Southwest Airlines Co. (BBB+/Stable). The rating differential is largely driven by Southwest's balance sheet, as it remains in a net cash position.

Delta compares well to investment grade peers in Europe. Fitch expects Delta to maintain gross leverage metrics in line with Deutsche Lufthansa (BBB-/Stable) and marginally better than Air France/KLM (BBB-/Stable). FCF metrics are expected to be better for Delta than its European counterparts. Delta's business profile also benefits from its relative size and scale and relative foreign exchange exposure compared European peers.

Transaction Summary

The transaction is backed by payments to be made to SMIP under a license agreement signed with Delta for the use of the SkyMiles Intellectual Property (IP) assets and backed by co-brand agreements with third parties for the purchase of SkyMiles. SMIP, as owner and licensor of the IP, licenses the IP to SMIF, which then sublicenses the IP to Delta. Through the license agreements, SMIP grants a worldwide license to Delta and its subsidiaries to use the IP to operate the loyalty program, SkyMiles. The licensees then pay a monthly license fee equivalent to all cash collections generated by the sale of miles to Delta as governed through an intercompany agreement. Additionally, the third-party agreements will be assigned to SMIP, and payment for the purchase of SkyMiles from third parties will be remitted directly to a collection account held at JP Morgan Chase Bank, N.A. in the name of SMIP. These agreements include the co-brand agreement and membership reward agreement with American Express, the largest third-party partner.

The senior secured financing is guaranteed, on a joint and several basis, by certain subsidiaries under Delta. Additionally, the issuers grant additional security to the lenders/bondholders, including a first priority perfected security interest in the cashflows from the SkyMiles program, a pledge of all rights under contracts/agreements related to the SkyMiles program, a pledge of the transaction accounts (including the collection, payment and reserve accounts) and a pledge over the equity interests in certain subsidiaries of Delta.

Fitch's rating reflects timely payment of interest and timely payment of scheduled principal when due by the legal final maturity date.

KEY RATING DRIVERS

Credit Quality of Delta: Cashflows backing the transaction will primarily come from payment obligations from Delta under the licensing agreement related to intellectual properties owned by the IP SPV/SMIP. Therefore, the Issuer Default Rating (IDR) of Delta acts as the starting point for the analysis. Delta is rated 'BB+' with a Negative Rating Outlook. For more information, please reference the Impact on Corporate IDR section below.

Performance Risk and GCA Score: Timely payment on the notes/loan depends on the ongoing performance of the licensee, Delta. Delta's going concern assessment (GCA) score of '2' acts as a cap for the transaction rating. The GCA score provides an indication of the likelihood that Delta continues to operate in the event of default and chapter 11 bankruptcy. The GCA score of '2' could allow for up to a three- to four-notch rating differential between Delta's IDR and the issuance's default rating.

Strategic Nature of Assets (Likelihood of Affirmation of License Agreement): The affirmation factor, which measures the likelihood that Delta would view this obligation as strategic and would affirm or continue to make the licensing fee in the event of a Chapter 11 bankruptcy, is considered high by Fitch. The strategic importance of the IP assets to Delta's operations coupled with the structural incentives supports this assessment. The assessment of high allows the transaction to obtain a three-notch uplift from Delta's current 'BB+'/Negative IDR.

The issuance of $9.0 billion is estimated to be greater than 20% of Delta's total consolidated liabilities at closing. Fitch considers this ratio to be relatively high and believes it acts as a constraining factor related to the rating differential between the transaction rating and the airline's IDR, according to Fitch's Future Flow Securitization Rating Criteria. Furthermore, the annual debt service payments are moderately sized obligations relative to the airline's other annual obligations. The maximum allowable uplift by the affirmation factor is three notches; therefore, the current transaction rating can support a one-notch downgrade of the airline's IDR.

Coronavirus Risk Included in Affirmation Factor: Fitch has made assumptions about the spread of the coronavirus and the economic impact of the related containment measures. The main consideration of this transaction is the time to return to, or near, pre-crisis levels. In particular, Fitch has designed a base case scenario in which a visible recovery continues by YE20 and overall through the course of 2021 and 2022, and a downside scenario, characterized by a more severe and prolonged period of stress. Both of these scenarios will mainly affect the affirmation factor, as the utility from a loyalty program may decline if global travel remains sluggish indefinitely. Fitch estimates cashflow to be more than sufficient to meet debt service obligations in both the baseline and downside scenarios it has tested.

Asset Isolation and Legal Structure: Fitch assesses the legal protections and structural protections incorporated into the transaction. In addition to having the IP assets legally conveyed, bondholders have a first perfected security interest in the contractual obligations due from American Express and Delta. The legal structure incentivizes Delta to continue to make payments on the license. Creditors would also benefit from other structural features, including other guarantees, potential liquidated damages and a three-month interest liquidity reserve.

Delta's Corporate Rating: The primary rationale for maintaining Delta's rating at 'BB+'/Negative is the financial flexibility that the company maintains if it undertakes this transaction. (SMIP) and Delta Air Lines (Delta). The rating considers a maximum program capacity of $9.0 billion issued through a term loan and two bond tranches. SMIP is a special purpose vehicle (SPV) incorporated under the laws of the Cayman Islands for the purpose of this transaction. SMIP is an indirect wholly owned subsidiary of Delta Air Lines. The proceeds of the issuance will be lent to Delta through an intercompany loan for general corporate purposes. Transaction Summary

The transaction is backed by payments to be made to SMIP under a license agreement signed with Delta for the use of the SkyMiles Intellectual Property (IP) assets and backed by co-brand agreements with third parties for the purchase of SkyMiles. SMIP, as owner and licensor of the IP, licenses the IP to SMIF which then sublicenses the IP to Delta. Through the license agreements, SMIP grants a worldwide license to Delta and its subsidiaries to use the IP to operate the loyalty program, SkyMiles. The licensees then pay a monthly license fee equivalent to all the cash collections generated by sale of miles to Delta as governed through an Intercompany Agreement. Additionally, the third-party agreements will be assigned to SMIP and payment for the purchase of SkyMiles from third parties will be remitted directly to a collection account held at JP Morgan Chase Bank, N.A. in the name of SMIP. These agreements include the co-brand agreement and membership reward agreement with American Express, the largest third-party partner.

The senior secured financing will be guaranteed, on a joint and several basis by certain subsidiaries under Delta. Additionally, the issuers grant additional security to the lenders/bondholders, including a first priority perfected security interest in the cash flows from the SkyMiles program, a pledge of all rights under contracts/agreements related to the SkyMiles program, a pledge of the transaction accounts (including the collection, payment and reserve accounts) and a pledge over the equity interests in certain subsidiaries of Delta.

Fitch's rating reflects timely payment of interest and timely payment of scheduled principal when due by the legal final maturity date.

For more information, please reference the Impact on Corporate IDR section below.

Performance Risk and GCA Score: Timely payment on the notes/loan depends on the ongoing performance of the licensee, Delta. The issuance considering a maximum capacity of $9.0 billion is estimated to be greater than 20% of Delta's total consolidated liabilities at closing. The main consideration of this transaction is the time to return to, or near, precrisis levels. Both these scenarios will mainly affect the affirmation factor, as the utility from a loyalty program may decline if global travel remains sluggish indefinitely. Fitch estimates cash flow to be more than sufficient to meet debt service obligations in both the baseline and downside scenario it has tested.

Asset Isolation and Legal Structure: Fitch assesses the legal protections and structural protections incorporated in the transaction. In addition to having the IP assets legally conveyed, bondholders have a first perfected security interest in the contractual obligations due from American Express and Delta. The legal structure incentivizes Delta to continue to make payments on the license. Creditors would also benefit from other structural features including other guarantees, potential liquidated damages, and a three-month interest liquidity reserve.

Fitch Rates Delta Guaranteed Revenue Bonds 'BB+'/'RR4'

Fitch Rating / Non Rating Action Commentary

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-25 August 2020: Fitch Ratings has assigned a rating of 'BB+'/'RR4' to a series of special facility revenue bonds totalling $1.3 billion to be guaranteed by Delta Air Lines, Inc. The bonds will be issued by the New York Transportation Development Corporation, and the proceeds of the bonds will be loaned to Delta to fund a portion of the total costs of the ongoing demolition of terminals C and D at New York's LaGuardia airport and the design and construction of new terminal facilities to be leased by Delta under a lease with the Port Authority of New York and New Jersey expiring in 2050. The guarantee will be secured by leasehold mortgages in Delta's leasehold interest in the terminal. The new bonds rank equal in right of payment to the series 2018 revenue bonds that were issued under the same master indenture. Fitch also rates the 2018 bonds at 'BB+'/'RR4'.

Key Rating Drivers

The 'BB+'/'RR4' rating on the notes is in-line with Delta's corporate Issuer Default Rating. Although Fitch views the revenue bonds as having a favorable position compared with Delta's unsecured issuances, the value of the collateral is difficult to ascertain, particularly during the construction phase of this project, and Fitch does not consider the benefit of the collateral to be sufficient to warrant a rating above its unsecured rating. If Delta were to experience financial difficulty while construction was on-going, a replacement lessee would need to find funding for the remainder of the project, making the benefit of the security to investors less certain than it is for other debt issuances backed by hard assets like aircraft, engines or spare parts.

Nevertheless, Fitch believes that revenue bondholders benefit from the strategic importance of Delta's position at LaGuardia. Delta is the largest single carrier at LaGuardia accounting for roughly 25% of passenger traffic according to Bureau of Transportation Statistics data. In addition, the guarantee from Delta would allow bond holders to file a general unsecured claim in a potential bankruptcy scenario for any amount owed under the bonds that is over the determined value of Delta's leasehold interest in the premises.

Delta Air Lines Credit Profile: Fitch downgraded Delta to 'BB+' from 'BBB-' in April of this year due to sharply lower demand stemming from the Coronavirus pandemic. Delta's ratings continue to have a Negative Outlook.

Substantial New Debt: Delta has materially increased its debt balance since the onset of coronavirus to shore up liquidity. Delta's cash balance is now well over pre-coronavirus levels, standing at over $15 billion at the end of the second quarter, such that the increase in net debt has been modest to date. Delta's total debt balance increased by approximately $13.7 billion from YE 2019 to June 30 2020, but net debt increased by only $4.8 billion, equivalent to about a half turn of pre-coronavirus EBITDAR. Fitch typically focuses on gross leverage for airlines, but Delta's current liquidity balance makes net cash relevant in this situation. The current increase in net debt is manageable, and Delta has substantial capacity to use cash to de-lever should cash flows improve. If cash burn remains above Fitch's expectations, failing to reach break-even by early in 2021, the ratings could be pressured further. Fitch expects gross and net leverage to remain elevated for the 'BB+' rating at least through YE 2021 due to a slower than expected rebound in passenger traffic.

Through the first six months of the year the company raised funds through a $3 billion 364-day term loan secured by aircraft, fully drawing down $3 billion in revolver capacity, raised $5 billion through a combination of secured term loans and notes, issued $1.3 billion in unsecured notes and completed $1.243 million in EETC debt. Delta also entered into $2.8 billion in sale-leaseback transactions.

Sufficient Liquidity: Fitch believes that the company has sufficient liquidity to manage through the current downturn, assuming that traffic continues to slowly improve. Delta is publicly targeting cash-burn breakeven by the end of this year Fitch's forecast is more conservative, showing a daily cash burn of approximately $20 million for the fourth quarter. Even if cash burn was sustained at this rate, the company maintains a significant liquidity cushion. Delta also maintains a sizable balance of unencumbered assets and the potential to raise funds secured by its loyalty program that could provide substantial additional liquidity.

Air Traffic Recovery Lagging Initial Expectations:

Ongoing high levels of reported coronavirus cases and travel restrictions are leading to a prolonged recovery in air traffic compared to Fitch's prior forecast. The industry saw a material rebound in demand off of April trough levels, but the pace of recovery slowed as reported cases began to rise again in June. There is a high degree of uncertainty in forecasts for travel demand in 2021 and beyond. The pace of recovery is likely to be dependent on how effective efforts are at controlling coronavirus cases through the fall and winter along with the timing of potential treatments or vaccines. In any scenario, 2021 traffic is unlikely to recover as quickly as Fitch previously expected placing further pressure on airline credit ratings.

Derivation Summary

This transaction is similar to American's JFK revenue bonds. Fitch Rates American's revenue bonds in line with its other secured debt, two notches above the corporate issuer default rating. In this case the 'BB+' rating is in-line with Delta's corporate rating and one notch below its other secured debt. The decision to align the note rating with the corporate rating reflects Fitch's view that the security for this transaction (Delta's leasehold interest in its space at LaGuardia) is not as beneficial to debtholders compared with other debt that is secured by hard assets like aircraft and spare parts and/or international route rights, particularly due to the risks involved with demolition and construction of the facilities.

Key Assumptions

Key Assumptions in Fitch's rating case include a steep drop in demand through 2020, with full recovery not occurring until 2023 or later. As of Fitch's last full review, the base forecast included revenues down by roughly 90% through the second quarter of the year, down as much as 60%-65% in the third quarter, and down at least 30% in the fourth quarter for domestic focused carriers and closer to 50% for carriers with more international exposure. These assumptions are under review and could be revised lower in light of slower than expected recovery in passenger traffic. Delta's actual liquidity balance at second-quarter 2020 is significantly better than previously expected ($15 billion versus an expectation of roughly $8.5 billion) as Fitch's prior forecasts did not incorporate the new debt issuances completed in the second quarter. Higher liquidity partly offsets Fitch's expectations for a slower recovery.

RATING SENSITIVITIES

Factors that could, individually or collectively, lead to positive rating action/upgrade:

-- FCF margins moving back to the low single digits;

-- EBIT margins in the midteens;

-- Sustained commitment to conservative financial policies;

-- Adjusted debt/EBITDAR sustained around or below 2.5x.

Factors that could, individually or collectively, lead to negative rating action/downgrade:

-- An extended drop in travel due to the coronavirus pandemic;

-- Sustained adjusted debt/EBITDAR above 3.3x;

-- FCF margins declining to neutral on a sustained basis;

-- FFO fixed-charge coverage falling below 3.5x on a sustained basis;

-- Change in management strategy that favors shareholder returns at the expense of a healthy balance sheet.
