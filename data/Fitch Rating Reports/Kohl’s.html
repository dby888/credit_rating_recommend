Fitch Rates Kohl's Proposed Secured Notes 'BB+'/'RR2'

Fitch Rating / Non Rating Action Commentary

Fitch Ratings-New York-13 May 2025: Fitch Ratings has assigned a 'BB+' rating with a Recovery Rating of 'RR2' to Kohl's Corporation's proposed $360 million secured notes due 2030. Proceeds will be used to repay a similar amount of unsecured notes due July 2025. Fitch has also affirmed Kohl's existing ratings, including its Long-Term Issuer Default Rating (IDR) at 'BB-'. The Rating Outlook remains Negative.

Kohl's rating and Outlook reflects its ongoing operational challenges. The company is adjusting its operating strategy, but its ability to stabilize market share, particularly in apparel, is uncertain. The rating recognizes Kohl's tools in executing its turnaround, including a reasonable asset base and ability to invest $400 million in capital expenditures.

Fitch projects 2025 EBITDA could decline 15% to 20% toward $800 million, with mid-single-digit revenue declines, yielding low-4x EBITDAR leverage. Fitch could revise the Outlook with evidence that Kohl's can improve its topline trajectory and stabilize EBITDA around $800 million.

Key Rating Drivers

Weakening Performance: Kohl's results have been weak, with 2024 revenue and EBITDA declining 20% and 50% from 2019 levels, respectively, despite growth in newer categories like beauty through Sephora. The company's market share declines in core categories like apparel appear worse than those of peers in the challenged department store industry, suggesting company-specific execution challenges. Recent events, including weak 2025 guidance implying another year of revenue and EBITDA declines, CEO changes, the announced closure of 27 stores, or about 2% of the base, and a 75% dividend reduction, underscore Kohl's near-term challenges.

The company's near-term focus is to sharpen the merchandise assortment, improve the company's value messaging, and optimize its omnichannel model to provide a frictionless shopping experience. Fitch believes these are reasonable focus areas but given the company's recent history, magnified by secular challenges in the department store industry and near-term headwinds for discretionary goods, the timing and level of operating stabilization remains uncertain.

Operating Challenges: Kohl's weak operating trajectory and evolving strategies to stabilize performance highlight the difficulties in effectively executing department store operations. Department stores will continue to face longer-term secular obstructions, including consumers spending less time in malls (which has less impact on Kohl's, given its off-mall positioning), changes in apparel buying behavior, and increasing competition from newer channels, including value-oriented and online players. Nearer-term challenges also include somewhat waning consumer sentiment and potential impacts from tariffs on consumer inflation and operational complexity.

Fitch historically viewed Kohl's as able to navigate industry challenges and defend or even gain share. The company has a reasonable store base with limited indoor mall exposure, good omnichannel capabilities, and cash flow generation to materially invest in topline strategies, along with productive relationships with merchandise vendors and key partners. Following recent performance, Fitch believes the company's medium-term turnaround prospects are limited, with revenue and EBITDA stabilizing around $15 billion and $800 million, respectively, as a best-case scenario.

Near-Term Volatility: Fitch expects the U.S. retail sector to face near-term challenges, including declines in consumer sentiment, business disruption and rising costs related to evolving U.S. tariff policies. Discretionary categories could see revenue down as much as mid-single digits with outsized EBITDA declines given tariff-related cost pressure. Kohl's could see EBITDA decline up to 20% in 2025, although the company should generate positive FCF and has reasonable liquidity to withstand near-term volatility assuming it successfully completes its current notes offering.

Leverage Around Low-4x: Kohl's EBITDAR leverage could be about 4.5x in 2025 and in the low-4x range beginning in 2026, with EBITDAR fixed charge coverage in the high-1x range. The company would need to demonstrate stabilizing operations to support its existing rating, with leverage remaining below 4.5x. Pro forma for this issuance, the company's debt structure would include $1.2 billion in unsecured notes and the new secured notes. The secured notes would be Kohl's next maturity in 2030.

Positive Cash Flow Generation: Kohl's strong cash generation is an asset, allowing the company some flexibility to invest in topline initiatives. The company plans to invest about $400 million, or mid-2% of revenue, in 2025 capex. These capex investments could support the company's omnichannel model, including enhancements to ecommerce and supply chain infrastructure, as well as store refreshes. Following the company's 75% dividend cut, Fitch projects FCF after dividends, with non-cash finance lease amortization added back, to be around $250 million in 2025.

Peer Analysis

Other Fitch-rated U.S. department store peers include national competitors Macy's, Inc. (BBB-/Stable) and Nordstrom, Inc. (BB/Stable), as well as regional player Dillard's, Inc. (BBB-/Positive). Each is contending with the industry's secular headwinds and is continuously refining strategies to defend market share. Their initiatives include investments in omnichannel models, reshaping portfolios to reduce exposure to weaker indoor malls, and efforts to strengthen merchandise assortments and service levels.

These initiatives have had varying levels of success in recent years, with Dillard's showing the best operating trajectory. Nordstrom and Kohl's have produced the weakest results in terms of topline and profitability. Macy's results have been somewhat mixed, with topline declines mitigated by good inventory and expense management with limited EBITDA contraction.

Structurally, the three national players should be best positioned to accelerate investment and transformation efforts, given their greater relative scale and cash flow generation. In particular, Kohl's should benefit from its off-mall real estate positioning, while Nordstrom should benefit from its exposure to the off-price subsector and the higher-end merchandise positioning of its full price locations. However, neither has demonstrated the ability to capitalize on these fundamental advantages, which Fitch believes is likely due to execution challenges.

Dillard's has modest leverage due to its limited debt levels. Fitch projects Macy's EBITDAR leverage to trend in the high-2x range, while Nordstrom's EBITDAR leverage could trend near the 3x range. Fitch expects Kohl's leverage to trend in the low-4x range, assuming it can stabilize EBITDA around $800 million.

Key Assumptions

Fitch projects Kohl's 2025 revenue to decline by about 7%, reaching $15 billion from $16.2 billion in 2024, and well below the $20 billion recorded in 2019. Declines in 2025 are forecast to be driven by comparable store sales decreases, particularly in the apparel and accessories categories. Revenue could stabilize around $15 billion, assuming the company can continue to grow newer categories like beauty while implementing strategies to stabilize the declining apparel business. Fitch projects Kohl's comparable store sales to remain flattish to slightly negative through the medium term.

EBITDA, which was about $1 billion in 2024 compared to $2 billion in 2019, could moderate to about $800 million in 2025, given revenue declines and some topline investments. EBITDA could stabilize around $800 million if revenue stabilizes around $15 billion, with margins trending in the mid-5% range, compared to about 10% in 2019.

Annual FCF (after dividends and adding back non-cash finance lease amortization) is projected to trend around $250 million over the medium term, given our EBITDA assumptions, neutral working capital, about $400 million of annual capex, and annual dividends in the $55 million range.

FCF could be directed toward debt reduction, share buybacks or additional business reinvestment. The company ended 2024 with $290 million outstanding on its ABL.

EBITDAR leverage (capitalizing leases at 8x), which was 3.9x in 2024 compared to the low-2x range between 2018 and 2021 (excluding pandemic-affected 2020) due to lower EBITDA, could sustain below 4.5x over the medium term, given Fitch's EBITDA projections.

Achieving the above projections, including stabilizing revenue and EBITDA, which supports Fitch's confidence in the company's ability to defend its market share over time, could result in a revision of Kohl's Rating Outlook.

Kohl's capital structure primarily consists of fixed-rate debt. Pricing for the company's ABL is based on the secured overnight financing rate (SOFR), with rates forecast to be in the 4% to 5% range over the medium term, given the higher interest rate environment.

Recovery Analysis

Fitch does not employ a waterfall recovery analysis for issuers assigned ratings in the 'BB' category. As a rating moves further up the speculative grade continuum, the notching becomes more compressed. We rate Kohl's ABL 'BB+'/'RR1', notched up two ratings from the IDR given its security package. The company's proposed secured notes are also notched up two ratings from the IDR to 'BB+'/'RR2', while its unsecured notes are rated 'BB-'/'RR4'.

RATING SENSITIVITIES

Factors that Could, Individually or Collectively, Lead to Negative Rating Action/Downgrade

--A downgrade could result from EBITDAR leverage sustained above 4.5x, due to either weaker-than-expected operating performance or financial policy decisions;

--EBITDAR fixed charge coverage sustained near 1.5x would also be a rating concern.

Factors that Could, Individually or Collectively, Lead to Positive Rating Action/Upgrade

--An Outlook revision could result from evidence of revenue and EBITDA stabilization, yielding EBITDAR leverage sustained below 4.5x and EBITDAR fixed charge coverage close to 2x;

--An upgrade to 'BB' would result from a combination of better-than-expected operating performance and debt reduction, which drives EBITDAR leverage below 4.0x and EBITDAR fixed charge coverage toward 2.5x.

Liquidity and Debt Structure

As of Feb. 1, 2025, Kohl's had $134 million in cash and $290 million borrowed on its $1.5 billion asset-based lending (ABL) credit facility, which matures in January 2028. The ABL is governed by a borrowing base of inventory and receivables.

As of Feb. 1, 2025, Kohl's debt included $1.5 billion of unsecured notes and $290 million in borrowings on its ABL facility. The company plans to issue $360 million in new five-year notes to repay a similar amount of notes due July 2025. The proposed debt, to be issued by Kohl's, will be secured by seven distribution centers and four ecommerce fulfillment centers, which together are valued at about $750 million. Fitch projects the notes will have a coupon near 10%. Post issuance, the new debt will be Kohl's next notes maturity.

Fitch applies the 8x rent methodology to calculate Kohl's lease-adjusted debt. The company has reset the useful life of many of its leased properties following capital investments to support the Sephora rollout, which has caused Kohl's implied lease multiple to increase from the 8x-9x range to over 10x. Given the accounting reset, Fitch views its historical 8x methodology as a more appropriate measurement of Kohl's lease obligations.

Issuer Profile

Kohl's is the second largest department store operator in the U.S. with approximately $16 billion in revenue across its digital presence and around 1,170 stores, largely in off-mall centers.

From a rating perspective, the company's low leverage is balanced by the company's relatively smaller scale and regional positioning compared with peers.

--Fitch projects Kohl's 2024 revenue could decline modestly toward $17 billion from $17.5 billion in 2023 and well below the $20 billion recorded in pre-pandemic 2019. Declines in 2024 are forecast to be driven by declines in credit income following regulatory changes, and the loss of a 53rd week from 2023. Fitch Ratings-New York-08 March 2023: Fitch Ratings has affirmed Kohl's Corporation's Long-Term Issuer Default Rating (IDR) at 'BBB-'. The Rating Outlook has been Revised to Negative from Stable.

The Outlook revision reflects Fitch's reduced confidence in Kohl's execution abilities following a challenging 2022 characterized by softening spending on discretionary categories like apparel and home, and management mis-execution, particularly around excess inventory, resulting in a significant deterioration in EBITDA.

While Fitch expects Kohl's operating results to improve beginning 2023 as the company reins in inventory purchases and operating expenses, the timing and extent of the rebound is uncertain. A downgrade to 'BB+' would result from Kohl's inability to improve adjusted leverage to below 3x from approximately 4.6x in 2022.

Kohl's ratings continue to reflect its reasonable position in the challenging U.S. department store space. The company benefits from its real estate positioning away from traditional regional malls, mix of national and exclusive/owned brands, and value price focus.

Key Rating Drivers

Operating Volatility: Following a strong 2021, Kohl's saw 2022 EBITDA decline around 65% to $820 million, compared with $2.0 billion in pre-pandemic 2019. Kohl's planned 2022 optimistically, which left the company with excess inventory as topline moderated below expectations. Revenue declined 7% to $18.1 billion in 2022, but outsized EBITDA declines resulted from aggressive promotions designed to clear inventory.

Fitch expects a material EBITDA rebound is likely beginning 2023 despite ongoing topline challenges related to softening consumer spending on discretionary goods given lower reduced inventory purchases and efforts to reduce selling expenses. However, Kohl's would need to nearly double EBITDA to the $1.5 billion range alongside planned debt reduction to drive adjusted leverage below 3.0x, supporting Kohl's 'BBB-' rating.

Kohl's weak performance and executive management turnover in 2022 raise questions about the company's ability to execute on the turnaround, particularly in the department store segment, which has ongoing secular challenges. Kohl's credit profile has historically reflected the company's ability to navigate industry challenges and defend or gain share over time. Evidence of improving execution, including material EBITDA growth from current levels and progress on initiatives to sustain longer-term top line growth, will be critical to improve Fitch's confidence in Kohl's ability to maintain market share.

Business Model Evolution: In October 2020 and March 2022, Kohl's outlined a strategy to drive low-single digit annual revenue growth through initiatives like the Sephora rollout (targeting $2 billion in revenue), continued investments in digital/omnichannel, growth in athleisure/activewear, new brand introductions, enhancements to the Kohl's loyalty program, and modest store growth through Kohl's new small store format. The company also set a long-term target of attaining a 7% to 8% operating margin, in line with 2016-2018 performance (mid-5% in 2019) through inventory and expense management, pricing optimization and supply chain efficiencies.

If executed successfully, Fitch expects Kohl's topline efforts could support market share defensibility with around 1% to 2% annual revenue growth longer term although the ability to maintain 7% to 8% margins appears to be more aspirational over the next 24 to 36 months. Fitch expects Kohl's could generate low-5% EBIT margin by 2025, with EBITDA margins close to 9%, modestly under the 10% recorded in 2019 given Fitch's expectation that department store players will need to intensify selling investments over time to support revenue stabilization. These projections would enable the company to generate EBITDA around $1.5 billion which, alongside debt reduction, would reduce leverage below 3.0x.

Challenged Department Store Segment: Fitch recognizes that department stores will continue to face longer-term secular headwinds, including time spent in malls (less impactful to Kohl's given its off-mall real estate positioning), apparel buying behavior (including ongoing trends toward casualization) and encroaching competition from newer channels, including value-oriented and online players.

Fitch expects that financially and operationally stronger players can at least maintain share in a difficult space. Stronger competitors' ability to stabilize share is further supported by some competitive fallout during the pandemic and relative inability of weaker players to invest in initiatives to reverse weak trajectories. Kohl's continued ability to partner with Sephora and expand its beauty presence, invest in omnichannel capabilities, and attract unique and exclusive brands should allow the company to maintain customer loyalty and traffic longer term, notwithstanding its current operating challenges.

Leverage Expected Below 3.0x: The company ended 2022 with adjusted leverage of approximately 4.6x, meaningfully above the low-2.0x range reported from 2018 through 2021 (excluding pandemic-affected 2020) given EBITDA challenges. The company is committed to maintaining Fitch-defined adjusted leverage at or below mid-2x and has indicated plans to repay $275 million of 2023 unsecured notes maturities using cash on hand, which would leave approximately $1.65 billion of debt outstanding at the end of 2023. The company has approximately $466 million of unsecured notes maturing in 2025 which could provide further deleveraging opportunities.

Strong Liquidity and FCF: Kohl's FCF has historically been strong, including around $300 million in 2019, although was an outflow of approximately $840 million in 2022 given weak EBITDA and increased working capital. Fitch expects FCF around low-$200 million in 2023 and mid-$300 million in 2024/2025 assuming an EBITDA rebound and neutral working capital. This would be after a healthy $650 million in annual capex spend and dividends in the low-$200 million range, or $2 per share.

Kohl's good cash flow generation is not only a competitive advantage but also supports its ability to manage through economic cycles or extreme challenges such as the pandemic, with some opportunity to improve competitive positioning particularly if weaker players are forced to retrench.

Derivation Summary

Kohl's 'BBB-' IDR reflects the company's reasonable position in the challenging U.S. department store space. The company benefits from its real estate positioning away from traditional regional malls, mix of national and exclusive/owned brands, and value price focus. The company's strong cash flow allows Kohl's to invest in revenue drivers such as its omnichannel model and brand partnerships, which should support market share defensibility over time.

The Outlook revision to Negative from Stable reflects Fitch's reduced confidence in Kohl's execution abilities rebound following a challenging 2022 characterized by softening spending on discretionary categories like apparel and home and management mis-execution, particularly around excess inventory and consequentially high promotional activity, pressuring margins. While Fitch expects Kohl's operating results to improve beginning 2023 as the company reins in inventory purchases and operating expenses, the timing and extent of the rebound is uncertain. Stabilization of the Outlook would result from an improvement to improve adjusted leverage to below 3x from approximately 4.6x in 2022.

Fitch Affirms Kohl's LT IDR at 'BBB-'; Outlook Stable

Fitch Ratings-New York-18 March 2022: Fitch Ratings has affirmed Kohl's Corporation's Long-Term Issuer Default Rating at 'BBB-'. The Rating Outlook is Stable.

Kohl's ratings reflect the company's scale (close to $20 billion of revenue) and good positioning in the challenging U.S. department store space. The company benefits from its real estate positioning away from traditional regional malls, mix of national and exclusive/owned brands and value price focus. The company's strong cash flows allow Kohl's to invest in revenue drivers such as its omnichannel model and brand partnerships, which should support market share defensibility over time. The ratings also incorporate good balance sheet management, including maintaining adjusted leverage at or below 2.5x (excluding 2020).

Fitch acknowledges that Kohl's is working with investment bankers and a sale of the company is a potential outcome given the presence of 20 possible buyers per the company. Should a sale be consummated, Kohl's rating trajectory would depend on the pro forma capital structure and longer-term financial policy.

Key Rating Drivers

EBITDA Rebound: Kohl's good 2021 results, including revenue nearly returning to 2019 levels just under $20 billion and approximately 17% EBITDA growth (to $2.4 billion) from 2019 levels on cost control efforts and reduced promotional activity, support Fitch's expectation of EBITDA settling close to pre-pandemic levels of $2 billion. The rebound in 2021 revenue following a 20% decline in 2020 is likely the result of both improvements in the selling environment (chiefly pandemic-challenged categories like apparel and cosmetics) and successful implementation of topline initiatives.

Kohl's revenue could grow modestly beginning 2022, given merchandising efforts, omnichannel investments, modest store growth and an expanding presence in the beauty category via its Sephora (owned by LVMH Möet Hennessy Louis Vuitton) partnership. EBITDA could return to pre-pandemic levels in the $2 billion range, assuming EBITDA margin in the 9.5% to 10% range (versus 12% in 2021 and 10% in 2019) given some reversal in the currently moderate promotional environment and increased selling investments to support its omnichannel initiatives.

Progress on Topline Initiatives: Kohl's 2021 performance demonstrates some progress against secular challenges facing department stores, including share shifts toward online and value channels. The company's positive topline trajectory, 32% digital sales penetration in 2021, and growth in customer count indicate some success in implementing various topline initiatives. Kohl's ability to stabilize share is further supported by some competitive fallout during the pandemic and the company's ability to invest in omnichannel and other customer-facing features, which less cash flow generative competitors cannot match.

Fitch recognizes that department stores will continue to face longer-term secular headwinds, including time spent in malls (less impactful to Kohl's given its off-mall real estate positioning), apparel buying behavior (including ongoing trends toward casualization) and encroaching competition from value-oriented and online channels. However, Kohl's recent actions and results support Fitch's view that the financially and operationally stronger players, such as Kohl's can at least maintain share in a difficult space.

Business Model Evolution: In October 2020 and March 2022 analyst days, Kohl's outlined a strategy to drive low-single digit annual revenue growth through initiatives like the Sephora rollout (targeting $2 billion in revenue), continued investments in digital/omnichannel (targeting an additional $2 billion in digital revenue from the current $6 billion), growth in athleisure/activewear, new brand introductions, enhancements to the Kohl's loyalty program and modest store growth through Kohl's new small store format. The company also targets 7% to 8% operating margins, in line with 2016-2018 performance (mid-5% in 2019) through inventory and expense management, pricing optimization, and supply chain efficiencies.

Fitch expects Kohl's topline efforts to support market share defensibility and its projecting around 1% to 2% revenue growth beginning 2022. EBITDA margins are forecast in the 9.5% to 10% range (with low-6% EBIT margins), modestly under the 10% recorded in 2019 given Fitch's expectation that department store players like Kohl's will need to intensify selling investments over time to support revenue stabilization. Achievement of Kohl's stated margin goals would yield meaningful upside to Fitch's base case forecast.

Demonstrated Financial Conservatism: Kohl's good credit profile is underscored by its actions and financial flexibility in 2020. At the onset of the pandemic, the company reduced to operating expenses, capex, and inventory and suspended its dividend (around $400 million annually) and share buybacks. Following better than expected operating performance, the company resumed dividends in late 2020 and share repurchase in 2021 although remains committed to maintaining Fitch-defined adjusted leverage at or below mid-2x.

Strong Liquidity and FCF: The company generated approximately $1.5 billion in FCF in 2021 (after approximately $150 million of dividends) on strong EBITDA. FCF in 2022 is forecast in the $150 million to $200 million range given projections of around $400 million in EBITDA declines, working capital usage following material benefits in recent years, higher capex and dividends. FCF could improve toward $400 million in 2023 on neutral working capital.

Kohl's good cash flow generation is not only a competitive advantage but also supports its ability to manage through economic cycles or extreme challenges such as the pandemic, with some opportunity to improve competitive positioning particularly if weaker players are forced to retrench.

Derivation Summary

Kohl's 'BBB-'/Stable ratings reflect the company's scale (close to $20 billion of revenue) and good positioning in the challenging U.S. department store space. The company benefits from its real estate positioning away from traditional regional malls, its mix of national and exclusive/owned brands, and its value price focus. The company's strong cash flow allows Kohl's to invest in revenue drivers such as its omnichannel model and brand partnerships, which should support market share defensibility over time. The ratings also incorporate Kohl's good balance sheet management, including maintaining adjusted leverage at or below 2.5x (excluding 2020).

Fitch acknowledges that Kohl's is working with investment bankers and a sale of the company is a potential outcome. Should a sale be consummated, Kohl's rating trajectory would depend on the pro forma capital structure and longer-term financial policy.

Fitch's rated U.S. department store coverage includes national competitors Kohl's Corp (BBB-/Stable), Macy's Inc. (BBB-/Stable), Nordstrom, Inc. (BBB-/Negative), and regional player Dillard's Inc. (BBB-/Stable). Each have developed operating and financial strategies to contend with long-term secular challenges inherent in their space. Initiatives include investments in omnichannel models, portfolio reshaping to reduce exposure to weaker indoor malls, and efforts to strengthen merchandise assortments and service levels.

The national players have been best positioned to accelerate investment and transformation efforts given greater relative scale and cash flow generation. Dillard's has less ability to dedicate resources to this transformation albeit is better positioned than many smaller department and specialty stores.

While Macy's portfolio most closely resembles a traditional department store model, Kohl's differentiates itself via its off-mall presence and relatively higher private brand penetration. Nordstrom's business model includes full-line stores, which are focused more on higher-end price points and exposed primarily to A malls; the company also has a substantial off-price business to take advantage of share shifts toward this value channel.

Prior to the pandemic, the three national players operated with adjusted debt/EBITDAR below 3.5x (closer to mid-2x for Kohl's) to support investment grade ratings. Over the medium term, Fitch expects Macy's and Kohl's could operate with leverage in the mid-2x with Nordstrom modestly higher at above 3.0x. Reduced leverage at Macy's relative to pre-pandemic is largely due to proactive debt reduction. Dillard's adjusted leverage is projected around 1.0x, modestly below pre-pandemic levels closer to 1.5x; from a rating perspective the company's low leverage is balanced by the company's relatively smaller scale and regional positioning compared with peers.

Key Assumptions

Fitch's Key Assumptions Within Our Rating Case for the Issuer:

--Fitch projects Kohl's 2022 revenue could expand around 2% to $19.9 billion, similar to 2019 results, given an ongoing recovery in apparel and positive momentum from Kohl's merchandising and other initiatives, including its Sephora rollout. Revenue growth could be around 2.5% annually beginning 2023.

--EBITDA, which was close to $2.4 billion in 2021 with 12% margins exceeding the 10% recorded in 2019, could decline toward 2019 levels of $2.0 billion in 2022 assuming an increase to the currently benign promotional environment. EBITDA could grow modestly alongside topline beginning 2023, with EBITDA margins trending in the high-9% range.

--FCF (after dividends) is projected in the $150 million to $200 million range in 2022, down from the $1.5 billion in 2022 given forecasted EBITDA declines, a $250 million increase in projected capex (to $850 million), around $135 million in higher dividends (to $280 million) and some projected reversal of strong working capital benefits from 2020/2021. FCF could be around $400 million in 2023, assuming neutral working capital. Fitch expects FCF to be used for share buybacks, in line with Kohl's public commentary.

--Adjusted leverage, which was around 2.0x in 2021 given strong EBITDA results, is projected to trend in the mid-2x range beginning 2022 on Fitch's EBITDA assumptions and flattish debt levels.

RATING SENSITIVITIES

Factors that could, individually or collectively, lead to positive rating action/upgrade:

--Fitch's base case, including modest revenue and EBITDA growth and adjusted debt/EBITDAR (capitalizing leases at 8.0x) in the mid-2x could support an upgrade. Positive rating momentum would require Kohl's to conclude its current engagement with potential buyers, yielding greater confidence in the company's ability and willingness to maintain adjusted leverage below 3.0x.

Factors that could, individually or collectively, lead to negative rating action/downgrade:

--A downgrade could result from adjusted debt/EBITDAR (capitalizing leases at 8.0x) above 3.5x, the result of either financial policy decisions or EBITDA trending below $1.3 billion.

Liquidity and Debt Structure

Kohl's ended 2021 with a cash balance of $1.6 billion and no borrowings under its $1 billion credit facility. In October 2021, the company entered into a new $1 billion senior unsecured revolving credit facility maturing Oct. 22, 2026 which replaced its previous $1.5 billion secured ABL facility maturing July 2024. Fitch has assigned a 'BBB-' rating to the new credit facility.

Kohl's debt includes $1.9 billion of unsecured notes, which have been affirmed at 'BBB-'. Upcoming maturities include $164 million due February 2023 and $111 million due December 2023. Fitch assumes these will be refinanced.

Kohl's is currently engaging in a proxy contest with Macellum Advisors, GP, LLC, which owns close to 5% of Kohl's outstanding shares. Macellum has argued for operational changes at Kohl's in addition to potential actions such as a sale of the company or its real estate. Kohl's has hired investment bankers to explore strategic alternatives, which could include a sale of the company. The outcomes of these discussions and contest are uncertain. Fitch's ratings are based on Kohl's current capital structure and forecasts borne out of the company's existing operating strategy.

Issuer Profile

Kohl's is the second largest department store operator in the U.S. with approximately $20 billion in revenue across its digital presence and around 1,260 stores, largely in off-mall centers.

Summary of Financial Adjustments

Fitch adjusts EBITDA for non-cash stock-based compensation. Additionally, Fitch treats interest on lease liabilities and amortization of leased assets as operating costs in accordance with Fitch's current lease criteria. Total operating lease cost is capitalized by 8.0x to calculate adjusted debt.

Kohl's has reconfigured 500 prototype (typically 88,000 sf) stores through its standard-to-small initiative since 2017 to resemble a 55,000 to 64,000 sf-box by removing inventory and fixtures without an adverse impact to sales. This frees up almost 25,000 sf per location, which it could potentially sublease or sell to high traffic concepts such as hard discount and fitness centers. Kohl's owns about 56% of stores (when including ground leases), and geographically, the company is concentrated in the Midwestern and Western regions of the country, which make up almost half of the store base. The company is currently testing co-location partnerships in 16 locations, including Aldi, Total Wine and Planet Fitness.

Kohl's also has a partnership with Amazon.com, Inc., where it accepts merchandise returns at Kohl's locations across the nation and offers Amazon-branded products on a wholesale basis. Kohl's launched the returns program with Amazon.com nationwide in 2019 after a successful test in approximately 100 Kohl's locations and has stated that it has driven traffic into its stores and led to increased levels of new customers. In addition, Kohl's sells Amazon-branded smart electronics on a wholesale basis in over 600 stores, which followed an initial test of a store-within-a store concept.

Emphasis on National Brand Expansion: Kohl's continues to focus on national brand presence, with the segment representing 63% of sales in 2019 versus 50% in 2011. This increase in penetration has been driven by strong sales by activewear brands such as Under Armour, Nike, Skechers, and brands such as Levi's (owned by Levi Strauss & Co) and Carters. The expansion of national brands has enabled Kohl's to more effectively respond to the significant growth in the off price channel, giving the company the opportunity to carry the right merchandise mix, be locally relevant, and tailor products to customers that shop across channels. Its activewear business, which accounts for nearly 20% of its total business, has accelerated every year since it became a focus area in 2014.

Private and exclusive brands (such as Apt. 9, Croft and Barrow, Sonoma, Jumping Beans, and LC Lauren Conrad) compose approximately 37% of sales and have helped drive margin growth. Four of the company's top five brands are private, with two generating over $1 billion in sales. Two-thirds of its women's business (28% of total revenue) comes from proprietary brands.

Kohl's, Nordstrom and Macy's continue to invest aggressively in their businesses while maintaining healthy cash flow. These retailers have well developed omnichannel strategies, with online sales contributing close to 25% of total revenue (over 30% at Nordstrom), which should benefit their top-line as retail sales continue to move online. Kohl's off-mall real estate footprint provides some insulation from mall traffic challenges.

Kohl's U.S. department store peers include Dillard's, Inc., Nordstrom, Inc., Macy's, Inc. and J.C. Penney Company, Inc.

Nordstrom (BBB/Negative): Fitch projects adjusted leverage increasing to 7x in 2020 from 3x in 2019, based on EBITDA declining to approximately $550 million from $1.6 billion on a sales revenue decline of over 20% to $12 billion. Adjusted leverage is expected to decline to the low 3x in 2021, assuming sales declines of around 10% and EBITDA declines of around 20% in 2021 from 2019 levels.

Nordstrom's ratings reflect its position as a market share consolidator in the apparel, footwear and accessories space, with its differentiated merchandise and high level of customer service enabling the company to enjoy strong customer loyalty. The company has a well-developed product offering across a diverse portfolio of full line department stores, off-price Nordstrom Rack locations and multiple online channels.

Macy's (BB+/Negative): Fitch anticipates leverage increasing to over 11x in 2020 from 2.9x in 2019, based on EBITDA declining to approximately $325 million from $2 billion on a revenue decline of nearly 25% to $19.2 billion. Adjusted leverage is expected to be around 4x in 2021, assuming revenue declines of around 15% and EBITDA declines of around 40% in 2021 from 2019 levels.

Macy's ratings continue to reflect its position as the largest department store chain in the U.S. and Fitch's view of a prolonged timeframe for the company's operating trajectory to stabilize on a lower EBITDA base, given weak mall traffic and heightened competition from alternate channels that include online and off-price.

Dillard's (BB/Negative): Fitch anticipates EBITDA dropping to under $50 million in 2020 from $392 million in 2019 on a revenue decline of over 20% to $5 billion. Adjusted leverage is expected to be over 2x in 2021, assuming sales declines in the low-teens and EBITDA of approximately $300 million or around 30% lower compared to 2019 levels.

Dillard's ratings reflect the company's below-industry-average sales productivity (as measured by sales psf), operating profitability and geographical concentration relative to its larger department store peers, Kohl's, Nordstrom and Macy's. The ratings consider Dillard's strong liquidity and minimal debt maturities, with adjusted debt/EBITDAR expected to return to the 2x range in 2021.

J.C. Penney (CCC-): Fitch expects J.C. Penney's EBITDA could turn materially negative in 2020 in the range of negative $400 million. While the company ended 2019 with over $1.8 billion in liquidity (cash and revolver), the significant disruption from coronavirus have led to heightened liquidity concerns over the next 12 months given the projected cash burn.

Key Assumptions

Fitch's Key Assumptions Within Our Rating Case for the Issuer

Below are Fitch's projections prior to disruption related to coronavirus:

-Top-line and comparable store sales to be flat in 2020 and modestly positive in 2021;

-EBITDA decline of 5% in 2020 from 2019 level of $2.0 billion and flat EBITDA thereafter, with EBITDA margins settling in around 9.5% from 10.1% in 2019;

-Annual FCF of approximately $400 milllion after dividends, with capex spend around $750 million annually. FCF was expected to be primarily used towards share buybacks;

-Adjusted debt/EBITDAR leverage to remain fairly stable in the 2.3x-2.4x range.

Below are Fitch's revised projections reflecting the significant business interruption from coronavirus and the ramifications for a likely downturn in discretionary spending extending well into 2021:

--Fitch projects Kohl's 2020 revenue could decline over 20% to $14.9 billion and EBITDA could decline up to 75% to around $550 million, assuming store closures through mid-May and a slow recovery in customer traffic for the remainder of the year. While 2021 revenue and EBITDA should significantly rebound from depressed 2020 levels, Fitch expects 2021 revenue of approximately $16.8 billion and EBITDA of around $1.2 billion to be approximately 15% and 40% below 2019 levels given our expectations of a likely downturn in discretionary spending that could extend well into late 2021. Fitch's revenue expectations reflect its views that retail discretionary spending will decline 40% in first half 2020, be down mid-to-high single digits in second half 2020, and sales in 2021 will be down 8%-10% from 2019 levels, with declines in department store sales more material on a relative basis.

--Beginning 2022, Kohl's could resume low-single digit topline and EBITDA growth.

-FCF is expected to be around negative $350 million in 2020, largely due to a near $1.5 billion reduction in EBITDA, somewhat mitigated by lower cash taxes and significantly reduced capex of $250 million. Fitch has not factored in a suspension of Kohl's dividend or potential working capital benefits as Kohl's cuts inventory receipts. FCF in 2021 could be close to breakeven as EBITDA improves to $1.2 billion in 2021.

--Adjusted debt/EBITDAR, which was 2.3x in 2019, is projected to climb to over 6x in 2020 and decline to the mid-3.0x range in 2021 on EBITDA swings. Adjusted debt/EBITDAR in 2020 is impacted by around 1.0x from Kohl's decision to draw $1 billion on its revolver; Fitch expects this to be repaid in 2021.

RATING SENSITIVITIES

Developments that May, Individually or Collectively, Lead to Positive Rating Action

--Sustained low single digit positive comps, EBITDA growth in the low to mid-single digits beginning 2022, combined with sustaining adjusted debt/EBITDAR under 3x.

--A stabilization case would require Kohl's to meet Fitch's revised projections that include EBITDA increasing to $1.2 billion in 2021 and adjusted leverage moderating to the mid 3x in 2021 and 3x in 2022.

Developments That May, Individually or Collectively, Lead to Negative Rating Action

--A negative rating action could result on a more protracted or severe downturn and reduced confidence to return to top line and profitability growth in 2022 such that adjusted leverage is sustained above the mid-3x.

Liquidity and Debt Structure

Kohl's ended 2019 with a cash balance of $723 million. On March 19, 2020, the company announced it had fully borrowed on its $1 billion senior unsecured revolving bank credit facility due July 2024 to enhance liquidity. Additionally, it announced that it would suspend its share repurchase program, which it had initially targeted at $300 million-$400 million in 2020. The company could also temporarily suspend its $2.82 per share or $425 million annual dividend as seen in the case of Nordstrom and Macy's. Kohl's also announced that it is cutting capex to $250 million from its initial projection of $750 million in 2020 and pulling back on inventory receipts.

Fitch would expect Kohl's to repay the $1 billion revolver draw in 2021, given Fitch's current operating assumptions. Kohl's has no debt maturities prior to 2023 when $350 million in debt comes due.
