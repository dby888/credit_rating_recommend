Fitch Ratings: North American Software Firms Face AI Disruption Risk, Opportunities
1896 words
26 August 2025
Fitch Rating / Non Rating Action Commentary
FITRA
English
Copyright 2025 by Fitch Ratings Inc., Fitch Ratings Ltd. and its subsidiaries.

Fitch Ratings-Toronto/Chicago-26 August 2025: Disruption from artificial intelligence (AI) will lower barriers to entry and increase competition for the North American software sector but incumbents that successfully incorporate AI will see opportunities for product enhancement and efficiency gains, says Fitch Ratings. Most rated corporates benefit from the mission-critical nature of their products and high switching costs that mitigate these risks and provide additional cushion to adapt to changing market conditions.

Improvement in AI capabilities has reduced the time and cost needed to develop software applications, raising concerns regarding the disruption of incumbent software business models. This will have an outsized impact on new products with narrow scope and simple implementation such as basic customer interfaces, workflow automation, and content creation.

Fitch-rated software companies, which typically have mature product portfolios, benefit from sticky recurring revenues. Products with mission critical applications, a large installed base and high switching costs, which typically include enterprise applications deeply embedded within an organization’s operations, are generally better protected against competition. Products with a strong network effect, where a large installed base translates into greater value, are also more resilient to competitive pressure. Most investment grade issuers including Oracle Corporation (BBB/Stable), Cadence Design Systems (A-/Stable), and Constellation Software (BBB+/Stable) have products that fall under this category.

Issuers with non-mission-critical products and low switching costs — such as consumer software and certain business-product categories like remote virtual working tools — are more vulnerable to potential disruption by AI.

In addition to being a competitive threat, AI represents an opportunity for software companies to enhance their product offerings and generate additional revenues. Companies are embedding AI into their product offerings to enhance data analytics, customer service, and sales and marketing. They are also using AI for workflow automation and software coding, which will improve operating efficiencies and lower costs.

While AI introduces additional uncertainty to incumbent software companies’ longer-term growth prospects, the earnings impact over the forecast horizon is likely limited by high switching costs. Even if downside scenarios materialize, the sectors’ strong recurring cash flows will give companies sufficient time to adjust to new market conditions. Industry disruption caused by AI may also lead to a wave of M&A in the sector, as software companies with outdated products may seek to acquire AI capabilities or be acquired by competitors.

With generally strong operating profiles and stable cash flows, negative rating actions in the sector are often driven by aggressive financial policies rather than operational concerns. Strong horizontal software companies in mature product segments with strong FCF often adopt shareholder-friendly capital allocation policies that may pressure ratings over time, as seen with Oracle Corporation (BBB/Stable) during 2018-2021.

The highly predictable FCF characteristics of smaller vertical software companies also attract private equity ownership, which tends to optimize return on equity through increased financial leverage. GoTo Group (CCC+) and RSA Security (Redstone Buyer LLC; CCC) have already stretched capital structures put in place when interest rate expectations were much lower, leaving them with little rating headroom to manage market disruption or operational weakness.

Fitch Rates Oracle's Senior Notes Offering 'BBB'
2785 words
30 January 2025
Fitch Rating / Non Rating Action Commentary
FITRA
English
Copyright 2025 by Fitch Ratings Inc., Fitch Ratings Ltd. and its subsidiaries.

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-30 January 2025: Fitch Ratings has assigned a 'BBB' rating to Oracle Corporation's proposed benchmark size unsecured bonds. The company will use net proceeds from the senior notes offerings for repayment of upcoming maturities and other debt obligations, including outstanding notes and term loans, and for general corporate purposes. Oracle's current Long- and Short-Term Issuer Default Ratings (IDRs) are 'BBB' and 'F2', respectively. The Rating Outlook is Stable.

The refinancing is consistent with Fitch's assumptions that the company would refinance its debt maturities starting in fiscal 2025.

The ratings and Outlook are supported by Fitch's expectation that EBITDA leverage will remain below 3.5x. Recurring and durable revenue streams from Oracle's enterprise resource planning (ERP), health care IT (HCIT) and cloud services, which are more resilient than average technology industry peers, also underpin the ratings.

Key Rating Drivers

Strong Financial Flexibility: Oracle has strong financial flexibility, with staggered debt maturities through 2064 and $11 billion due by fiscal 2026. Oracle's available cash and FCF generation provides it ample financial flexibility. Oracle had about $11.3 billion in cash, cash equivalents and marketable securities available as of fiscal 2Q25. While FCF is depressed in the near term due to elevated capex in support of cloud services growth, Fitch estimates normalized annual post-dividend FCF will be near $10 billion.

Normalizing Financial Leverage: Following the Cerner acquisition, Oracle has limited incremental debt issuances and share buybacks, and repaid maturities through fiscal 2Q25. Fitch forecasts EBITDA leverage to approach 3x during FY25, and assumes the company will continue debt refinancing and share buybacks while maintaining EBITDA leverage near 3x.

Significant Recurring Revenue: Fitch expects significant recurring revenue and FCF from Oracle's large installed customer base, high renewal rate and increasing scale of its cloud products. During fiscal 1H25, nearly 80% of Oracle's revenue was recurring in nature. These strengths should continue as Oracle solidifies its market position in cloud services. Fitch believes cloud revenue, including SaaS and IaaS, has reached critical mass and will offset revenue declines in other categories, including on-premise revenue, resulting in consistent revenue growth.

Solid Competitive Position: Fitch believes Oracle is uniquely positioned for cloud-service leadership, supported by its solid as-a-service markets, including enterprise resources, human resources, supply chain, manufacturing, data and marketing. The company can leverage its large database and middleware installed base across both on-premise and cloud. Additionally, Oracle has established a solid position within the growing HCIT industry through Oracle Health. Fitch believes that customer and product diversification, along with missional criticality and high switching costs, will fortify Oracle's operating profile.

Significant Shareholder Returns: Fitch expects shareholder returns to be the primary focus of capital allocation without large acquisitions. The annual dividend will likely grow in line with profits, with the remainder allocated to share repurchases. The company had a net share repurchase of $2.5 billion during fiscal 2024 and $891 million during fiscal 1H25. This was substantially below the annual pace of $15 billion to $20 billion before the Cerner acquisition. Fitch anticipates that ongoing capital not used for acquisitions or debt repayment will be returned to shareholders, consistent with management's strategy.

Historically Acquisitive: Fitch recognizes potential event risk around acquisitions and expects Oracle to constrain share repurchases after large acquisitions. Large acquisitions that require external financing could lead to incremental debt. Besides the Cerner acquisition, Oracle acquired NetSuite, a provider of cloud applications, in 2016 for $9.1 billion. Oracle subsequently acquired several smaller companies, including CrowdTwist, DataFox, Grapeshot, Iridize, Moat, Oxygen Systems, Vocado, Adi Insights, FOEX, Newmetrix and Next Technik. Fitch believes more acquisitions are likely, potentially leading to significant aggregate acquisition spending.

Derivation Summary

Despite Oracle's strengthening credit metrics, the company faces competitive pressure from other cloud-based enterprise application providers like Salesforce.com, Inc. and Workday, Inc. Although these competitors focus on narrower sub-segments, they are growing significantly faster and potentially gaining market share.

Oracle also competes with other HCIT providers, including Epic Systems, Athenahealth, and McKesson. Despite these competitors, Fitch believes ERP's and HCIT's products are mission critical with high switch costs, resulting in some of the most durable revenue streams among technology industry peers.

Fitch compares Oracle to peers in similar rating categories within the broader technology industries, including International Business Machines (A-/Stable), Cadence Design Systems (A-/Stable) and Intel (BBB+/Stable). Fitch views Oracle's business model and recurring revenues as more durable than IBM and Intel, and greater end-market diversity than Cadence Design Systems.

IBM operates at similar revenue scale as Oracle with lower EBITDA margins and higher CFO-capex/debt ratios and provides greater clarity around its capital allocation policies. Cadence Design Systems has significantly smaller revenue scale. However, its EBITDA leverage is consistently below 0.5x and CFO-capex/debt ratios above 50%. Intel also operates at similar revenue scale as Oracle. It is in the midst of a capex super-cycle with pre-dividend FCF likely to remain negative for the next two years.

Key Assumptions

--Organic revenue growth in the high-single digits;

--EBITDA margins remain stable near 50%;

--Dividends of $4 billion-$5 billion annually;

--Upcoming debt maturity is refinanced;

--Acquisitions average $1 billion per year;

--Excess cash used for share repurchases.

RATING SENSITIVITIES

Factors That Could, Individually Or Collectively, Lead To Negative Rating Action/Downgrade

--EBITDA leverage sustains above 3.5x;

--(CFO-capex)/debt sustains below 15%;

--Revenue declines over a sustained period and EBITDA margins remain below 40%, reflecting an eroding market position.

Factors That Could, Individually Or Collectively, Lead To Positive Rating Action/Upgrade

--EBITDA leverage sustains below 3.0x;

--(CFO-capex)/debt sustains above 20%;

--Revenue growth at or above mid-single digits and EBITDA margin remaining stable at over 45%, reflecting a stable competitive position.

Liquidity and Debt Structure

Solid Liquidity: Fitch expects Oracle's liquidity to remain solid. As of Nov. 30, 2024, liquidity was supported by $11.3 billion of cash, cash equivalents and marketable securities; a $6 billion CP program; and Fitch's expectation for substantial annual pre-dividend FCF.

Manageable Maturity Profile: Oracle's maturity schedule is manageable, and Fitch believes the company has sufficient financial flexibility through expected FCF generation and cash on the balance sheet to address maturities. Upcoming maturities through fiscal 2026 total $10.6 billion of unsecured notes.

Issuer Profile

Oracle is a provider of enterprise application systems with over 80% of its revenues derived from the core enterprise application products and cloud services and remains one of the major players in the space.

Fitch believes cloud revenue, including SaaS and IaaS, has reached critical mass and will offset revenue declines in other categories, including on-premise revenue, resulting in consistent revenue growth.

Solid Competitive Position: Fitch believes Oracle is uniquely positioned for cloud-service leadership, supported by its solid as-a-service markets, including enterprise resources, human resources, supply chain, manufacturing, data and marketing. The company can leverage its large database and middleware installed base across both on-premise and cloud. Fitch believes that customer and product diversification, along with missional criticality and high switching costs, will fortify Oracle's operating profile.

Significant Shareholder Returns: Fitch expects shareholder returns to be the primary focus of capital allocation without large acquisitions. The annual dividend will likely grow in line with profits, with the remainder allocated to share repurchases. The company had a net share repurchase of $2.5 billion during fiscal 2024 and $891 million during fiscal 1H25. Fitch Ratings-Chicago-25 September 2024: Fitch Ratings has assigned a 'BBB' rating to Oracle Corporation's proposed benchmark size unsecured bonds. Oracle's current Long- and Short- Term Issuer Default Ratings (IDRs) are 'BBB' and 'F2', respectively. The Rating Outlook is Stable.

Key Rating Drivers

Strong Financial Flexibility: Oracle has strong financial flexibility, with staggered maturities of its debt through 2061, and $13.7 billion due through fiscal 2026. Fitch Ratings-Chicago-09 January 2024: Fitch Ratings has affirmed Oracle Corporation's (Oracle) Long-Term Issuer Default Rating (IDR) and unsecured debt at 'BBB'. Fitch has also affirmed the Short-Term IDR and CP rating at 'F2'. The Rating Outlook has been revised to Stable from Negative.

The ratings and Outlook revision reflect Fitch's expectation that EBITDA leverage will decline to 3.5x or below during the current fiscal year on the back of successful integration of Cerner as indicated by the normalization of EBITDA margins. The ratings are supported by recurring and durable revenue streams from Oracle's enterprise resource planning (ERP) and health care IT (HCIT) that are substantially more resilient than average technology industry peers.

Key Rating Drivers

Strong Financial Flexibility: Oracle has strong financial flexibility, with staggered maturities of its debt through 2061, and $14.7 billion due through fiscal 2026. Oracle had access to about $8.7 billion of cash and cash equivalents and marketable securities as of fiscal 2Q24 and generates approximately $6.5 billion-$7 billion of post-dividend FCF annually, lower than previously estimated due to higher capex investments in expanding Oracle Cloud Infrastructure capacity to support rising demand.

Normalizing Financial Leverage: Following the acquisition of Cerner, Oracle has limited incremental debt issuances and share buybacks and repaid maturities through FY24. With EBITDA leverage forecasted to be below 3.5x, Fitch assumes the company will resume debt refinancing and share buybacks in FY25 while maintaining EBITDA leverage below 3.5x.

Significant Recurring Revenue: Fitch expects significant recurring revenue and FCF from Oracle's large installed customer base, high renewal rate and increasing scale of its cloud products. During fiscal 2Q24, 74% of Oracle's revenue was recurring in nature. These strengths should continue throughout the transition to cloud from on-premise solutions. Fitch believes cloud revenue, including SaaS and IaaS, has reached critical mass and will offset revenue declines in other categories, including on-premise revenue, resulting in consistent revenue growth.

Solid Competitive Position: Fitch believes Oracle is uniquely positioned for cloud-service leadership supported by its solid as-a-service markets, including enterprise resources, human resources, supply chain, manufacturing, data and marketing, as well as its ability to leverage its large database and middleware installed base across both on-premise and cloud. Oracle has also established a solid position within the growing HCIT industry through Oracle Health.

In the ERP market, Oracle faces tough competition from strong but narrowly focused as-a-service companies, particularly Workday, Inc (human capital management and financial planning and analysis) and Salesforce.com, Inc. (sales, marketing and service). Historical on-premise competitors, particularly SAP SE, also are shifting to cloud offerings but lag Oracle in scale and growth. In the HCIT market, Oracle faces established competitors including Epic Systems, Athenahealth, and McKesson. Fitch believes the customer and product diversification that comes with scale along with the missional criticality and high switching cost will fortify Oracle's operating profile.

Normalizing Profitability: After a brief period of decline in EBITDA margins post the Cerner acquisition, Oracle's profitability has improved in recent quarters and approaching pre-acquisition levels. Fitch expects its EBITDA margins to trend toward 50% over the intermediate term as Cerner is fully integrated and Oracle's continuing growth in cloud products offsets a decline in on-premise products.

Significant Shareholder Returns: Beyond the commitment associated with the Cerner acquisition to limit shareholder returns, Fitch expects shareholder returns to be main area of capital allocation in the absence of large acquisitions. The annual dividend is likely to grow in line with profits, with the remainder going to share repurchases. The company repurchased $1.3 billion in shares during fiscal 2023 and $600 million during fiscal 1H24, substantially below the annual pace of $15 billion to $20 billion prior to the Cerner acquisition. Upon returning of EBITDA leverage to below 3.5x, Fitch anticipates ongoing capital not used for acquisitions or repayment of maturing debt to be returned to shareholders, consistent with management's articulated strategy.

Historically Acquisitive: Fitch recognizes potential event risk around acquisitions and expects Oracle to constrain share repurchases in lieu of making large acquisitions. Incremental debt is possible with large acquisitions that require external financing. In addition to the Cerner acquisition, the company acquired NetSuite, a provider of cloud applications, in 2016 for $9.1 billion.

Oracle subsequently made several smaller acquisitions, including CrowdTwist, DataFox, Grapeshot, Iridize, Moat, Oxygen Systems, Vocado, Adi Insights, FOEX, Newmetrix and Next Technik. Fitch believes more acquisitions are likely, potentially resulting in significant aggregate acquisition spending.

Derivation Summary

Oracle's ratings are supported by Fitch's expectation that the company will reduce its debt at a pace aligned with its debt maturities and limit its shareholder returns until its gross leverage declines to below 3.5x. Beyond the company's intended gross leverage target, Fitch expects the company to primarily allocate its FCF towards shareholder returns and acquisitions while maintaining its gross leverage at levels consistent with Fitch's rating sensitivities.

In spite of the shareholder-friendly policies, Oracle's material cash balance and cash generation capabilities provide the company with ample financial flexibility. Fitch expects Oracle to generate approximately $6.5 billion-$7 billion in post-dividend FCF and had approximately $8.7 billion in cash and cash equivalent and marketable securities as of fiscal 2Q24. Unexpected deterioration in Oracle's operating profile, potentially due to change in market dynamics, would also have consequential impact on Oracle's credit protection metrics.

Fitch believes, in spite of Oracle's strong operating profile, Oracle faces competitive pressure from other cloud-based enterprise application providers such as Salesforce.com, Inc. and Workday, Inc. While these competitors compete in narrower sub-segments, they are growing at significantly faster pace, potentially gaining share against the incumbents. Oracle also competes with other HCIT providers including Epic Systems, Athenahealth, and McKesson. Despite the numerous competitors, Fitch believes ERP and HCIT products are characterized as mission critical with high switch cost resulting in revenue streams that are among the most durable among technology industry peers.

Key Assumptions

--Organic revenue growth in the mid-single digits;

--EBITDA margins remain stable near 50%;

--Dividends rise to $4 billion-$5 billion annually;

--Debt maturity starting in fiscal 2025 is refinanced;

--Starting in FY25, share repurchase in the range of $5 billion-$10 billion annually partially financed with incremental debt;

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-07 November 2022: Fitch Ratings has downgraded Oracle Corporation's Long-Term Issuer Default Rating (IDR) and unsecured debt to 'BBB' from 'BBB+', and resolved the Rating Watch Negative. Fitch has also assigned 'BBB' rating to the new unsecured notes issuance. The Rating Outlook is Negative. Fitch has also downgraded the Short-Term IDR and CP rating to 'F2' from 'F1'.

The downgrades follow Oracle's incurrence of term loan and plan to issue unsecured notes for refinancing of outstanding DDTL for the Cerner Corporation acquisition that was closed in June 2022. The proceeds from the new notes will be used to repay the $10.1 billion bridge financing currently outstanding. The ratings also contemplate Oracle's intent to limit share repurchases to approximately $600 million per quarter and dividend increases in a manner consistent with its historical pattern until gross leverage is reduced to approximately 3.5x.

Key Rating Drivers

High Financial Leverage: Oracle's financial leverage is high for the rating category. Fitch estimates Oracle's gross leverage to approach 3.5x by FYE 2024 through a combination of EBITDA growth and $4.75 billion debt maturity payment. Fitch assumes Oracle will repay maturities until its gross leverage reaches 3.5x.

Strong Financial Flexibility: Oracle has strong financial flexibility, with staggered maturities of its debt through 2061, and $20.8 billion due through fiscal FY 2026. Oracle had access to about $11.2 billion of cash and cash equivalents and marketable securities as of fiscal 1Q23 and generates approximately $9 billion of post-dividend FCF annually.

Significant Recurring Revenue: Fitch expects significant recurring revenue and FCF from Oracle's large installed customer base, high renewal rate and increasing scale of its cloud products. These strengths should continue throughout the transition to cloud from on-premise solutions. Fitch believes cloud revenue has reached critical mass and will offset revenue declines in other categories, including on-premise revenue, resulting in modest revenue growth.

Solid Competitive Position: Fitch believes Oracle is uniquely positioned for cloud-service leadership by its solid as-a-service markets, including enterprise resources, human resources, supply chain, manufacturing, data and marketing, as well as its ability to leverage its large database and middleware installed base across both on-premise and cloud. With the Cerner acquisition, Oracle has established a solid position within the growing health care IT industry.

Oracle faces tough competition from strong but narrowly focused as-a-service companies, particularly Workday, Inc (human capital management and financial planning and analysis) and Salesforce.com, Inc. (sales, marketing and service). Historical on-premise competitors, particularly SAP SE, also are shifting to cloud offerings but lag Oracle in scale and growth. Fitch believes the customer and product diversification that comes with scale will fortify Oracle's operating profile.

Improving Cloud Profitability: Fitch expects margins to gradually expand over the medium term on the back of rising scale efficiency in Oracle's cloud products. Oracle's operating EBITDA rose by mid- to high-single digits in the previous few quarters, stronger than revenue growth, reflecting modest margin expansion. Fitch expects its operating EBITDA margin to trend toward 50% over the intermediate term, as growth in cloud products offsets a decline in on-premise products and operational optimization post Cerner merger is realized.

Significant Shareholder Returns: Beyond the commitment associated with the debt issuance to limit shareholder returns, Fitch expects shareholder returns in excess of annual FCF in the absence of large acquisitions. The annual dividend is likely to grow, with the remainder going to share repurchases. The company repurchased $16.2 billion in shares during fiscal 2022 and $552 million in 1Q FY23. Fitch anticipates ongoing capital not used for acquisitions or repayment of maturing debt to be returned to shareholders, consistent with management's articulated strategy.

Historically Acquisitive: Fitch recognizes potential event risk around acquisitions and expects Oracle to constrain share repurchases in lieu of making large acquisitions. Incremental debt is possible with large acquisitions. Incremental debt is possible with large acquisitions that require external financing. In addition to the Cerner acquisition, the company acquired NetSuite, a provider of cloud applications, in 2016 for $9.1 billion. Oracle subsequently made several smaller acquisitions, including CrowdTwist, DataFox, Grapeshot, Iridize, Moat, Oxygen Systems and Vocado. Fitch believes more acquisitions are likely, potentially resulting in significant aggregate acquisition spending.

Derivation Summary

Fitch's ratings are supported by our expectation that Oracle will reduce its debt at a pace aligned with its debt maturities and limit its shareholder returns until its gross leverage reaches 3.5x. Beyond the company's intended gross leverage target, Fitch expects the company to primarily allocate its FCF towards shareholder returns and acquisitions while maintaining its gross leverage at levels consistent with Fitch's rating sensitivities.

In spite of the shareholder-friendly policies, Oracle's material cash balance and cash generation capabilities provide the company with ample financial flexibility. Fitch expects Oracle to generate approximately $9 billion in post-dividend FCF and had approximately $11.2 billion in cash and cash equivalent and marketable securities as of fiscal 1Q23. Unexpected deterioration in Oracle's operating profile, potentially due to change in market dynamics, would also have consequential impact on Oracle's credit protection metrics.

In spite of Oracle's strong operating profile, Fitch believes Oracle faces competitive pressure from other cloud-based enterprise application providers such as Salesforce.com, Inc. and Workday, Inc. While these competitors compete in narrower sub-segments, they are growing at significantly faster pace, potentially gaining share against the incumbents.

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-21 December 2021: LEAD PARAGRAPH

Fitch Ratings has placed Oracle Corporation's ratings on Rating Watch Negative (RWN), including the 'BBB+' Long-term Issuer Default Rating (IDR) and unsecured debt, and the 'F1' Short-term IDR and commercial paper. The RWN follows the company's announced agreement to acquire Cerner Corporation.

Fitch expects the transaction to increase leverage at the time of closing. The potential rating outcome ranges from single-notch to multi-notch downgrades as the leveraging transaction deviates from Fitch's previous expectations of debt reduction.

On Dec. 20, 2021, Oracle announced an agreement to acquire Cerner Corporation in an all-cash tender offer, valuing the company's equity at $28.3 billion. Cerner also has approximately $1.8 billion debt outstanding. Cerner is a supplier of health information technology services, devices and hardware. During 2020, the company generated revenue of $5.5 billion, EBITDA of $1.7 billion and post dividend-FCF of $635 million. The proposed transaction values the company at 17.5x EBITDA.

Key Rating Drivers

Potential Debt Issuance Deviates from Deleveraging Expectations: Oracle's funding options could include incremental debt issuance that deviates from Fitch's expectation for debt reduction to levels consistent with the 'BBB+' rating category, despite the incremental EBITDA contribution from the pending acquisition. The rating outcome post acquisition would depend on any plan the company may have to reduce financial leverage within a reasonable period of time.

Cerner Acquisition Provides Access to Healthcare Industry: Oracle's proposed acquisition of Cerner could provide the company with access to the healthcare industry and expand Oracle's addressable market. While the strategic value may not be realized in the near term, the transaction could accelerate Oracle's growth in the healthcare industry in the longer term. In the near term following the acquisition, Cerner's products could benefit from Oracle's cloud infrastructure.

High Financial Leverage: Oracle's financial leverage is high for the rating category. Prior to the funding for the announced acquisition of Cerner, Fitch estimates gross leverage of 3.7x LTM 2Q FY2022, declining to near 3x by FY24 assuming the company continues to repay its debt maturities through FY24.

Strong Financial Flexibility: Oracle has strong financial flexibility, with staggered maturities of its debt through 2061, and $20 billion due through fiscal FY2025, despite shareholder-friendly financial policies. Oracle had access to about $22.8 billion of cash and cash equivalents and marketable securities as of fiscal 2Q22 and generates approximately $11 billion of post-dividend FCF annually.

Significant Recurring Revenue: Fitch expects significant recurring revenue and FCF from Oracle's large installed customer base, high renewal rate and increasing scale of its cloud products. These strengths should continue throughout the transition to cloud from on-premise solutions. Fitch believes cloud revenue has reached critical mass and will offset revenue declines in other categories, including on-premise revenue, resulting in modest revenue growth.

Solid Competitive Position: Fitch believes Oracle is uniquely positioned for cloud-service leadership due to its solid as-a-service markets, including enterprise resources, human resources, supply chain, manufacturing, data and marketing, as well as its ability to leverage its large database and middleware installed base across both on-premise and cloud.

Oracle faces tough competition from strong but narrowly focused as-a-service companies, particularly Workday, Inc (human capital management and financial planning and analysis) and Salesforce.com, Inc. (sales, marketing and service). Historical on-premise competitors, particularly SAP SE, also are shifting to cloud offerings but lag Oracle in scale and growth. Fitch believes the customer and product diversification that comes with scale will fortify Oracle's operating profile.

Improving Cloud Profitability: Fitch expects margins to gradually expand over the medium term on the back of rising scale efficiency in Oracle's cloud products. Oracle's operating EBITDA rose by mid-to high-single-digits in the previous few quarters, stronger than revenue growth, reflecting modest margin expansion. Fitch expects its operating EBITDA margin to trend toward the high-40s over the intermediate term, as growth in cloud products offsets a decline in on-premise products.

Significant Shareholder Returns: Beyond the commitment associated with the proposed transaction to limit shareholder returns, Fitch expects shareholder returns in excess of annual FCF in the near term in the absence of large acquisitions. The annual dividend is likely to grow, with the remainder going to share repurchases. Fitch anticipates ongoing capital not used for acquisitions or repayment of maturing debt to be returned to shareholders, consistent with management's articulated strategy.

Historically Acquisitive: Fitch recognizes potential event risk around acquisitions and expects Oracle to constrain share repurchases in lieu of making large acquisitions. Incremental debt is possible with large acquisitions that require external financing. In addition to the proposed transaction, the company acquired NetSuite, a provider of cloud applications, in 2016 for $9.1 billion. Oracle subsequently made several smaller acquisitions, including CrowdTwist, DataFox, Grapeshot, Iridize, Moat, Oxygen Systems and Vocado. Fitch believes more acquisitions are likely, potentially resulting in significant aggregate acquisition spending.

Substantial Cash on Balance Sheet: The tax law revisions in 2017 have enabled Oracle to efficiently reposition its offshore cash for domestic use; as of fiscal 2Q FY22, it had approximately $22.8 billion in cash and cash equivalents and marketable securities. The company repurchased $15 billion in shares during fiscal 1H22 and $35 billion since 1Q FY21.

Derivation Summary

Fitch's ratings are supported by the expectation that Oracle will reduce its debt at a pace aligned with its debt maturities and use its excess cash for shareholder returns or acquisitions. The announced agreement to acquire Cerner could result in the company deviating from its previous plan to reduce its debt to $76 billion by FY2022. During fiscal 1H 2021, Oracle has repurchased $15 billion of its own shares.

In spite of the shareholder-friendly policies, Oracle's material cash balance and cash generation capabilities provide the company with ample financial flexibility. As of fiscal 2Q 2022, the company generated over $10 billion in LTM FCF and had approximately $22.8 billion in cash and cash equivalent and marketable securities. Unexpected deterioration in Oracle's operating profile, potentially due to change in market dynamics, would also have consequential impacts on Oracle's credit protection metrics.

In spite of Oracle's strong operating profile, Fitch believes Oracle faces competitive pressure from other cloud-based enterprise application providers such as Salesforce.com, Inc. and Workday, Inc. While these competitors compete in narrower sub-segments, they are growing at significantly faster pace, potentially gaining share against the incumbents.

Fitch has affirmed Oracle's Short-Term IDR at 'F1'. The Rating Outlook is Negative.

The downgrade follows a deviation from Fitch's expectation of continued debt reduction on maturity to bring credit metrics to levels consistent with an 'A-' rating. Fitch expects Oracle's operating profile to remain stable with annual post-dividend FCF generation of over $8 billion. In conjunction with its material cash balance, Fitch expects Oracle to have ample liquidity and financial flexibility to address debt maturities.

Key Rating Drivers

Issuance Deviates from Deleveraging Expectations: Oracle's proposed debt issuance deviates from Fitch's expectation of debt reduction to levels consistent with the 'A-' rating category, despite its intention to use the proceeds to repay debt maturities through FY22. The 'BBB+' rating category appropriately reflects the strong operating profile of the company and the aggressive capital structure given the higher credit risk resulting from high gross leverage and lower (CFO-capex)/Total Debt ratio.

High Financial Leverage: Oracle's financial leverage is high for the rating category. Fitch estimates gross leverage of 4.4x for FY21, declining to 3.2x by FY24 assuming the company continues to repay its debt maturities through FY24. However, Oracle's recent capital market activities suggest that the company could maintain debt at elevated levels that are inconsistent with the 'A' rating category.

Strong Financial Flexibility: Oracle has strong financial flexibility, with the staggered maturities of its debt through 2060, and $12 billion due through to FY23, despite its shareholder-friendly financial policy. Oracle had access to about $36 billion of cash and cash equivalents and marketable securities as of 3QFY21 and generates over $12 billion of pre-dividend FCF annually.

Significant Recurring Revenue: Fitch expects significant recurring revenue and FCF from Oracle's large installed customer base, high renewal rate and increasing scale of its cloud products. These strengths should continue throughout the transition to cloud from on-premise solutions. Fitch believes cloud revenue has reached critical mass and will offset revenue falls in other categories, including on-premise revenue, resulting in modest revenue growth.

Solid Competitive Position: Fitch believes Oracle is uniquely positioned for cloud-service leadership by its solid as-a-Service markets (including enterprise resources, human resources, supply chain, manufacturing, data and marketing) as well as its ability to leverage its large database and middleware installed base across both on-premise and cloud. Oracle faces tough competition from strong but narrowly focused as-a-service companies, particularly Workday, Inc (human capital management and financial planning and analysis) and Salesforce.com, Inc. (sales, marketing and service). Historical on-premise competitors, particularly SAP, also are shifting to cloud offerings but lag Oracle in scale and growth. Fitch believes the customer and product diversification that comes with scale will fortify Oracle's operating profile.

Less Profitable Cloud to Improve: Fitch expects margins to gradually expand over the medium term on the back of rising scale efficiency in Oracle's cloud products. Oracle's operating EBITDA rose by mid to high-single-digits in the previous few quarters, stronger than revenue growth, reflecting modest margin expansion. Fitch expects its operating EBITDA margin to trend toward the high-40s over the intermediate term, as growth in cloud products offsets a decline in on-premise products.

Significant Shareholder Returns: Fitch expects shareholder returns in excess of annual FCF in the near-term in the absence of large acquisitions. Annual dividend is likely to grow, with the remainder going to share repurchases. Fitch anticipates ongoing capital not used for acquisitions or repayment of maturing debt to be returned to shareholders, consistent with management's articulated strategy. The company authorized an additional $20 billion in share repurchase at the start of 4QFY21.

Historically Acquisitive: Fitch recognizes potential event risk around acquisitions and expects Oracle to constrain share repurchases in lieu of making large acquisitions. Since then, Oracle made a number of smaller acquisitions, including CrowdTwist, DataFox, Grapeshot, Iridize, Moat, Oxygen Systems and Vocado. Fitch believes continuing acquisitions are likely, potentially resulting in significant aggregate acquisition spend.

Substantial Cash on Balance Sheet: The tax reform of 2017 has enabled Oracle to efficiently reposition its offshore cash for domestic use; as of 3QFY21, it had approximately $36 billion in cash and cash equivalent, and marketable securities. Fitch expects Oracle to use the cash primarily for shareholder returns and acquisitions while repaying debt as it matures. The company repurchased $4 billion in shares during 3QFY21 and $18.2 billion in the last 12 months. LTM through fiscal 3Q 2021, Oracle has repurchased $18.2 billion of its own shares.

As of fiscal 3Q 2021, the company generated nearly $9 billion in LTM FCF and had approximately $36 billion in cash and cash equivalent and marketable securities. Fitch expects Oracle's gross leverage to decline with debt maturities and eventually to less than 3.0x. Deviations from Fitch's expectations could result in negative rating actions. Unexpected deterioration in Oracle's operating profile, potentially due to change in market dynamics, would also have consequential impact on Oracle's credit protection metrics.

In spite of Oracle's strong operating profile, Fitch believes Oracle faces competitive pressure from other cloud-based enterprise application providers such as Salesforce.com, Inc. and Workday, Inc. While these competitors compete in narrower sub-segments, they are growing at significantly faster pace, potentially gaining share against the incumbents.

Fitch anticipates ongoing capital not used for acquisitions or repayment of maturing debt to be returned to shareholders, consistent with management's articulated strategy. The company authorized an additional $15 billion in share repurchase at the start of 4QFY20.

Historically Acquisitive: Fitch recognizes potential event risk around acquisitions and expects Oracle to constrain share repurchases in lieu of making large acquisitions. Incremental debt is possible with large acquisitions that require external financing. The company acquired NetSuite, a provider of cloud applications, in 2016 for $9.1 billion. Since then, Oracle made a number of smaller acquisitions, including CrowdTwist, DataFox, Grapeshot, Iridize, Moat, Oxygen Systems and Vocado. Fitch believes continuing acquisitions are likely, potentially resulting in significant aggregate acquisition spend.

Substantial Cash on Balance Sheet: The tax reform of 2017 has enabled Oracle to efficiently reposition its offshore cash for domestic use; as of 3QFY0, it had approximately $26 billion in cash and cash equivalent. Fitch expects Oracle to use the cash primarily for shareholder returns and acquisitions while repaying debt as it matures. The company repurchased $4 billion in shares during 3QFY20 and $20 billion in the last 12 months. Fitch expects the company's announced $15 billion for share repurchase to continue while excess cash remains on its balance sheet.

Derivation Summary

Fitch's ratings are supported by the agency's expectation that after the proposed notes issuance, Oracle will reduce its debt at a pace that is aligned with its debt maturities and use its excess cash for shareholder returns or acquisitions. Oracle's strong balance sheet and cash generation capabilities provide the company with ample financial flexibility, in spite of its shareholder-friendly policies. The company repaid $4.5 billion of debt and returned $22 billion to shareholders, while generating over $9 billion in FCF, in the last 12 months through 3QFY20. It also had approximately $26 billion in readily available cash. Fitch expects Oracle's gross leverage to decline with debt maturities to 2.9x by FY22 and eventually to below 2.5x. Deviations from Fitch's expectations could result in negative rating action. Unexpected deterioration in Oracle's operating profile, potentially due to changes in market dynamics, would also affect Oracle's credit-protection metrics.

Fitch expects Oracle to remain a major competitor in the enterprise application segment as it transitions to cloud-based software as a service, from on-premise enterprise applications. Its revenue rose by 3% on a constant currency basis during 3QFY20.

However, Fitch believes Oracle faces competitive pressure from other cloud-based enterprise application providers, such as Salesforce.com and Workday. These companies compete in narrower sub-segments, but are expanding at significantly faster pace, potentially gaining share against incumbents.
