Fitch Rates General Motors' Proposed Senior Unsecured Notes 'BBB'

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-05 May 2025: Fitch Ratings has assigned a 'BBB' rating to General Motors Company's (GM) proposed issuance of senior unsecured notes. GM's Long-Term Issuer Default Rating (IDR) is 'BBB' and the Rating Outlook is Positive.

GM's ratings and Outlook reflect Fitch's expectation that GM's EBIT and FCF margins will remain relatively strong over the longer term. This is due to increased vehicle profitability, benefits from the company's $2.0 billion fixed-cost reduction program and cost savings from the wind-down of the Cruise robotaxi business. Near-term profitability could be affected by the imposition of automotive tariffs in the U.S. However, Fitch expects GM to have sufficient financial and operational flexibility to keep its credit profile largely intact.

Key Rating Drivers

Potential Tariff Effects: GM expects current U.S. tariffs on imported autos and auto parts to reduce its adjusted EBIT by $4.0 billion-$5.0 billion in 2025. However, it plans to offset at least 30% of this through various cost and operational adjustments. The company lowered its adjusted automotive FCF guidance but still anticipates generating $7.5 billion-$10.0 billion in 2025, including spending $10.0 billion-$11.0 billion in capex and battery joint venture investments.

Although GM reduced its 2025 guidance because of tariffs, Fitch believes the revised figures demonstrate the company's sufficient operational and financial flexibility to manage the current tariff environment. The company has not detailed what, if any, tariff-related operational changes it may make over the longer term. However, Fitch expects it will continue to produce solid margins while maintaining a strong liquidity position and low leverage, consistent with the Positive Outlook.

Mid-Single-Digit EBIT Margins: GM's profitability has improved markedly over the past several years as the company's efforts to reduce complexity in its vehicles has increased operational efficiency. These initiatives have led to significantly higher margins on new and updated models. GM has reduced its fixed costs by about $2.0 billion vs. 2022 levels, net of depreciation. This reduction is in addition to previous fixed-cost reduction programs completed over the past five years. The company expects to save about $500 million in 2025 with the wind-down of Cruise.

Because of these initiatives, GM's global automotive EBIT margin, according to Fitch's methodology, was 6.2% in 2024, at the high end of Fitch's expectations. Fitch expects margins to run at a lower level in 2025, largely due to tariff impacts. However, over the longer term, Fitch expects margins to increase, partly driven by improved profitability on the company's battery electric vehicles (BEVs) as those programs continue to scale up.

Proposed Notes: The proposed notes will rank equally with GM's other senior unsecured debt. The company intends to set aside an amount equal to the proceeds of the notes for general corporate purposes, including refinancing a portion of its $1.25 billion of 6.125% notes due October 2025, as well as funding a portion of the $1.8 billion term loan it has agreed to make to its Ultium Cells LLC (Ultium) joint venture to facilitate the repayment of Ultium's U.S. Department of Energy Advanced Technology Vehicles Manufacturing (ATVM) loan.

Strong FCF Generation: GM's post-dividend automotive FCF margin (based on Fitch's methodology) was 6.5% in 2024, higher than expected, due to solid net vehicle pricing and mix, and relatively low working capital cash usage. Post-dividend FCF was also supported GM's decision to return cash to shareholders via discretionary share repurchases, while keeping common dividends relatively low. Fitch expects capex as a percentage of revenue to run in the 6.0%-6.5% range over the next few years. Fitch expects General Motors Financial Company, Inc. (GMF) to continue paying dividends to GM, which Fitch incorporates into its automotive FCF calculation.

1.0x Automotive Leverage: Fitch expects EBITDA gross leverage to generally run at about 1.0x over the intermediate term. GM's leverage will be partly influenced by dividends from GMF and the company's China JVs, as Fitch includes GMF's dividends and dividends received from unconsolidated JVs to GM's automotive EBITDA (including Cruise) when calculating leverage. GM's actual automotive gross EBITDA leverage at YE 2024 was 0.9x.

Peer Analysis

GM's operating profile is generally in line with its mass-market automotive peers in the 'BBB' rating category. GM has a relatively strong competitive position, with a top five share in most of the markets where it operates. However, it is geographically less diversified than most major global mass-market auto manufacturers, with very little presence in Western Europe and a heavy reliance on North America for much of its profit and FCF generation.

GM's product diversity is relatively high, with Chevrolet and Cadillac serving as its anchor brands, augmented by Buick and GMC. The company manufactures passenger and light commercial vehicles spanning the mass-market and luxury segments. However, luxury sales, represented by Cadillac, remain much lower than those of dedicated luxury manufacturers, such as Mercedes-Benz Group AG (A/Stable), Bayerische Motoren Werke AG or the luxury divisions of global mass-market competitors such as Toyota Motor Corporation (A+/Stable).

GM's financial profile is relatively strong compared to similar mass-market peers. EBIT, EBITDA and FCF margins have generally been at the higher end of its peer group, driven largely by its relatively strong performance in North America. However, margins could come under some pressure over the intermediate term as the company accelerates its electrification initiatives. Leverage remains relatively low compared to its peer group, with gross EBITDA leverage generally running around 1.0x.

Key Assumptions

--In 2025, industry sales are negatively affected by tariffs in the U.S., while outside the U.S., sales are mixed. Beyond 2025, industry sales rise at a low- to mid-single-digit rate annually;

--In the U.S., GM manages dealer inventories at targeted levels;

--Margins over the next several years are supported by higher profitability on new models and the company's cost reduction efforts, offset by lower net pricing on carryover vehicles and unfavorable mix as BEVs grow as a share of overall production;

--Capex runs at about 6.0%-6.5% of revenue over the next several years as the company makes BEV and technology investments;

--Working capital is a source of cash over the longer term on increased wholesale volumes and a normalized production environment;

--Post-dividend FCF margins run in the 5.5%-6.0% range over the longer term, reflecting solid EBITDA, positive working capital, continued dividends from GMF and relatively low common dividends;

--The company maintains a strong automotive cash position over the forecast horizon, with total liquidity, including credit facility capacity, above $30 billion;

--GMF dividends to GM, which are included in Fitch's FCF calculation, run near recent historical levels over the next several years.

RATING SENSITIVITIES

Factors that Could, Individually or Collectively, Lead to Negative Rating Action/Downgrade

--A shift in financial policy that lowers the company's automotive cash target below $18 billion (excluding Cruise's cash);

--Sustained global automotive EBIT margins below 3.0%;

--Sustained FCF margins below 1.5%;

--Sustained EBITDA leverage above 2.0x.

Factors that Could, Individually or Collectively, Lead to Positive Rating Action/Upgrade

--Continued execution and maturation of GM's BEV strategy;

--Production and inventory discipline, leading to sustained North America automotive EBIT margins above 9.0% (according to GM's calculations) and global automotive EBIT margins above 5.0% (according to Fitch's calculations);

--Sustained FCF margins above 2.0%;

--Sustained EBITDA leverage below 1.0x;

--Retention of capital allocation priorities, including dividends and share repurchases, that support through-the-cycle financial flexibility.

Liquidity and Debt Structure

GM had $19.0 billion of automotive cash and marketable securities as of March 31, 2025 (including Cruise). In addition, GM had $13.8 billion of availability on its credit facilities, excluding a $2.0 billion 364-day facility that has been designated for exclusive use by GMF.

Fitch has treated $2.8 billion of GM's automotive cash as not readily available for purposes of calculating net metrics. In addition, for its captive finance adjustment, Fitch has calculated an allowable debt to equity ratio of 5.0x for GMF and assumed that GM makes a $7.6 billion equity injection into GMF, funded with available cash, to bring GMF's debt to equity ratio down to 5.0x. Fitch has added this adjustment to its calculation of GM's not readily available cash.

As of March 31, 2025, GM's automotive debt consisted of $15.6 billion of senior unsecured debt, primarily senior notes, and $146 million of secured debt. In April 2025, GM repaid $500 million of senior unsecured notes at maturity. Also, in April 2025, GM entered an agreement to fund a $1.8 billion term loan to its Ultium joint venture to facilitate a prepayment of Ultium's ATVM loan.

Issuer Profile

GM is a global auto manufacturer that offers passenger vehicles, crossovers and SUVs in the mass-market and luxury segments, as well as light and medium-duty commercial vehicles. GMF offers financial services to dealers and customers.

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-29 October 2024: Fitch Ratings has affirmed the Long-Term Issuer Default Ratings (IDRs) of General Motors Company (GM), General Motors Financial Company, Inc (GMF), and GMF's General Motors Financial of Canada, Ltd. subsidiary (GMF Canada) at 'BBB'. In addition, Fitch has affirmed the Short-Term IDR of GMF at 'F2'. Fitch has also affirmed GM's and GMF's senior unsecured debt at 'BBB' and GMF's preferred shares at 'BB+'. Fitch has affirmed GMF's commercial paper (CP) rating at 'F2' and GMF's and GMF Canada's Shareholder Support Ratings (SSRs) at 'bbb'.

The Rating Outlook has been revised to Positive from Stable.

The Positive Outlook reflects Fitch's expectation that GM's EBIT and FCF margins will remain relatively strong over the longer term. This is due to increased profitability on both the company's internal combustion engine vehicles (ICEVs) and battery electric vehicles (BEVs). The company's fixed-cost reduction program will further support margins by reducing costs by $2.0 billion (vs. 2022 levels), net of depreciation, by YE 2024.

Fitch expects these improvements to offset headwinds from normalized net pricing, BEV growth and a challenging competitive environment in international markets, especially China.

Fitch could upgrade GM's ratings in the next 12-18 months.

Key Rating Drivers

Mid-Single-Digit EBIT Margins: GM's profitability has improved markedly over the past several years as the company's efforts to reduce complexity in its ICEVs and BEVs has increased operational efficiency. These initiatives have led to significantly higher margins on new and updated models. GM is also on-track to reduce its fixed costs by about $2.0 billion vs. 2022 levels, net of depreciation, by YE 2024. This reduction is in addition to previous fixed-cost reduction programs completed over the past five years.

As a result of these initiatives, Fitch expects GM's global automotive EBIT margins (according to Fitch's methodology) to range between 6.0%-6.5% over the next several years, approximately 200 bps higher than Fitch's previous expectations. Fitch expects margins to run at the lower end of that range over the next year, largely driven by a decline in net pricing as the auto market normalizes. However, over the longer term, Fitch expects margins to increase, partly driven by improved profitability on the company's BEVs as those programs scale up.

Strong FCF Generation: Fitch forecasts GM's post-dividend automotive FCF margins (based on Fitch's methodology) to range between 5.5%-6.0% over the next few years, despite headwinds from pricing and vehicle mix. The increasing proportion of the company's sales comprised of lower-margin BEVs will weigh on FCF in the near term.

Fitch expects working capital cash flows to normalize as a source of cash over the next few years as production grows and supply chain-related production volatility subsides. Fitch expects capex as a percentage of revenue in the 6.0%-6.5% range over the next few years, or about $10 billion-plus annually (not including investments into the Ultium Cells JV), due to new product introductions and the continued BEV rollout.

Low Dividends Support FCF: GM's decision to keep its common dividend low while it prioritizes technology investments will support near-term FCF. Although the company raised its dividend in conjunction with the accelerated share repurchase in 2023, Fitch expects dividends to run at roughly $500 million per year in the near term.

Fitch also expects GMF to continue paying dividends to GM, which is another source of cash that Fitch incorporates into its automotive FCF calculation. GMF paid $1.8 billion in dividends to GM in 2023 and $1.35 billion in dividends in the first three quarters of 2024. GM's actual automotive FCF margin in 2023 was 4.6%, according to Fitch's methodology, and was weighed down by the United Auto Workers strike late in the year.

1.0x Automotive Leverage: Fitch expects EBITDA gross leverage to generally run at about 1.0x over the intermediate term. GM's leverage will be partly influenced by dividends from GMF and the company's China JVs, as Fitch includes GMF's dividends and dividends received from unconsolidated JVs to GM's automotive EBITDA (including Cruise) when calculating leverage. GM's actual automotive gross EBITDA leverage at YE 2023 was 1.1x.

BEV Investments: Similar to other auto OEMs, GM has adjusted the pace of its BEV ramp to align production levels with demand. GM remains committed to BEV development, and it continues to have a goal of eliminating tailpipe emissions from new light-duty vehicles in the U.S. by 2035. Previous issues with a supplier of automation equipment for battery module production have been largely resolved, and BEV production has increased substantially over the course of 2024. GM held the second spot in U.S. BEV sales in 3Q24, and it expects to produce and wholesale about 200K BEVs for the full year.

There is business risk associated with the BEV transition, but tightening regulations in many markets continue to shift the industry toward electrification, and GM is well positioned to manage the change. GM could also use BEVs to grow sales in the European market, which could help mitigate the company's relatively concentrated sales footprint.

Product Simplification Strategy: A key pillar of GM's cost reduction plans is its Winning with Simplicity strategy, which aims to reduce complexity and part counts. GM's next generation of full-size trucks and SUVs will reduce trim counts by 35%, selectable options by 60% and buildable combinations by 80%. This will decrease the total number of parts needed by over 1,000. Fitch expects this strategy to significantly increase vehicle unit profitability through part cost savings and operational efficiencies.

Net Charge-off Pressure: Retail delinquencies (60+ days past due) as a percentage of total retail receivables were 0.8% at 3Q24, up from 0.7% a year prior. Annualized net charge-offs were 1.1% in the nine months ended Sept. 30, 2024 (9M24), up from 0.9% in 9M23. Fitch expects GMF's retail credit losses to increase modestly in the near term as consumer credit performance and used-vehicle values normalize but remain below historical averages due to the portfolio's improved credit mix.

Earnings Headwinds: GMF's annualized pre-tax ROAA in 9M24 was 2.2%, down from 2.4% in 9M23 and below the four-year average of 3.2% from 2020-2023. The decline was primarily driven by increased funding costs and a larger provision for credit losses, offset partially by higher interest income given the growth in outstanding finance receivables. Fitch expects that further deterioration of consumer credit performance may drive provisions higher and pose a headwind to earnings in the near term, although lower interest expense given expectations for future rate cuts will be a mitigant.

Management-calculated leverage (net earning assets to tangible equity) was 8.7x at 3Q24, up from 8.3x at YE23. Fitch expects GMF's leverage to remain under management's target of 10.0x.

The company has meaningfully increased its proportion of unsecured funding since transitioning to a full-service captive, equal to 60.9% at 3Q24, up 57.0% at YE23 and above the four-year average of 56.9% from 2020-2023 as well as management's target of 50%. While below the peer average, Fitch believes GMF's proportion of unsecured debt provides enhanced funding flexibility in times of stress, as a larger pool of unencumbered assets could be pledged to secured facilities or securitized to generate additional liquidity.

Solid Liquidity: Fitch views GMF's liquidity profile as solid and sufficient to cover upcoming debt maturities over the next 12-24 months. At 3Q24, available liquidity totalled $34.3 billion, up from $30.9 billion at YE23, consisting of cash, borrowing capacity under its various credit facilities, and unpledged unencumbered assets. Liquidity is further supported by the cash flow generated by the business on an ongoing basis.

GMF's preferred stock rating is two notches below the Long-Term IDR, in accordance with Fitch's "Corporate Hybrids Treatment and Notching Criteria." The preferred stock rating includes two notches for loss severity, reflecting the preferred units' subordination and heightened risk of non-performance relative to other obligations, namely existing secured and unsecured debt. Fitch affords the preferred stock 50% equity credit given the cumulative nature of the dividends and because the preferred stock is perpetual.

Derivation Summary

GM's operating profile is generally in line with its mass-market automotive peers in the 'BBB' rating category. GM has a relatively strong competitive position, with a top five share in most of the markets where it operates. However, it is geographically less diversified than most major global mass-market auto manufacturers, with very little presence in Western Europe and a heavy reliance on North America for much of its profit and FCF generation.

GM's product diversity is relatively high, with Chevrolet and Cadillac serving as its anchor brands, augmented by Buick and GMC. The company manufactures passenger and light commercial vehicles spanning the mass-market and luxury segments. However, luxury sales, represented by Cadillac, remain much lower than those of dedicated luxury manufacturers, such as Mercedes-Benz Group AG (A/Stable), Bayerische Motoren Werke AG or the luxury divisions of global mass-market competitors such as Toyota Motor Corporation (A+/Stable).

GM also has a presence in the North American medium-duty truck market through collaborations with Isuzu Motors Ltd. and Traton SE's Navistar, Inc. subsidiary. GM has one of the more ambitious electrification strategies among the global mass-market manufacturers, with a goal of eliminating tailpipe emissions from new U.S. light-duty vehicles by 2035.

GM's financial profile is relatively strong compared to similar mass-market peers. EBIT, EBITDA and FCF margins have generally been at the higher end of its peer group, driven largely by its relatively strong performance in North America. However, margins could come under some pressure over the intermediate term as the company accelerates its electrification initiatives. Leverage remains relatively low compared to its peer group, with gross EBITDA leverage generally running around 1.0x.

GM has recently begun returning significant levels of cash to shareholders via share repurchases, including a $10 billion ASR that was entered into in late 2023. The company has funded its share repurchases with FCF and existing balance sheet cash. GM also pays a common dividend, although at a significantly lower level than prior to the coronavirus pandemic.

Unsecured debt as a percentage of total debt was 58.7% at 2Q23, up from 6.2% at 2007, and remains above management's target of 50%. Fitch views GMF's access to unsecured funding favorably as it increases the pool of unencumbered assets that could be pledged during periods of stress, providing enhanced funding flexibility.

Fitch believes GMF has sufficient liquidity to address its secured and unsecured debt maturities over the next 12 months. As of June 30, 2023, GMF had $33.0 billion in available liquidity, which included cash and borrowing capacity under its various credit facilities, and unpledged unencumbered assets. Available liquidity is further supported by projected cash flow collections on loans and lease receivables. Approximately $10.6 billion of unsecured debt and $11.1 billion of secured debt matures over the remainder of 2023.

According to Fitch's "Non-Bank Financial Institutions Rating Criteria," a Long-Term IDR of 'BBB' maps to a Short-Term IDR of 'F3' or 'F2'. GMF's Long-Term IDR is driven by shareholder support, and as such, Fitch assigns the higher Short-Term IDR at 'F2' as the propensity to support is typically more certain in the near term.

GMF's CP is equalized with the company's Short-Term IDR.

GMF's unsecured debt rating is equalized with the Long-Term IDR and reflects the proportion of unsecured debt in the capital structure and the expectation for average recovery prospects in a stressed scenario.

GMF's preferred stock rating is two notches below the Long-Term IDR, in accordance with Fitch's "Corporate Hybrids Treatment and Notching Criteria," dated November 2020. The preferred stock rating includes two notches for loss severity, reflecting the preferred units' subordination and heightened risk of non-performance relative to other obligations, namely existing secured and unsecured debt. Fitch affords the preferred stock 50% equity credit given the cumulative nature of the dividends and because the preferred stock is perpetual.

Derivation Summary

GM's operating profile is generally in line with that of mass-market automotive peers in the 'BBB' rating category. GM has a relatively strong competitive position, with a top five share in most of the markets where it operates. However, GM is geographically less diversified than most of the major global mass-market auto manufacturers, with very little presence in Western Europe and a heavy reliance on the North American market for much of its profit and FCF generation.

GM's product diversity is relatively high, with Chevrolet and Cadillac serving as its anchor brands, augmented by several other brands, such as Buick and GMC. The company manufactures a range of passenger and light commercial vehicles spanning the mass-market and luxury segments. However, luxury sales, represented by the Cadillac brand, remain much lower than those of dedicated luxury manufacturers, such as Mercedes-Benz Group AG or Bayerische Motoren Werke AG, or even the luxury divisions of certain global mass-market competitors, such as Toyota Motor Corporation.

In addition to light vehicles, GM also has a presence in the North American medium-duty truck market through collaborations with Isuzu Motors Ltd. and Traton SE's Navistar, Inc. subsidiary. GM has one of the more ambitious electrification strategies among the global mass-market manufacturers, with a goal of phasing out tailpipe emissions from new light-duty vehicles in the U.S. by 2035.

GM's financial profile is relatively strong compared to similar mass-market peers. EBIT, EBITDA and FCF margins have generally been at the higher end of its peer group, driven largely by its relatively strong performance in North America, although margins could come under some pressure over the intermediate term as the company accelerates its electrification initiatives.

Leverage remains relatively low compared to its peer group, with gross EBITDA generally running near 1.0x. Prior to the coronavirus pandemic, capital deployment was more shareholder focused than at some of its global peers, but the company temporarily suspended its common dividends and share repurchases during the pandemic. Although GM has since reinstated dividends and share repurchases, both have been running at lower levels than before the pandemic.

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-28 July 2022: Fitch Ratings has assigned a 'BBB-' rating to General Motors Company's (GM) proposed issuance of senior unsecured green bonds. GM's Long-Term Issuer Default Rating (IDR) is 'BBB-'. The Rating Outlook is Positive.

GM's ratings and Outlook reflect improvements in the company's profitability and FCF that Fitch expects will lead to credit metrics that are in line with its positive rating sensitivities over the intermediate term. Although Fitch expects supply chain and inflationary pressures to lead to some moderation in margins in 2022 vs. the levels seen in 2021, Fitch nonetheless expects GM's profitability to remain solid over the intermediate term.

Key Rating Drivers

Proposed Green Bonds: GM is issuing its green bonds following the establishment of a Sustainability Finance Framework, which is a voluntary framework for its issuance of green, social and sustainability financings. GM intends to allocate an amount equal to the net proceeds from the bond offering to finance or refinance eligible projects related to clean transportation solutions, as outlined in the bonds' prospectus supplement. Categories of eligible projects will include research and development, manufacturing facilities and operations.

GM will publish an annual Sustainable Financing Report on its website until it has fully allocated an amount equal to the net proceeds of the offering. It has also retained an outside consultant that has provided a second party opinion on the environmental and social benefits of GM's sustainability framework. In addition, the consultant has provided an opinion on the framework's alignment with Green Bond Principles, Social Bond Principles, Sustainability Bond Guidelines and Green Loan Principles.

All of these factors will weigh on profitability and FCF generation over the next several years.

Despite these headwinds, vehicle demand remains strong, and it has outpaced supply in the major global regions for the past two years. Fitch expects GM will continue to see a relatively strong pricing environment, which will mitigate some of the inflationary pressure on the company's margins in the near term. Pent-up demand could also soften the effects of a weakening macroeconomic environment on the company's credit profile. Fitch expects GM's overall profitability to remain solid, but the combination of inflation and technology-related investment spending will result in some margin dilution over the intermediate term.

(GMF) to GM, which Fitch incorporates in its automotive FCF calculation, could also be lower in the near term, consistent with GM's expectations for lower earnings at the unit in 2022.

Fitch expects GM's automotive FCF margins to run in the low-single-digit range over the next couple of years. Fitch expects FCF margins to be weighed down by substantial capex tied to the EV rollout. Future FCF growth will depend, in part, on whether the company decides to reinstate common dividends. Key Rating Drivers

Rating Concerns: Near-term rating concerns include ongoing production difficulties stemming from supply-chain related challenges and the coronavirus pandemic, elevated commodity and logistics costs, rising interest rates, and the impact of inflation on consumer confidence. Other concerns include tightening emissions regulations in various global regions, as well as substantial investments that the company is making in both electrification and vehicle autonomy. All of these factors will weigh on profitability and FCF generation over the next several years.

Despite these headwinds, vehicle demand remains strong, and it has outpaced supply in the major global regions for the past two years. As such, Fitch expects GM will continue to see a relatively strong pricing environment, which will help to mitigate some of the inflationary pressure on the company's margins. That said, Fitch expects the combination of inflation and technology-related investment spending to result in some margin dilution over the intermediate term, although overall profitability is likely to remain solid.

Concentrated Operational Footprint: GM's ratings incorporate its relatively concentrated operational footprint. Compared with many of its global mass-market competitors, automotive profitability and FCF are highly dependent on the company's performance in the North American and Chinese markets, and they are particularly sensitive to demand for full-size pickups and SUVs in North America. This results in a heightened diversification risk in the company's business profile. However, GM's willingness to exit underperforming markets over the past several years has also contributed to its solid margin and FCF performance.

EV Investments: GM has announced an ambitious electrification strategy, with a plan to offer 30 electric vehicles (EVs) globally by 2025 and a goal of phasing out tailpipe emissions from new light-duty vehicles by 2035. GM's EVs will be largely based on its flexible Ultium platform, with the first vehicle produced on that platform, the GMC Hummer EV, launched in late 2021. Spending on the EV plan will result in elevated capex over the next several years, which will weigh on intermediate-term FCF. Although there is business risk associated with the EV transition, tightening emissions regulations in many global regions are moving the industry toward electrification, and GM appears to be well positioned to manage this shift.

GM's majority-owned Cruise subsidiary is an industry leader in autonomous vehicle (AV) technology, currently offering fully autonomous rides to the public in San Francisco. In March 2022, GM agreed to repurchase a stake held by the SoftBank Vision Fund (AIV M2) L.P. for $2.1 billion. Simultaneously, GM agreed to invest a further $1.35 billion in Cruise, growing its stake to about 80%.

Solid FCF: Fitch expects GM's automotive FCF (according to Fitch's methodology) to be solid over the intermediate term, despite some near-term headwinds from elevated commodity and logistics costs, as well as increased capex, as the company prepares for the rollout of more Ultium-based EVs over the next several years. FCF will be supported in the near term by GM's decision to hold off on reinstating its dividend while it prioritizes investments. Fitch expects dividends from GMF to GM, which Fitch incorporates in its automotive FCF calculation, could also be lower in the near term, consistent with GM's expectations for lower earnings at the unit in 2022.

Fitch expects GM's automotive FCF margins to run in the low-single-digit range over the next couple of years. Fitch expects FCF margins to be weighed down by substantial capex tied to the EV rollout. Future FCF growth will depend, in part, on whether the company decides to reinstate common dividends. However, if GM chooses to reinstate a dividend at some point, Fitch expects it would be sized to allow the company to generate healthy post-dividend FCF.

Low Leverage: Fitch expects GM's automotive EBITDA leverage (according to Fitch's methodology) to remain relatively low and in-line with pre-pandemic levels. This is despite the company carrying roughly $2 billion more debt today than it did prior to the pandemic. Based on its methodology, Fitch includes dividends from GMF in EBITDA when calculating GM's automotive leverage, and increased dividends from GMF have helped to keep GM's leverage down. In 2021, GMF paid $3.5 billion in dividends to GM, and Fitch has incorporated this into its leverage calculations.

Fitch expects EBITDA gross leverage (according to Fitch's methodology) to remain in the low-1.0x range over the intermediate term. Actual EBITDA leverage at YE 2021 was 1.2x.

Key Rating Drivers

Rating Rationale: GM's ratings continue to reflect the auto manufacturer's strong liquidity position, solid profitability and positive FCF-generating capability. The ratings also reflect the company's relatively concentrated operational footprint. Compared with most of its global mass-market competitors, automotive FCF is highly dependent on the performance of the North American and Chinese markets, and it is particularly sensitive to demand for full-size pickups and SUVs in North America. This results in a heightened diversification risk in the company's business profile. However, its willingness to exit less-profitable markets, such as Western Europe, has also contributed to its relatively solid margin and FCF performance over the past several years.

GM's overall performance in 2020 was stronger than Fitch had expected, largely due to better-than-expected demand conditions in North America and China. The company also flexed its costs down during the worst period of the pandemic, which helped to limit cash burn. It maintained a very strong liquidity position through the year, although this was partly due to the increase in long-term debt. FCF was also positive in 2020, which was significantly better than Fitch's expectation of a substantial cash burn for the year.

Advanced Technology Investments: GM has announced one of the auto industry's most ambitious electrification strategies, with plans to offer 30 electric vehicles (EVs) by 2025 and a goal of phasing out production of internal combustion engines (ICEs) by 2035. GM's EVs will be largely based on its new Ultium platform, with the first vehicles on that platform launching for the 2022 model year. Spending on the EV plan will result in elevated capex over the next several years, which will weigh on intermediate-term FCF. Although there is business risk associated with the EV transition, tightening emissions regulations in many global markets are moving the industry toward electrification, and GM appears to be well positioned to manage this shift.

GM's Cruise subsidiary has also positioned itself as an industry leader in autonomous vehicle (AV) technology, although it is not clear when it will begin commercial operations. The unit has attracted substantial third-party funding from a range of investors, including the SoftBank Vision Fund, Honda, Microsoft, Walmart and others, and the most recent funding round valued Cruise at $30 billion. Fitch includes Cruise in its calculation of automotive metrics, and in the near term, AV development costs will weigh on GM's margins and FCF. That said, a substantial portion of the development cost has been covered by the third-party investments.

Solid FCF Expected: Fitch expects GM's automotive FCF to be pressured in 2021 by elevated capex, as the company catches up on projects deferred from 2020 and prepares for the rollout of the first Ultium-based EVs over the next several years. FCF in 2021 will also be affected by the semiconductor shortage, which GM estimates will have a $1.5 billion-$2.5 billion cash impact for the year. Fitch expects capex to remain somewhat elevated in 2022 and 2023 as well, but not at the $9.0 billion-$10.0 billion level expected in 2021. Despite the higher capex, Fitch expects GM to generate positive FCF in 2021 and beyond.

Fitch expects GM's automotive FCF margins, according to Fitch's calculations, to run below 1% in 2021 as a result of the increased capex and semiconductor shortage. However, Fitch expects the FCF margin to rise above 1.5% in 2022 and potentially toward 2.5% in 2023 as capex moderates and operating cash flow increases. FCF will be affected over the next several years by any decision the company might make to reinstate its common dividend, which Fitch would include in its FCF calculation. That said, Fitch expects the company would set any dividend at a level that would allow it to continue generating a healthy level of FCF.

Low Leverage Expected: Fitch expects GM's leverage to remain relatively low for the next couple years but somewhat elevated compared with pre-pandemic levels, largely due to a net $3.5 billion increase in senior unsecured notes that the company issued in 2020. In May 2020, GM issued $4.0 billion in new notes, but it repaid $500 million in notes at maturity in August 2020. Notably, before YE20, GM had sufficient cash to repay all the revolver borrowings it made early in the pandemic.

Fitch expects EBITDA gross leverage (according to Fitch's methodology) to be near the mid-1.0x range by YE21 and to decline to the low-1.0x range by YE22. Fitch expects EBITDA leverage to remain in the low-1.0x range over the longer term, essentially in line with the pre-pandemic level. Fitch expects FFO leverage to run slightly below 2.0x by YE21 and to decline toward the mid-1.0x range by YE22, also in line with the pre-pandemic level. Actual leverage in 2020 rose a bit due to weak market conditions and higher debt, but it still remained relatively low. EBITDA leverage at YE20 was 1.5x, while FFO leverage was 1.9x.

GMF

The affirmation of GMF's ratings and maintenance of the Stable Rating Outlook are driven by the affirmation and the maintenance of the Stable Rating Outlook for GM. GMF's ratings and Rating Outlook are linked to those of GM as Fitch considers GMF a core subsidiary of GM.

This view is supported by the actual and potential support provided by GM, strong financial and operational linkages between the firms and the formal support agreement between GM and GMF. The ratings also reflect GMF's seasoned management team, diversified funding profile, consistent operating performance and adequate liquidity.

Despite the impact of the coronavirus pandemic, GMF's credit performance improved in 2020 as it benefitted from unprecedented government support to consumers and widespread forbearance programs provided by consumer lenders to borrowers. Additionally, GMF benefitted from higher used vehicle prices, resulting in elevated gains on lease terminations as well as higher recoveries on defaulted loans. GMF's credit performance has also improved in recent years due to the portfolio shift toward prime assets, a trend which continued in 2020, as subprime loans accounted for 20.6% of GMF's retail loan portfolio at YE20, down from 23.6% at YE19. Fitch expects GMF's credit metrics to weaken modestly in 2021 as the effects of the stimulus fade.

GMF's profitability, as measured by pretax return on average assets (ROAA), improved to 2.4% in 2020 from 1.9% in 2019, as pretax earnings benefited from lower borrowing costs, coupled with higher recovery rates and gains on lease terminations, given the strong used vehicle price environment in the second half of the year. Similar to its credit metrics, Fitch expects GMF's profitability to deteriorate modestly in 2021 as economic conditions begin to normalize, although this could be mitigated by continued strength in used vehicle prices.

Fitch believes that GMF has sufficient liquidity to address its 2021 debt maturities (both secured and unsecured debt) with $27.6 billion in available liquidity (which includes cash and borrowing capacity under its unencumbered assets and various credit facilities) as of Dec. 31, 2020. Available liquidity is further supported by projected cash flow collections on loans and lease receivables. 2021 debt maturities include $14.7 billion of unsecured debt maturities and $21 billion of secured debt maturities.

GMF's funding diversification has steadily improved in recent years, in Fitch's view, with a greater mix of unsecured debt. Unsecured debt as a percentage of total debt increased to 57.2% at YE20, from 55.4% at YE19 and 53.3% at YE18. Fitch views GMF's unsecured debt funding favorably as it increases the pool of unencumbered assets that could be pledged during periods of stress, providing enhanced funding flexibility.

Leverage, as measured by debt to tangible equity, improved slightly, to 8.2x at YE20 from 8.3x at YE19, when affording GMF's outstanding preferred stock 50% equity credit. During 2020, GMF paid an $800 million dividend to its parent, up from the $400 million dividend paid in 2019 and the $375 million dividend paid in 2018. The dividend increase was supported by growth in pretax earnings for the year.

Management-calculated leverage (earning assets to tangible equity) also decreased, to 8.0x at YE20 from 8.3x at YE19. Still, the ratio remains well below the 11.5x ceiling permitted by the support agreement between GM and GMF, as well as the 10.0x management-stated target.

Once net earning assets exceed $100 billion ($98.2 billion at YE20), the support agreement permits leverage (earning assets to tangible equity) to increase to 12.0x, which Fitch views as high. Fitch expects GMF to remain under the management-stated target of 10.0x near term, given the ongoing economic uncertainties caused by the pandemic. Further increases in GMF's leverage without a commensurate improvement in portfolio risk would be viewed as a credit negative by Fitch.

Derivation Summary

GM's operating profile is generally in line with that of mass-market automotive peers at a similar rating level, such as Volkswagen AG, Stellantis N.V. and Ford Motor Company. GM has a relatively strong competitive position, with a top five share in most of the markets where it operates. However, GM is geographically less diversified than most of the major global mass-market auto manufacturers, with virtually no presence in Western Europe and a heavy reliance on the North American and Chinese markets for nearly all its profit and FCF generation.

GM's product diversity is relatively high, with Chevrolet and Cadillac serving as its anchor brands, augmented by several smaller regional brands. The company manufactures a range of passenger and light commercial vehicles spanning the mass-market and luxury segments. However, luxury sales, represented by the Cadillac brand, remain much lower than those of dedicated luxury manufacturers, such as Daimler AG or Bayerische Motoren Werke AG, or even the luxury divisions of certain global mass-market competitors, such as Toyota Motor Corporation. GM also has a presence in the North American medium-duty truck market through collaborations with Isuzu Motors Ltd. and Navistar International Corporation. GM also has one of the most ambitious electrification strategies among the global mass-market manufacturers, with a goal of offering only zero-emission vehicles by 2035.

GM's financial profile is relatively strong compared to similar mass-market peers. EBIT, EBITDA and FCF margins (excluding restructuring) have generally been at the higher end of its peer group, driven largely by its relatively strong performance in North America, although margins could come under some pressure over the intermediate term as the company moves forward with its electrification initiatives.

Leverage remains relatively low compared to its peer group, with debt/EBITDA generally running near 1.0x in a normalized macro environment. Prior to the coronavirus pandemic, capital deployment was more shareholder focused than at some of its global peers, but it did suspend its dividend and share repurchases during the pandemic, and Fitch expects it will be judicious in reinstating any of these payments as the pandemic wanes.

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-07 May 2020: LEAD PARAGRAPH

Fitch Ratings has assigned a rating of 'BBB-' to General Motors Company's (GM) proposed issuance of senior unsecured notes in a benchmark, multi-tranche offering. Proceeds from the notes will be used for general corporate purposes.

GM's Long-Term Issuer Default Rating (IDR) is 'BBB-', and its Rating Outlook is Stable.

Key Rating Drivers

Note Proceeds Provide Additional Liquidity: Proceeds from the proposed notes will bolster GM's liquidity as the company contends with the operational challenges caused by the coronavirus pandemic, although over the longer term, gross leverage will be slightly higher than Fitch's previous expectations. GM's operations outside China are currently shut down, but the company is hoping to restart its North American manufacturing operations in mid-May 2020. However, Fitch expects global macro conditions will remain relatively weak through most of 2021, lessening the likelihood of a full return to pre-coronavirus production levels until at least 2022.

Recent Downgrade: The downgrade of GM's IDR to 'BBB-' from 'BBB' was based on Fitch's expectation that the company's credit profile will remain weak relative to its prior negative rating sensitivities for a prolonged period due to a likely extended period of macroeconomic weakness stemming from the coronavirus pandemic. The company's more concentrated operations, with its automotive FCF completely dependent on the North American and Chinese auto markets, could also pose some risk in the future, although it has resulted in less cash burn in the current environment.

The Stable Outlook reflects Fitch's expectations that the company will be able to maintain an investment-grade credit profile once the worst of the pandemic has passed. At the 'BBB-' level the company has some headroom in its credit profile to withstand further negative developments.

GM's ratings also reflect the auto manufacturer's strong liquidity position, solid pre-pandemic profitability, and positive FCF generating capability in a normalized operating environment. Fitch expects the company will burn a substantial amount of cash in 2020 as a result of pandemic-related plant shutdowns, but the level of cash burn will be mitigated by the company's smaller global footprint, with its lack of European operations a particular benefit in the current environment.

The company also successfully weathered a six-week shutdown of its North American facilities as a result of the UAW strike in late 2019 that essentially provided the company with a playbook that it has been using during the current shutdown.

Heaviest Cash Outflows Largely Complete: GM said on its first quarter 2020 earnings call that the cash outflow in the second quarter would be $7 billion to $9 billion assuming global production is down 60% to 70% in the period. Once it cycles past the working capital-related outflows, cash burn will be considerably lower, and the company should start generating cash once production restarts.

Fitch estimates that FCF for the full year will be an outflow of a little less than $3.0 billion. Incorporated in this figure is Fitch's estimate of capex running at roughly $5.0 billion for the year. GM recently suspended its common dividend, and Fitch assumes that dividends will remain suspended through 2020.

Derivation Summary

GM's operating profile is generally in line with mass-market automotive peers at a similar rating level, such as Volkswagen AG, Fiat Chrysler Automobiles N.V. and Ford Motor Company. GM has a relatively strong competitive position, with a top-five share in most of the markets where it operates. The company's product lineup has also improved significantly in the post-recession period, which has translated into higher revenue and profit per vehicle, particularly in North America.

Traditionally, GM has been one of the most globally diversified auto manufacturers, with operations in nearly every major market. However, in recent years the company has aggressively restructured its business to improve profitability. This has included divesting its European operations, ending manufacturing in Australia, ending Chevrolet sales in India, and scaling back or ending operations in several other countries. These actions have resulted in a smaller company that is significantly more profitable but much less globally diversified than it was a few years ago.

GM's product diversity is relatively high, with Chevrolet and Cadillac serving as its global anchor brands, augmented by a host of smaller regional brands. The company manufactures a wide range of passenger and light commercial vehicles spanning the mass-market and luxury segments. However, luxury sales, represented by the Cadillac brand, remain much lower than those of dedicated luxury manufacturers or even the luxury divisions of certain global mass-market competitors, such as Toyota Motor Corporation. GM has also re-entered the medium-duty truck market in North America through collaborations with Isuzu Motors Ltd. and Navistar International Corporation.

GM's financial profile is relatively strong compared to similar mass-market peers, such as Ford. EBIT and EBITDA margins have recently been at the higher end of its peer group, driven by GM's strong performance in North America, and Fitch expects the company's restructuring actions to support margins. Post-dividend FCF margins have also been at the higher end of the peer set and will be enhanced by lower capital spending needs.

Leverage remains low relative to its peer group, with debt/EBITDA generally running slightly around 1x in a normalized macro environment. However, capital deployment in recent years has been more shareholder focused than some of its global peers, although the company recently suspended its dividend and share repurchases in the wake of the coronavirus pandemic.

Key Assumptions

Fitch's Key Assumptions Within Our Rating Case for the Issuer

--GM's North American operations restart in the latter half of May 2020.

--Automotive net revenue declines by about 20% on a strike-adjusted basis in 2020 due to weak global market conditions.

--The company begins to repay its revolver borrowings with excess cash once operating conditions normalize.

--The common dividend is suspended until the company repays most of its revolver borrowings.

--Capex in 2020 runs at roughly $5.0 billion to conserve cash, rising back toward $7.0 billion in the following years.

--Automotive FCF burn in 2020 is a little less than $3.0 billion for the full year, but it turns positive in the following years, with FCF margins running at 1.0%-1.5%.

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-07 May 2020: Fitch Ratings has downgraded the Long-Term Issuer Default Ratings (IDRs) of General Motors Company (GM) and its subsidiary General Motors Financial Company, Inc. (GMF) to 'BBB-' from 'BBB'. Fitch has also downgraded the senior unsecured debt ratings of GM and GMF to 'BBB-' from 'BBB' and downgraded GMF's short-term ratings to 'F3' from 'F2'.

Key Rating Drivers

Ratings Downgrade: The downgrade of GM's IDR to 'BBB-' from 'BBB' is based on Fitch's expectation that the company's credit profile will remain weak relative to its prior negative rating sensitivities for a prolonged period due to a likely extended period of macroeconomic weakness stemming from the coronavirus pandemic. Fitch expects the macro environment to remain weak through the rest of 2020 and much of 2021, which will likely keep sales volumes well below the 2019 level into much of 2022. The company's more concentrated operations, with its automotive FCF completely dependent on the North American and Chinese auto markets, could also pose some risk in the future, although it has resulted in less cash burn in the current environment.

The Stable Outlook reflects Fitch's expectations that the company will be able to maintain an investment-grade credit profile once the worst of the pandemic has passed. At the 'BBB-' level the company has some headroom in its credit profile to withstand further negative developments.

GM's ratings also reflect the auto manufacturer's strong liquidity position, solid pre-pandemic profitability, and positive FCF generating capability in a normalized operating environment. Fitch expects the company will burn a substantial amount of cash in 2020 as a result of pandemic-related plant shutdowns, but the level of cash burn will be mitigated by the company's smaller global footprint, with its lack of European operations a particular benefit in the current environment.

The company also successfully weathered a six-week shutdown of its North American facilities as a result of the UAW strike in late 2019 that essentially provided the company with a playbook that it has been using during the current shutdown.

Liquidity Preservation Actions: In March 2020, GM drew a total of $15.9 billion on its various revolvers. The company later suspended its regular dividend, which Fitch estimates would have totaled $500 million to $600 million per quarter, to further conserve cash. The company also took a number of other austerity measures, including a zero-based budgeting program, employee furloughs and compensation deferrals. Fitch expects GM to look to begin repaying its revolver borrowings once its plants in North America are largely up and running and the company begins generating positive FCF.

In April 2020, GM amended its $4.0 billion revolving credit facility, which is fully drawn, shifting the maturity of $3.6 billion of the commitment to April 2022 from April 2021. The company also has a $10.5 billion revolver that matures in 2023 and a $3.0 billion supplemental revolver that matures 2022 that steps down to $2.0 billion in July 2020.

As part of the amendment of the $4.0 billion facility, GM agreed to suspend share repurchases while there are outstanding borrowings on the credit facilities (excluding the supplemental revolver), and it agreed to suspend paying common dividends as long as total borrowings on the revolvers are higher than $5.0 billion (again, excluding the supplemental revolver).

Heaviest Cash Outflows Largely Complete: It is typical for North American auto manufacturers to burn substantial levels of cash when their operations are disrupted due to their working capital position. This is because the manufacturers generally receive cash from revenue shortly after vehicles are released from the plants, but they typically pay their suppliers on a roughly 45-day lag.

GM said on its first quarter 2020 earnings call that the cash outflow in the second quarter would be $7 billion to $9 billion assuming global production is down 60% to 70% in the period. Once it cycles past the working capital-related outflows, cash burn will be considerably lower, and the company should start generating cash once production restarts.

Fitch estimates that FCF for the full year will be an outflow of a little less than $3.0 billion, although this is based on the company's North American plants restarting in the latter half of May 2020. Also incorporated in this figure is Fitch's estimate of capex running at roughly $5.0 billion for the year. GM recently suspended its common dividend, and Fitch assumes that dividends will remain suspended through 2020.

Fitch's forecasts do not consider a potential balance sheet unwind at GMF with sales volumes down significantly. In a period of much lower sales, GMF could become more cash generative, which could be an additional source of cash for GM. However, much would depend on the ongoing performance of GMF's underlying asset portfolio in a recessionary environment.

Transformation Plans: In 2019, GM completed the transformation plans it had initiated in the fourth quarter of 2018. These largely consisted of plant rationalizations and salaried headcount reductions in North America and had been undertaken to better prepare the company for an eventual downturn.

As of year-end 2019, the company had recorded cumulative charges of $3.1 billion related to the plans, including $1.8 billion in 2019. Cash costs associated with transformation totaled about $1.1 billion in 2019. As of Dec. 31, 2019, the company expected remaining cash flows associated with the program to total about $400 million in 2020.

GM had expected run-rate cost savings from the transformation plan to total between $4.0 billion and $4.5 billion by year-end 2020. Given that elements of the plan are completed, Fitch expects the transformation to help support GM's margins and FCF through the current period of macroeconomic stress.

UAW Strike: In the late third and early fourth quarters of 2019, the UAW engaged in a strike at GM's U.S. operations that eventually resulted in a shutdown of the company's plants for about six weeks. GM estimates the strike reduced the company's net revenue by about $11 billion and its automotive FCF by about $5.4 billion in 2019. Overall, the company managed through the strike relatively well, ending the year close to its automotive cash target.

The strike did leave dealer inventories of certain vehicles a little lower than usual, especially for full-size pickups. With industry pickup sales holding up better than those of other vehicles over the past couple of months, GM's dealer inventories could become leaner, which could bode well for the company's production if it is able meet demand for these vehicles later in the year.

KEY RATING DRIVERS - General Motors Financial Company, Inc.

IDRs and Senior Debt: The downgrade of GMF's ratings and maintenance of the Stable Outlook are driven by the downgrade and the maintenance of the Stable Outlook for General Motors. GMF's ratings and Outlook are linked to those of GM as Fitch considers GMF a core subsidiary of GM.

This view is supported by the actual and potential support provided by GM, strong financial and operational linkages between the firms and the formal support agreement between GM and GMF. The ratings also reflect GMF's seasoned management team, diversified funding profile, consistent operating performance and adequate liquidity.

GMF's credit performance had improved modestly in recent years given the portfolio shift toward prime assets and an improvement in recovery rates. However, the magnitude and duration of the global economic impact related to the COVID-19 pandemic is highly uncertain and will likely result in a sharp increase in credit losses in coming months.

GMF recently announced options for existing customers affected by COVID-19 to defer payments on their loans. Through April 2020, payment deferrals accounted for about 3.5% of GMF's retail finance receivables outstanding. Fitch believes forbearance programs should help mitigate defaults for those borrowers that are temporarily displaced, but they could also delay losses for those that will remain unemployed for an extended period and distort traditional delinquency to charge-off flow patterns.

The shutdown of used vehicle auctions across the country and a more uncertain economic environment will pressure used vehicle prices for some time, resulting in lower recoveries on defaulted loans and declines in lease residual values, which, when combined with higher loss rates, will pressure GMF's earnings over the near term. At end-1Q20, GMF's lease portfolio represented 42.9% of earning assets, down modestly from 44.3% a year before.

Likewise, a prolonged decline in new auto sales would result in a reduction in new loan originations and a contraction of GMF's loan portfolio. While a decline in loan originations would be supportive of GMF's liquidity position, it would also pressure near-term earnings. GMF's earning assets declined $1.1 billion in 1Q20, or 1.2%, from 1Q19 and are likely to decline further in the near term due to lower auto sales at GM.

Fitch believes that GMF has sufficient liquidity to address its 2020 total debt maturities (both secured and unsecured debt) as of March 31, 2020. Liquidity included cash, available capacity on credit facilities, and projected cash flow collections on loans and lease receivables.

GMF's funding profile and diversification has steadily improved in recent years with greater unsecured debt issuance. Unsecured debt as a percentage of total debt decreased to 52.0% at 1Q20, from 54.8% at 1Q19, which was driven by increased borrowings on secured facilities in 1Q20 to raise additional liquidity. Fitch views GMF's unsecured debt funding favorably as it improves the firm's funding flexibility in times of stress.

Leverage, measured as debt to tangible equity, was 10.3x at end-1Q20, up from 9.2x a year before when affording GMF's preferred stock 50% equity credit. During 1Q20 GMF paid a $400 million dividend to the parent and increased borrowings on committed facilities to bolster its cash position in response to COVID-19.

The management-calculated leverage ratio (earning assets to tangible equity) increased to 9.3x in 1Q20, from 8.8x a year before, which was below the 11.5x ceiling permitted by the support agreement between GM and GMF. Fitch expects leverage to remain high over the near term as GMF maintains higher cash balances for liquidity purposes and profitability declines, which may be offset by portfolio contraction.

Once net earning assets exceed $100 billion, the support agreement permits leverage to increase to 12.0x, which Fitch views as high compared with peers, most of which also have a lower-risk portfolio composition. GMF will likely remain under this amount in the near term due to expected declines in GM sales resulting from COVID-19. Further increases in GMF's leverage without a commensurate improvement in portfolio risk would be viewed as credit negative by Fitch.

GMF's unsecured debt ratings are equalized with the Long-Term IDR and reflect the proportion of unsecured debt in the capital structure and the expectation for average recovery prospects in a stress scenario.

The downgrade of GMF's Short-Term IDR to 'F3' from 'F2' reflects the rating actions on GMF and the rating linkage between the Short-Term IDR and the Long-Term IDR.

GMF's commercial paper rating remains equalized with the company's Short-Term IDR, reflecting Fitch's view that GMF is a core subsidiary of GM.

GMF Canada's Long-Term IDR and senior unsecured debt ratings are directly linked to GMF's ratings as GMF has an unconditional guarantee on GMF Canada's unsecured debt.

Other Hybrid Securities: GMF's preferred stock is rated two notches lower than GMF's Long-Term IDR in accordance with Fitch's 'Corporate Hybrids Treatment and Notching Criteria', dated Nov. 11, 2019. The preferred stock rating includes two notches for loss severity, reflecting the preferred units' subordination and heightened risk of non-performance relative to other obligations, namely existing secured and unsecured debt.

Fitch affords the preferred stock 50% equity credit given the cumulative nature of the dividends and because the preferred stock is perpetual.

Derivation Summary

GM's operating profile is generally in line with mass-market automotive peers at a similar rating level, such as Volkswagen AG, Fiat Chrysler Automobiles N.V. and Ford Motor Company. GM has a relatively strong competitive position, with a top-five share in most of the markets where it operates. The company's product lineup has also improved significantly in the post-recession period, which has translated into higher revenue and profit per vehicle, particularly in North America.

Traditionally, GM has been one of the most globally diversified auto manufacturers, with operations in nearly every major market. However, in recent years the company has aggressively restructured its business to improve profitability. This has included divesting its European operations, ending manufacturing in Australia, ending Chevrolet sales in India, and scaling back or ending operations in several other countries. These actions have resulted in a smaller company that is significantly more profitable but much less globally diversified than it was a few years ago.

GM's product diversity is relatively high, with Chevrolet and Cadillac serving as its global anchor brands, augmented by a host of smaller regional brands. The company manufactures a wide range of passenger and light commercial vehicles spanning the mass-market and luxury segments. However, luxury sales, represented by the Cadillac brand, remain much lower than those of dedicated luxury manufacturers or even the luxury divisions of certain global mass-market competitors, such as Toyota Motor Corporation. GM has also re-entered the medium-duty truck market in North America through collaborations with Isuzu Motors Ltd. and Navistar International Corporation.

GM's financial profile is relatively strong compared to similar mass-market peers, such as Ford. EBIT and EBITDA margins have recently been at the higher end of its peer group, driven by GM's strong performance in North America, and Fitch expects the company's restructuring actions to support margins. Post-dividend FCF margins have also been at the higher end of the peer set and will be enhanced by lower capital spending needs.

Leverage remains low relative to its peer group, with debt/EBITDA generally running slightly around 1x in a normalized macro environment. However, capital deployment in recent years has been more shareholder focused than some of its global peers, although the company recently suspended its dividend and share repurchases in the wake of the coronavirus pandemic.

Key Assumptions

Fitch's Key Assumptions Within Our Rating Case for the Issuer

--GM's North American operations restart in the latter half of May 2020.

--Automotive net revenue declines by about 20% on a strike-adjusted basis in 2020 due to weak global market conditions.

--The company begins to repay its revolver borrowings with excess cash once operating conditions normalize.

--The common dividend is suspended until the company repays most of its revolver borrowings.

--Capex in 2020 runs at roughly $5.0 billion to conserve cash, rising back toward $7.0 billion in the following years.

--Automotive FCF burn in 2020 is a little less than $3.0 billion for the full year, but it turns positive in the following years, with FCF margins running at 1.0%-1.5%.

Liquidity and Debt Structure

Strong Liquidity Position: GM's strong automotive liquidity position is a key consideration in its ratings. Fitch expects GM's automotive liquidity to provide it with substantial financial flexibility as it navigates the global downturn caused by the coronavirus pandemic. GM had over $34 billion in automotive cash and marketable securities as of March 31, 2020 (including Cruise cash but excluding Fitch's adjustments for not readily available cash), after drawing $15.9 billion on its revolving credit facilities.

Under Fitch's Corporate Rating Criteria, when analyzing a corporate issuer with a captive finance subsidiary, the agency calculates an appropriate target debt-to-equity ratio for the finance subsidiary based on its asset quality, funding and liquidity. If the finance subsidiary's target debt-to-equity ratio, based on Fitch's calculations, is lower than the actual ratio, Fitch assumes that the parent injects additional equity into the finance subsidiary to bring the debt-to-equity ratio down to the appropriate target level.

Fitch then considers the effect of this equity injection in its analysis of the parent's credit profile. Fitch has calculated a target debt-to-equity ratio of 4.0x for GMF, below the actual ratio of 8.3x at Dec. 31, 2019. As a result of its analysis, Fitch has assumed that GM makes a $9.3 billion equity injection into GMF, funded with available cash, to bring GMF's debt-to-equity ratio down to 4.0x. The resulting adjustment reduces Fitch's calculation of GM's readily available automotive cash, but the company's metrics remain supportive of its 'BBB' IDR.

In addition to the captive-finance adjustment, according to its criteria, Fitch has treated an additional $4.0 billion of GM's automotive cash as "not readily available" for purposes of calculating net metrics. This is based on Fitch's estimate of the amount of cash needed to cover typical seasonality in GM's automotive business. However, even after excluding the amounts noted above from its liquidity calculations, Fitch views GM's automotive liquidity position as strong.

Debt Structure: GM's debt consists of about $15.9 billion in revolver borrowings, $12.6 billion in senior unsecured notes and about $2.0 billion of other debt. Fitch also applies 50% equity credit to the $900 million in Cruise preferred stock issued to SoftBank Group, treating $450 million of the preferred stock as debt.
