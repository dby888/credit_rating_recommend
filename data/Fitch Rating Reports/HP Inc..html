Fitch Rates HP Inc.'s Senior Unsecured Debt Offering 'BBB+'

Fitch Ratings-Toronto-14 April 2025: Fitch Ratings has assigned a 'BBB+' rating to HP Inc.'s new unsecured issuances. The company will use the offering's net proceeds for general corporate purposes which may include refinancing of debt, including the $1.15 billion of unsecured notes maturing June 17, 2025, at maturity. The transaction is near leverage neutral with Fitch's forecast 2025 EBITDA leverage unchanged at 2.1x.

Key Rating Drivers

Mature Segments with Strong FCF: HP's Print segment generates about one-third of revenues and has a mature profile with some revenue growth fluctuations that exceed economic trends. Longer term, Fitch anticipates flat to modest annual contractions within this segment, with sustained above-GDP growth requiring M&A. Growth potential in the segment is challenged, but its higher relative operating margin results in strong contributions to HP's FCF.

HP's Personal Systems, where HP has a top three global position, provides stable revenues with upside from secular trends such as increased artificial intelligence requirements and video conferencing from hybrid work environment trends. An aging install base and Windows refresh cycle that has been delayed to date compared to previous cycles, as well as HP's inventory investments in 1Q25, should benefit 2H25 FCF. Within these segments, HP's scale and market penetration has also positioned it to take advantage of several opportunities with growth and margin prospects above its core portfolio, such as AI PCs and Hybrid Systems.

Low Leverage Headroom: HP's EBITDA leverage for fiscal year end (Oct. 31, 2024) was 2.1x, roughly in line with Fitch's previously forecast 2.0x and downgrade sensitivity of 2.0x. Fitch forecasts fiscal 2025 EBITDA leverage to be at 2.1x with HP maintaining leverage at the 2.0x downgrade sensitivity thorough the remainder of the forecast.

Fitch has not taken a rating action as the agency does not forecast EBITDA leverage to be sustained above 2x. HP also has a track record of maintaining a conservative capital structure and FCF generation, which Fitch forecasts after common dividends to be more than $2 billion annually.

Capacity to Maintain Leverage: HP aims for gross leverage of 1.5x-2.0x and has a track record of allocating FCF to discretionary debt repayments to maintain this range. One difference in Fitch's EBITDA leverage calculation versus HP's gross leverage calculation is that Fitch attributes factored receivables to gross debt, while HP does not. This can lead to Fitch-calculated EBITDA leverage modestly exceeding 2.0x, with HP's gross leverage remaining at the top end of its 1.5x-2.0x target. Fitch believes HP's capacity and willingness to continue gross debt reductions that support below 2.0x gross leverage is an important credit profile consideration in the 'BBB+' rating.

Emphasis on Capital Returns: Net of discretionary debt repayments, HP aims to distribute 100% of FCF to shareholders using dividends, which roughly increase with earnings growth and share buybacks. HP's flexibility and willingness to moderate share buyback levels when required to support debt repayments helps support its current credit profile. HP has maintained a cash balance that more than adequately supports standard cyclicality in its business despite returning 100% of FCF.

Low Capital Spending Benefits FCF: The Personal Systems and Printing segments generated operating margins of about 6% and 19%, respectively, supporting overall EBITDA margins of approximately 10%. Cost increases from possible additional tariffs may pressure margins, but with over 90% of North American products manufactured outside of China and some supply chain agility, HP has capacity to mitigate the cost pressures. In addition, a low and stable capital cost structure consistently between 1% and 2% of revenues benefits HP's ability to efficiently convert EBITDA to FCF.

Peer Analysis

Dell Technologies, Inc.'s (BBB/F2/Positive) relative business profile benefits from its exposure to the server and storage market. HP has a comparable PC business and faces secular challenges in its printing business, which is reflected in EBITDA margins (both about 10%). HP's financial profile has historically remained stronger than Dell's, with higher FCF margins and a historically more conservative financial structure. However, in recent years Dell has shrunk the financial structure differences.

HP's financial structure has traditionally been more conservative than Hewlett Packard Enterprise Company's (HPE; BBB+/F2/Stable). However, it has been more exposed to secular challenges, such as within its printing business. Inclusive of current and expected financing related to HPE's Juniper acquisition, HPE's core EBITDA is expected to increase approximately 1x above HP's, if the acquisition closes. However, this increase is expected to normalize over a period of deleveraging.

HP has a larger shareholder return policy than Dell or HPE, which returns about 100% of FCF to shareholders compared to 80%+ FCF at Dell and 60% at HPE. The differences in return policy are partially explained by HPE's practice of considering M&A a core component of its expected growth. Dell focuses on smaller, more strategic value tuck-in acquisitions.

HP's' return policy of 100% of FCF is consistent with many highly rated issuers, and the company has significant financial flexibility. However, while the underlying sector trend is a constraint on the rating, Fitch views HP's financial policy in the context of its explicit leverage target as supportive of its credit profile.

Key Assumptions

--In 2025, commercial performance in Personal Systems, supported by the Windows refresh cycle, more than offsets caution among consumers, resulting in mid-single-digit revenue growth in the segment. Longer-term growth is modestly above GDP, supported by higher prices and the increasing sales mix contribution from AI PCs;

--Print segment declines modestly in fiscal 2025. Longer term yoy growth is directionally flat to down, with competitive pricing pressures expected to persist in the segment;

--HP maintains EBITDA margins above 10% during the forecast period supported by cost rationalization initiatives, strong print margins with a customer lifetime profitability focus and upside constrained by supply chain cost pressures;

--Accounts receivable factoring levels stable during the forecast period;

--Capex is 1.25% revenue, in line with the historical low capital expense model and the expectation of no large capital buildouts during the forecast period;

--No material M&A during forecast period;

--Maturing debt refinanced at similar levels with small discretionary debt repayments made as applicable from FCF to maintain gross leverage at HP's 1.5x-2.0x target. Remaining FCF after common dividends distributed to shareholders in buybacks;

--Annual interest rates applicable to variable rate debt obligations decline from 4.2% to 3.5% before generally leveling out, reflecting the current SOFR forward curve. No revolver draws expected during the forecast.

RATING SENSITIVITIES

Factors that Could, Individually or Collectively, Lead to Negative Rating Action/Downgrade

--Shift toward aggressive financial policies or abandonment of leverage target;

--Accelerating declines in underlying PC or printing markets or loss of market position;

--EBITDA leverage expected to be sustained above 2.0x;

--CFO-capex/debt sustained below 20%;

--Material M&A without the expectation of returning to target leverage within 18-24 months.

Factors that Could, Individually or Collectively, Lead to Positive Rating Action/Upgrade

--A clear shift to a higher long-term organic revenue growth trends;

--EBITDA leverage sustained below 1.5x;

--Structural improvement in EBITDA margins relative to peers;

--Articulation of and adherence to a more conservative financial policy.

Liquidity and Debt Structure

At end-fiscal 1Q25, HP's liquidity position was supported by $2.9 billion cash and equivalents and an undrawn $5 billion sustainability-linked senior unsecured revolving credit facility maturing August 2029 that backstops HP's $6 billion CP program. Additional liquidity is supported by $0.9 billion of uncommitted credit lines and Fitch's expectation of over $2 billion in annual FCF after dividends during the forecast period.

HP's maturity structure is reasonably balanced over the next 10 years, with one $1.2 billion bond maturing beyond that in 2041.

Issuer Profile

HP Inc. is a leading global provider of personal computers, printers and supplies, as well as related solutions and services.

Fitch Ratings-Toronto-04 April 2024: Fitch Ratings has affirmed HP Inc.'s Long- and Short-Term Issuer Default Ratings (IDRs) at 'BBB+' and 'F2', respectively. Fitch has also affirmed HP's senior unsecured ratings at 'BBB+' and Short-Term CP ratings at 'F2'. The Rating Outlook is Stable.

HP's ratings reflect Fitch's expectations that HP will continue to operate within HP's target 1.5x-2.0x gross leverage financial policy target, and take proactive measures to reduce gross debt when needed. The rating reflects HP's FCF generation consistency, which continues to benefit from its scale and market penetration despite a mature revenue profile in its Print Segment and recent demand softness in its Personal Systems segment.

Key Rating Drivers

Mature Segments with Strong FCF: HP's Printing segment, which generates approximately one-third of revenues, has a mature revenue profile with some revenue growth fluctuations that exceed economic trends. Longer term, Fitch anticipates flat to modest contraction within this segment, with sustained above GDP growth requiring M&A. While growth potential in the segment is challenged, the segment's higher relative operating margin results in strong contributions from the segment to HP's FCF.

HP's Personal Systems provides stable revenues with upside from secular trends such as increased artificial intelligence requirements and video opportunities from hybrid work environment trends. Fitch expects HP's commercial revenues to benefit over the longer term from the acquired Poly product suite and an improvements in enterprise spending.

Low Leverage Headroom: HP's EBITDA leverage for fiscal year end (October 31) 2023 was 2.1x, slightly below Fitch's previously 2.2x forecast, but above Fitch's downgrade sensitivity of 2.0x. Fitch forecasts fiscal 2024 EBITDA leverage to be at 2.0x before HP builds modest headroom through the remainder of the forecast. Fitch has not taken a rating action given EBITDA leverage is not forecast to be sustained above 2x. Additionally, HP's has a track record of maintaining a conservative capital structure and FCF generation, which Fitch forecasts after common dividends, is in excess of $2 billion annually.

Capacity to Maintain Leverage Target: HP targets gross leverage in the 1.5x-2.0x range and has a track record of allocating FCF to discretionary debt repayments to maintain this range. One difference in Fitch's EBITDA leverage calculation versus HP's gross leverage calculation, is that Fitch attributes factored receivables to gross debt while HP does not. This can lead to Fitch calculated EBITDA leverage modestly exceeding 2.0x, with HP's gross leverage remaining within the top end of its 1.5x- 2.0x target.

Fitch's believes HP's capacity and willingness to continue gross debt reductions that support below 2.0x gross leverage is an important credit profile consideration in the 'BBB+' rating.

Leveraging Scale in Growth Areas: HP's scale and market penetration allows it to it to take advantage of a number of opportunities with growth and margin prospects above its core portfolio. HP has identified these 'Key Growth Areas' and estimates they will grow to $15 billion of revenues by 2026. During fiscal 2023, these areas grew at mid-single digits compared to an overall revenue contraction of 14.6%.

Emphasis on Capital Returns: Net of discretionary debt repayments, HP aims to distribute 100% of FCF to shareholders using dividends, which roughly increase with earnings growth and share buybacks. HP's flexibility and willingness to moderate share buyback levels when required to support debt repayments helps support its current credit profile. This was evidenced in fiscal 2023, when share buybacks decreased to $100 million from $4.3 billion in 2022 and HP retired $1.6 billion of debt. Despite returning 100% of FCF, HP has maintained a cash balance that more than adequately supports standard cyclicality in its business.

Low Capital Spending Benefits FCF: Personal Systems and Printing segments generated operating margins of approximately 6% and 20%, respectively in fiscal 1Q24, resulting in an overall EBITDA margin of 10.7%. HP's EBITDA margin should benefit from its three-year structural cost savings plan, which it originally announced in November 2022 and subsequently increased the goal from $1.4 billion to $1.6 billion. A low and stable capital cost structure, which is consistently between 1%-2% of revenues annually, benefits HP's ability to efficiently convert EBITDA to FCF.

Derivation Summary

Compared to Dell Technologies, Inc. (BBB/Stable), Dell's industry profile rating sub-factor is two notches above HP at 'bbb-'. Dell's business profile benefits from its exposure to the server and storage market. HP has a comparable PC business and faces secular challenges in its printing business, which is reflected in EBITDA margins (both at approximately 10%). HP's financial profile is stronger than Dell's, with higher FCF margins and a historically more conservative financial structure. However, Dell has in recent years shrunk the financial structure difference.

Compared to Hewlett Packard Enterprise Co. (HPE; BBB+/Stable), HP's financial structure has traditionally been more conservative. However, it has been more exposed to secular challenges, such as within its printing business. HP has better CFO-capex/debt at 23% in its fiscal 2023, compared to 12.2% for HPE. Similar to HP, HPE's leverage is expected to increase to above its 2.0x, depending on how HPE replaces the commitments for the $14 billion in term-loan financing at close of its Juniper acquisition.

HP has a larger shareholder return policy then Dell or HPE, with a plan to return approximately 100% of FCF to shareholders compared to 80%+ FCF at Dell and 60% at HPE. The differences in return policy are partially explained by HPE's practice of considering M&A a core component of its expected growth. Additionally, Dell is targeting smaller more strategic value tuck in acquisitions.

HP's' return policy of 100% of FCF is consistent with many highly rated issuers, and the company has significant financial flexibility. However, while the underlying sector trend is a constraint on the rating, Fitch views HP's financial policy in the context of its explicit leverage target as supportive of its credit profile.

Key Assumptions

--Personal Systems growth in fiscal 2024 yoy performance improves modestly in 2H24 supported by market share in commercial, growth in video market and a slightly less cautious consumer. Longer term growth modestly above GDP;

--Print segment declines modestly in fiscal 2024, but improves over 1Q24 trend. Longer term yoy growth approximately flat with visibility support adoption of HP All-In subscription offsetting overall hardware volume pressure;

--HP maintains EBITDA margins in excess of 10% during forecast period supported by cost rationalization initiatives and sustained strong print margins with customer lifetime profitability focus;

--A/R securitization levels stable during forecast period;

--Capex of 1.25% revenue, in line with historic low capital expense model and the expectation no large capital build outs during forecast;

--No material M&A during forecast period;

--Discretionary debt repayments made as applicable from FCF to maintain gross leverage between HP's 1.5x-2.0x target. Remaining FCF after common dividends distributed to shareholder in buyback.

RATING SENSITIVITIES

Factors that Could, Individually or Collectively, Lead to Positive Rating Action/Upgrade:

--A clear shift to a higher long-term organic revenue growth trend;

--EBITDA Leverage sustained below 1.5x;

--Structural improvement in EBITDA margins relative to peers;

--Articulation of and adherence to a more conservative financial policy.

Factors that Could, Individually or Collectively, Lead to Negative Rating Action/Downgrade:

--Shift toward aggressive financial policies or abandonment of leverage target;

--Accelerating declines in underlying PC or printing markets or loss of market position;

--EBITDA Leverage expected to be sustained above 2.0x;

--CFO-Capex/debt sustained below 20%;

--Material M&A without the expectation of return to target leverage within 18-24 months;

Liquidity and Debt Structure

Multiple Liquidity Sources: At fiscal 1Q24, HP's liquidity position was supported by $2.4 billion cash and equivalents, an undrawn $5 billion sustainability-linked senior unsecured revolving credit facility maturing May 26, 2026 that back-stops HP's $6 billion commercial paper program. Further liquidity is supported by $1.2 billion of uncommitted credit lines and Fitch's expectation of over $2 billion after dividend annual FCF during the forecast period.

HP's maturity structure is reasonably balanced over the next 10 years, with one $1.2 billion bond maturing beyond that in 2041.

Fitch Ratings-Chicago-29 March 2022: Fitch Ratings has affirmed HP, Inc.'s ratings, including its Long-Term Issuer Default Rating (IDR) and senior unsecured ratings, on the company's announcement that it will acquire Plantronics, Inc. Fitch has also assigned 'BBB+' ratings to HP's proposed senior unsecured notes. The Rating Outlook is Stable. Fitch's affirmation affects $12.1 billion of total debt, including its undrawn revolving credit facility but excluding the proposed notes.

The acquisition of Plantronics adds leading positions in video-conferencing solutions, peripherals and software to HP's ecosystem of devices, software and digital services for workforce management across customers' hybrid IT environments. Plantronics also adds $1.7 billion of trailing 12-month revenue with accretive profit margins immediately upon close and $500 million of expected revenue synergies by fiscal year 2025 by leveraging HP's go-to-market and installed base. HP also anticipates 600bp of operating profit margin expansion by FY25 from scale efficiencies.

HP will acquire Plantronics in an all-cash transaction for approximately $3.3 billion, including Plantronics' net debt, which was approximately $1.3 billion as of January 1, 2022. HP expects the transaction to close by end-2022, subject to customary closing conditions, regulatory approvals and approval by Plantronics' stockholders. HP will fund the transaction with available cash and debt, and Fitch estimates total debt will be 1.8x operating EBITDA, pro forma for the transaction.

The proposed senior notes will be pari passu with HP's existing senior unsecured indebtedness and net proceeds will be used for general corporate purposes, re-financing of its 2022 maturities, working capital, a portion of the acquisition and share repurchases.

Acquisition Boosts Growth Profile: Fitch expects the acquisition of Plantronics will strengthen HP's growth profile, even before revenue synergies, given meaningful anticipated underlying market growth as businesses invest in optimizing their hybrid IT environments. Fitch Ratings-New York-07 June 2021: Fitch Ratings has assigned a 'BBB+' rating to HP Inc.'s announced senior unsecured notes and sustainability notes. Fitch has also assigned a 'BBB+' rating to HP's new five-year sustainability-linked revolving credit facility, which HP entered into May 26. HP will use the net proceeds of the senior notes for general corporate purposes including redemption of all of the outstanding $412 million 4.375% notes and $586 million 4.650% notes due 2021. HP will use the net proceeds of the sustainability notes to finance or refinance projects that meet one or more eligibility criteria including renewable energy, green buildings, energy efficiency, clean transportation, pollution prevention, eco-efficient technologies, sustainable land use and socioeconomic advancement.

The interest margin and commitment fee on the revolver depend on HP's senior unsecured debt rating with adjustments for meeting specified sustainability targets with respect to greenhouse gas reduction and diversity and inclusion.

Key Rating Drivers

Better than Expected Pandemic Performance: HP's business showed resilience during the coronavirus pandemic, buoyed by consumer demand for both personal systems (PS) and printing. Consumer PS revenue increased 42% in 3Q20, 24% in 4Q20, 34% in 1Q21 and 72% in 2Q21. Consumer print revenue increased 7%, 21%, 55% and 77% respectively, in the same periods.

Overall revenue for the LTM period ended Jan. 31, 2021 was down only 1.7% and nearly flat on a constant currency basis as a result. Fitch had expected revenue to decline high-single digits in 2H20 led by double digit declines in print. While historically viewed as a liability, HP's consumer segment provided an important offset to reduced office PS and printing demand. By contrast, Xerox revenue fell 23% in 2020. HP's prints segment declined 9% in the LTM period ended Jan. 31, 2021. Fitch now expects HP's consolidated revenue to grow in the low-double digits in 2021.

Leverage & FCF Profile: Despite near-term operational challenges due to the coronavirus pandemic, HP has maintained strong credit protection metrics. HP's total debt with equity credit to operating EBITDA (inclusive of debt attributable to trade receivable sales) was 1.4x at April 30, 2021, below Fitch's 1.5x positive rating sensitivity. Gross debt to FCF (post-dividend) was 1.9x, also below Fitch's 3.5x positive sensitivity. Fitch sees HP's gross leverage exiting FY21 at 1.6x while gross debt to FCF will be approximately 3.5x. Fitch expects HP's FCF margin will approach 5% of revenue in FY21 as working capital uses are offset by continued cost discipline.

Fitch further assumes that HP continues to return approximately $12 billion to shareholders in the form of share repurchases in FY21 and FY22 via allocation of 100% of FCF and incremental borrowings of approximately $3 billion. Leverage under this scenario rises to 1.7x, leaving ample headroom to Fitch's sensitivities to accommodate weak operating performance. As a result, the agency sees HP's credit protection metrics and overall business profile remaining consistent with 'BBB+'. However, this view is uncertain and subject to the workplace and consumer demand patterns and HP's further ability to reduce it cost structure while maintaining its market position, although the company's resiliency through the coronavirus is highly encouraging.

Financial Policy: Given HP's better than expected operating performance we believe the company will not delay or downsize its Value Creation Plan. Previously, HP committed to prudently maintain leverage at the lower end of its 1.5x to 2.0x range (calculated excluding share-based compensation and debt attributable to trade receivable sales) during the brunt of the coronavirus pandemic impact. HP has underscored its flexibility to alter its plan while stressing its desire to maintain its credit rating. This is supported by its post-separation track record of fiscal conservatism.

Gross leverage has largely remained below 2.0x since FY15 and only temporarily exceeding in conjunction with the company's acquisition of Samsung's printer business. Additionally, the company has shown discipline in resisting a transaction with Xerox both due to that company's operational performance and potential capital structure impacts. While HP's Value Plan continues to represent a more aggressive financial policy, its concurrent adoption of an explicit leverage target and willingness to maintain it in the face of operational challenges is consistent with its rating given operational uncertainty.

Strategic Options: Fitch continues to believe secular pressure in the print sector may warrant further industry consolidation. While a combination with Xerox appears to be off the table at this time, HP's CEO said previously that he saw strategic merit in a combination with Xerox. However, Fitch no longer assumes the risk of a pro forma capital structure that would be leveraging and require HP to maintain credit protection metrics above its stated leverage target or abandon its shareholder plan, which previously weighed on HP's rating. Additionally, in Fitch's view this is also no longer offset by operational challenges posed by the coronavirus pandemic and its potential longer-term impacts, which have been less severe than expected.

Derivation Summary

The ratings reflect HP's ability to manage through a temporary shock like the coronavirus pandemic and address secular headwinds to a greater degree than its comparably rated technology peers. Fitch believes HP's organic growth, profitability and FCF prospects are meaningfully greater than its most closely related peers and competitors, including Xerox Corporation, Dell Technologies and Lexmark. HP has improved its PC market share in an overall market that has faced continued pressures, and achieved revenue growth reflecting its product mix as well as supply chain scale amid component shortages. HP has also maintained its leading market share in the aggregate global printer market.

HP's profitability metrics are high for the 'BBB' category, reflecting its margin profile and FCF generation, and the company is poised to expand its margin owing to ongoing restructuring. HP's financial structure is comparable with more highly-rated corporates, and the company enjoys significant financial flexibility. However, while the underlying sector trend is limiting to the rating we view HP's financial policy in the context of its explicit leverage target.

Fitch assigned the lower of two available short-term rating options based on the following observations:

--Fitch has determined the three-notch midpoint for HP's Financial Flexibility factor is 'a-', below the minimum required of 'a' to achieve a higher short-term rating. HP has very comfortable liquidity consistent with an 'a' factor rating (Fitch believes HP would not have to use external funding in the next 12 months even under the very unlikely event of a severe stress scenario whereby the company loses access to external funding). However, Fitch assesses HP's Financial Discipline factor to be 'bbb' since HP is shifting away from its historical practice of maintaining significant headroom in its credit protection metrics, representing a material weakness in Fitch's view, constraining the factor rating and thus the short-term rating. HP has traditionally maintained a conservative financial policy in terms of leverage although this appears to be trending downwards as the company pursues significant shareholder return;

--The Financial Structure factor is 'a', consistent with HP's leverage metrics;

--The Operating Environment factor (upper end of the rating band) is 'aa' given HP's geographic orientation heavily weighted towards developed economies.

No parent/subsidiary linkage is applicable, no Country Ceiling constraint was in effect for these ratings, and no Operating Environment influence was in effect for these ratings. Liquidity and Debt Structure

Solid Liquidity and Debt Structure: HP's liquidity is supported by $4.1 billion total cash and cash equivalents (approximately $3.8 billion in Fitch-defined readily available cash) at April 30, 2020. While HP historically maintained efficient access to its cash held abroad, Fitch assumes nearly unfettered access in light of U.S. tax reform. HP has access to its undrawn $4 billion senior unsecured revolving credit facility, which backs up its $6 billion CP program. Additionally, on May 29, 2020 the company entered into a 364-day revolving credit facility with $1 billion in aggregate lending commitments. HP had $613 million in CP borrowings at April 30, 2020.

Fitch views HP's revolver availability, readily available cash, and FCF as fully backstopping HP's CP program. Fitch expects HP will generate around $1 billion in annual FCF (post-dividend) in FY20 and in excess of $2 billion annually over the ratings horizon. HP's maturities are well staggered the first being $648 million principal outstanding due in December. Fitch assumes HP will refinance maturities as they come due and may issue debt to fund share repurchases in excess of FCF while managing its capital structure to its leverage target.
